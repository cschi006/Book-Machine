# Chapter 1 — Bianca

A phone alert buzzing through my desk woke me up. My fingers were sticky with ink and there was a finished portrait under my face. Neither detail was ideal.

The lamp over my desk burned itself into a small white sun behind my eyes. Outside my studio window, late afternoon glow had gone dark, which I hoped meant late evening and not early morning. I rubbed at the faint damp patch on my sleeve where my mouth had drooled just a little.

On the page, a familiar face waited for me.

Ink clean and deliberate, shadows laid in with a patience I definitely had not possessed an hour ago, if an hour ago was even the correct number. The head was turned slightly, eyes aimed just past the viewer, mouth set in that Mona Lisa expression of "I know something you don't know." The person had short dark hair and fine, dark brows. There was a line of light at one cheekbone even the best make-up influencer would be hard pressed to recreate. Oh, and of course the cute pointy ears of a fae.

Like all the others, this one had a name written at the bottom. Caelith Noct-Aravain.

Like all the others, I had no memory of making it.

Arms overhead, my head swiveled about the room. The stacks of completed drawings laid haphazardly about my workspace judged me. Here I was creating a new drawing instead of taking care of the ones waiting to be photographed or packed. I needed to be better at maintaining my Instagram. I was pretty good at paying attention to my Etsy, the money maker, but Instagram, the 'salesperson' part of my business, made my skin crawl just a little.

I was just at the doorway of my bedroom when my phone buzzed again. Ring had caught movement at the front door.

At 11 pm I doubted it was the squirrel that lived somewhere near the shared fence or my occasional visit from Mr. Terry from next door, standing on my stoop with a misdelivered package. I liked my neighbor; he was quiet, retired, impossible to place politically, and not too nosy.

My jaw unhinged as the live camera feed loaded. Two strange men stood on my porch.

One of them was a little back from the door, hands visible, posture relaxed. Even through the warped fish-eye view, there was something off about how their face resolved on the screen. Not ugly. Not even remotely that. It has a bit of uncanny valley in there; proportions were too exact.

The other man was worse.

Nothing about him was overtly threatening, though he was taller by half a foot, and he was just as gorgeous, but he was wearing what I was fairly sure was a costume piece. A dark, structured coat that looked like something a general might wear from the 1800's. He had excellent posture as he squared up with my door.

"Hello, Miss Bianca," he said.

My stomach dropped. This did not sound like someone whose car broke down and their phone was dead. He dripped cool confidence even through the mic.

I hit the microphone button. "That's already too familiar for a stranger on my porch."

The beautiful wrong one glanced sideways at him, eyebrows lifting but mouth staying clamped.

"We need to speak with you," he said.

"No, you don't, especially at 11 o'clock at night."

The other one stepped a little closer. Their voice was not masculine, but not exactly feminine either. "I know this is strange," they said. "We'd only like a few minutes."

People only said that when they planned to annex the next forty minutes of your life.

"Unfortunately, I'm using all of mine."

The dark-coated man stepped closer to the camera, close enough that the fisheye warped his face slightly at the edges. "If you would open the door, we need to ask you some questions."

I stared at the tiny image of him on my phone.

"No," I said, and cut the audio.

I smashed the volume button on the side of my phone just to be sure as I crept to my front door. On the way I grabbed my old field hockey stick in the hall closet. My stomach hurt, my palms were sweating, but yes, my door was locked. I did the same for all my back doors, and then set the alarm in my house. They knew my name.

I got ready for bed per usual, but checked my Ring camera one more time to make sure they had left. They had not. I called the non-emergency line to let them know two people were in front of my house and wouldn't leave. As shaken as I was, I didn't feel safe enough to take my "as needed" prescriptions for anxiety that made me a little sleepy. I wanted to be fully alert, just in case.

My alarm went off with its usual soft tones. I rolled over wanting to hit snooze when something heavy hit my wooden floor and jerked me awake. My field hockey stick I kept next to my bed last night just in case had fallen over from me moving around.

I snatched my phone off my nightstand, ripping the charging cord out. Opening my Ring app, I saw a bazillion movement notifications overnight, or 42. One at 3:42 am was longer than the others. A police car came into view, parking on the opposite side of my street. An officer got out walking toward two people on my curb. They were lying down, their feet in the street and their backs flat in my yard. One of them lifted a hand in the air, and the police officer turned around, drove away. They had not made it halfway across the street from what I could tell, never even spoke. Just left.

My soul left my body. I went through every mundane explanation I had, distracted, called off on the radio, deciding they weren't worth the paperwork…none of them felt good.

I switched to the live feed, and there they were, lying down. I downloaded the videos of the officer walking away AND the video of me talking to them last night. I sent them to Mara with a run down. She wouldn't see it for hours, 6:30 was way to early for her.

*There are two people outside my door that will not leave since last night. They waved OFF the police. Like magic or something. Wtf. What do you do when police don't work.*

My stomach was doing something unhelpful. My legs knew what was waiting at the end of the front walk and had opinions about it that I was choosing not to entertain. My own heartbeat was audible in my ears.

"What would Mara do?" I said, trying to calm myself down, "She would march right out there with her 9mm gun and tell them to fuck off."

I was not Mara, and didn't own a gun, but I did have nasty resting bitch face and a mean-looking field hockey stick. That would have to do.

With every step I made myself angrier. They had slept on my lawn. They had ignored a police officer. They had done it on purpose, in front of my house, because they had decided I was a person who could be waited out. That was not information I was going to let stand. By the time I reached my front door, I was 90% certain I wouldn't throw up.

The early morning cold air slid into the entryway as I pulled open my door. The stalking duo were already standing and halfway up my walkway. Up close, neither man improved.

The gentler one, if that description was going to hold, smiled at me without teeth. Their dark hair was tucked behind one ear. They wore dockers with a golfer's belt and a navy blue polo tucked in. Maybe a golfer?

The other man looked like bad news in a tailored coat. His confident swagger was still there, but now he was pissed, like maybe someone who spent all night sleeping on asphalt. Good.

He took in the hockey stick, the narrow opening, my position behind the door, and said nothing about any of it. Which was, irritatingly, the correct move.

"That is close enough." I braced my weight against the door.

"We aren't here to cause harm. We just have a few questions." Cai's voice was the gentler of the two.

Felix's expression suggested he might, in fact, be here exactly for that.

I kept my weight braced against the door. "Great. Then this can stay a very short interaction."

The gentler one gave a small nod. "I'm Cai. This is Felix."

Felix said, "Is there anyone else in the house with you or have there been strange people coming around?"

For a second I thought I had misheard him.

Then I laughed once because the other options were worse. "That's an unhinged opening question. And no other than you there are no strange people around. You do not need to know who does and does not live here."

"Answer it."

"No."

His face did not change. "Are you by chance a witch, or do you have fae ancestry?"

The porch seemed to narrow.

There were a lot of bad ways for a stranger on your porch. Most of them existed on a scale between scam and violence. None of them included this.

I tightened my grip on the stick. "If this is some kind of performance art thing, I hate to tell you, but your audience research was off."

Cai shot Felix a look that translated clearly as *you are doing badly*. "We know how this sounds," they said.

"Congratulations on your self-awareness. Look, I don't have time for this. I need to get to work." I cringed giving them any detail about my life. "If you need help, you should have talked to the officer last night. You are not welcome here."

Felix ignored that. "Have you had electrical malfunctions or unexplained lapses in memory, maybe last night before we spoke?"

That one landed hard enough that I felt it in my teeth.

Every visible inch of myself in the doorway registered at once, the open slice of house at my back, the fresh portrait still upstairs on my desk like evidence. *Can they see me in my house?*

"You need to leave." My anger was going to turn into an anxiety monster if I did not wrap this up.

Cai's voice softened further. "Has anything appeared recently that frightened you?"

"Ok, still not conversation topics for strangers in my yard." Why was I answering him? I needed to go in, shut the door, and wait this out.

Felix said, "Something happened last night. It happened in your house and we are trying to figure out what it was. If you know something, please help us."

"I know you're trespassing on several levels."

His gaze flicked once over my shoulder into the house, not trying to push past me so much as assessing the space.

My thoughts split cleanly in two. One half stayed at the door doing the conversation. The other began running through exit strategy. Close door, lock it, call someone. Yell, create witnesses. Hopefully Mr. Terry had his hearing aids already on. Swing hockey stick if it came to that, though I had never actually been in a fight.

For a fraction of a second, Cai's face blurred.

It was like a bad layer comp in Photoshop, two versions of the same image slipping half a degree out of register before snapping back together. I blinked hard. My tired brain reached for exhaustion, bad light, anything with a receipt.

I had been staring. I looked at the porch railing instead.

"Bianca," Cai said, and even their voice seemed briefly double-tracked, one note over another. "Please. We are trying to prevent harm."

Felix's attention snapped to Cai. It was a tiny change, but it altered him completely. The control didn't vanish; it compressed.

"Enough." He wasn't talking to me.

Cai made a minute dismissive gesture like they had heard him and planned to ignore him. "I'm fine."

That was the moment the situation tipped from threatening to impossible.

As they spoke, the careful arrangement of their features gave way for an instant, like a mask slipping on skin that wasn't built for it. I saw another face underneath.

Then Cai folded. One second they were standing there, one hand lifted slightly in what might have become reassurance. The next, their knees gave and the whole of them dropped sideways.

Felix caught them before they hit the ground.

The speed of it was ugly. He moved with no wasted motion, one arm across Cai's shoulders, the other bracing their fall, but the thing that jolted through me wasn't the competence. It was the break in his composure.

"Cai."

Their name came out stripped down, raspy.

And in that split second, with their head tipped back in his arm and whatever had been holding their face together no longer doing it, I saw them clearly.

Caelith Noct-Aravain. The portrait on my desk.

The same angle of cheekbone catching light. The same dark sweep of brow. The same pointy ears.

My hand loosened on the hockey stick. It hit the floorboards with a hard wooden knock.

Felix looked up at me sharply. "Open the door."

There was an unconscious impossible person in his arms and my ability to process had taken a temporary leave of absence.

I opened the door all the way and stepped back.

He did not thank me, which was good because I wouldn't have trusted him if he had.

He got Cai over the threshold and into my living room. I kicked the hockey stick farther out of the way with my foot, shoved a stack of padded mailers off one end of the couch, and pulled the throw blanket down where it had bunched against the armrest.

Felix lowered Cai onto the couch, one hand at the back of their neck, the other at their wrist.

Cai's skin had gone pale with labored breathing. Their face was simply their face now, and the lines I had laid down hours ago were all I saw. Or yesterday.

Felix said something low to them I didn't catch. Then he looked at me.

"What did you see?"

I stayed by the entryway instead of moving closer. The distance wasn't enough to qualify as safety, but it was a line and I wanted one.

"I saw a person collapse in my yard."

His patience, if he had arrived with any, had clearly died before crossing town. "Before that."

Felix stood. Not quickly. That would have been easier, somehow. He rose with deliberate control, but now urgency sat right under it, close enough to break the surface.

"What has been happening here?" he asked. "Speak plainly."

The instinctive answer was *none of your business*.

The problem with that answer was Cai on my couch wearing a face I had drawn, the fae face I had drawn. Cai made a faint sound, not quite conscious. My mouth was dry. I swallowed once, annoyed at my own body for participating in this.

"I don't know what you are talking about. But how did they turn into my drawing?" I asked.

His expression did not move, but the room did, somehow. It seemed to gather around the words.

"I know their face," I added, before I hyperventilated. "That's why I let you in. They look exactly like one of my drawings."
