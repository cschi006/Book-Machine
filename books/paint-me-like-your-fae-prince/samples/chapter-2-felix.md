# Chapter 2 — Felix

Confirmation we were in the right place didn't feel so great when it came at such a high cost.

Cai lay half-curled against the back of the mortal's couch, one hand still braced where they had tried to push upright too soon. Their glamour had torn badly. It was no longer a clean veil over their body. I put two fingers to the inside of their wrist and counted. It was beating a little fast but steady, a good sign.

I steadied what I could with a thread of power. I felt them accept it, their heart beat evening out.

Cai opened their eyes.

"If you wanted to try out a mortal couch that badly, I would have gotten us a hotel room."

"Ha. My head is hurting. Where is she?"

She made a small sound that might have been disbelief. "Um, source of what? I want to call an ambulance but not if you are just going to wave them away. Which how did you do that by the way? Do you need water?"

"Yes. That would be lovely thank you."

"Ok, I am going to grab some, and then you are going to explain to me why you look like my drawing."

She backed away from us before disappearing into what I assumed was the kitchen. Without taking my eyes off the room she went into, I helped Cai to a sitting position, legs still outstretched longways taking up the length of the couch.

"Cai, she drew you." I said.

"Truly? Where is the drawing? Have you seen it?" they said, controlling their breath.

"Not yet. But I intend to one way or another."

"Be nice, don't scare her anymore."

"Scared? She is not scared. She is surly and contrite, maybe distrustful but not scared."

"Prince Felix, you catch more pixies with nectar than a silver net."

I huffed, letting the conversation drop as I could hear her approaching. She carried a glass of water with straw and a ceramic mug with a paper tag hanging out.

"I brought you some tea with honey too, in case you are having a sugar crash or something. Here." She set the tea down in front of them, handing the water directly. Both mine and Cai's eyebrows rose when she went a step further to put a small pillow on the floor behind him. *Nectar it is then.*

"Thank you, Bianca." I said. "I think we may have gotten off on the wrong foot earlier. Could we sit down and talk calmly?" I tried to keep my voice level, and the slight nod from Cai told me I had accomplished my goal.

Without a word she reached behind the couch and pulled out a folding chair with a hammock bottom. She sat down closest to Cai, causing me to step aside to give her room.

"Ah, I don't suppose you have another chair?"

"Afraid not. But the floor is clean unless you want to force your ill friend to move their legs."

So much for nectar.

I chuckled, taking a seat on the floor near Cai's feet. Even sitting on the floor, I was nearly eye level with the small angry mortal.

Before I could speak, Cai jumped in.

"I know you likely have an endless amount of questions for us, as we do you. I am so sorry for the shock my fainting must have given you. I understand that even more shocking, you seem to recognize…" they made a gesture towards their face. They had yet to put their glamour back into place.

"Yes. I drew you last night."

Cai looked at me.

"Last night, was it right before we knocked on your door?"

"Um, yeah I guess."

"You guess… Do you not know?"

"I do it's just, I kind of lose track of time when I draw."

"What do you mean lose track of time?" I pressed, leaning forward.

She began fiddling with the bottom hem of her shirt.

"How about you tell me WHO you are and why you are so interested in me."

I leaned back, not bothering to correct the displeasure expressed on my face.

Cai set the drained water glass on the floor to reach for the tea.

"You know," they said between blowing on the tea, "that is an excellent question. It may be hard to accept, but we are different than you. We are not mortals…"

"You are fae," she said slowly.

"Indeed. There is a problem with our magic. Something is causing interference and we think it is coming from your domicile. We felt something last night, shortly before we knocked on your door…"

"…when I was drawing your portrait."

Cai just nodded.

"Wait here." This time, Bianca was not careful about backing away to keep her eyes on us. Cai just smiled at me, sipping the tea, while I sat seething.

When she came back downstairs moments later, she was texting on her phone while a portfolio of something was under her arm.

"Ok," she said, putting her phone in her pocket. "I will show this to you, but I want to know more. You promise to answer more of my questions?"

"Of course." Cai said, then they both looked to me. I sighed.

"This was a discovery mission, I suppose discovery can extend to you…"

She handed the hardbacked folder to Cai as they set their tea down.

Their expression changed to something like horror, hand coming up to cover their mouth.

"What is it…" I said getting up too quickly for mortal sensibilities. I felt her nearly fall over in her hammock chair next to me.

It was Cai, but not just their true form. It had their true name in the bottom corner for all to see.

"How…" I said looking from the picture back to the anxious human.

"Weird right?" she said wetting her lips.

I could feel the faint pull of magic. This was the source. I needed to contain this problem, lock down the townhouse and the human in front of me.

"Are there more?"

"Of Caelith Noct-Aravain?" she dropped their name like they were casual friends, pronunciation perfect.

"Of any. Do you have more drawings of them, of any fae person?"

"Yeah…" she looked more and more uncomfortable. "I mean I draw, that's what I do."

"Show me."

"No." She said, leaning back. "I have questions too. Like why is my drawing causing problems and what does your face look like? Do you have pointy ears?" she asked looking over my face, "Can you cast spells?"

"Bianca, this is serious. We need to know how much of a problem this is before we know how to solve it. What you are doing is dangerous for us." My hand hovered gently over the paper, stirring the notes of magic embedded within. "This is why Cai collapsed. You are hurting them with your drawings…"

"How do you know my—" I cut her off with a raised hand.

"Fine," she said. "But if you touch anything without asking, I'm… I will start screaming and my neighbor will come over and he is a vet and he has guns."

I opened my mouth to tell Cai to stay here, but one look told me it was futile.

The townhouse was small by my court standards. My bedroom was larger than her ground floor. The front room was a bit chaotic as a landing area, but the rest of home was labeled, controlled. I inspected the neatly hung portraits on the wall at the top of her stairs. One reminded me of my father, King Idris. Bianca noticed me noticing and went still for half a step.

"That one's normal," she said.

*Normal.* The word rang absurdly in a house where Cai's true name had appeared under a mortal hand. Still, I clung to everything she said. Anything could be a hint, a clue as to what we are dealing with.

"How would you know what is normal or not? Can you sense magic?"

She turned, walking backwards towards a closed door on the landing.

"Because I didn't… I didn't lose myself when I drew it. I have this thing, a medical condition, it's called dissociative amnesia. Sometimes I black out when I am drawing, like a seizure."

*Seizure?*

"Did you lose yourself when you drew Cai last night? Is that why you didn't know when you drew it?"

She nodded once before turning back to the closed door. Her shoulders tucked up under her ears with one hand on the door knob.

"Open it." I commanded.

"I want to call my friend here… This doesn't feel right."

Cai gently placed one hand on the backside of her arm.

"Whatever happens, I promise we will not harm you."

Her shoulders sagged, the handle turned with a click.

There were two workstations on either side of the room. One had a white laptop and two monitors. There was barely anything else on the desk, besides a mug that said "no drama lama," filled with pens. The other desk had pencils, pens, ink, stacks of paper. There was a lamp attached to the side of the desk, facing down to the surface. Another laptop sat open but off with stickers all over it. Shelves were full of books and little dolls with big eyes.

Along bottom of the wall were stacks of empty glass frames and the mattes. There was packing supplies, giant plastic totes labeled in neat handwriting, and stacks of hardbacked notebooks in neat piles everywhere.

Cai took a few steps in, only to sink down to the absurdly fluffy white rug dominating the room. "My Prince… it is everywhere."

It felt like walking into the royal armory. Ancient magical artifacts were stored there, waiting to be used. The magic was both active and inert in this room.

"This is wrong. So wrong," I said.

"My prince?" she said as she stooped down to my ailing friend. "Cai, are you ok?"

"Yes, Prince Felix. He is the prince of Loren, our kingdom."

I approached the desk with the drawings, starting to sift through them carefully.

"Loren? That's the name of where you are from?" Before they could answer she noticed what I was doing. "Um, excuse me, prince or not, you can't just touch my stuff. Seriously, what is happening to Cai?"

"You are happening to Cai." I said, a little too harshly. This idiot woman was killing my friend, draining my kingdom, and she was concerned about me touching her 'stuff.'

"What is that supposed to mean? You don't even care they collapsed again. Fae or whatever we need to get them to a hospital… Are you listening to me?"

I began to look through her 'stuff' and found portraits of fae I knew. There were multiple portraits of the same person, only a few in the multiples had the tingle of magic. I didn't understand what I was seeing. Not yet.

"It means," I said, "that this room is not safe."

Her laugh came out short and sharp.

I opened another portfolio. There were enough exposed names in here to compromise a household, perhaps more. Enough that if any had already left this room — I did not want to finish that thought. I had an inkling I knew what this was, and it would explain everything.

I began touching each notebook, each drawer, laying a containment sigil on the surface. It flashed pale once, then sank into the material. The pressure in the room changed by a degree, easing up. I went to every container, box, folder, bookcase, anything that could contain the stolen names. Mortal eyes would not slide past them; this was not that kind of working. It would, however, slow accidental contact and keep the active pieces from answering so loudly to one another until I returned with proper containment.

"What did you do?"

"Limited access."

"So you can cast spells."

I turned around to see her kneeling next to my friend, who sat legs crossed on their bottom. Both her arms were around their shoulders in a side hug. She was both supporting and protecting them. Cai's eyes were shut, while hers were wetter than usual. Maybe she was scared. Maybe she should be.

I took Cai's portrait from they still held under their arm. I slid it between two sheets of inert backing paper. Even through the buffer, the name bit. Their eyes fluttered open.

"You are coming with us," I said.

"No."

"I will not leave you here. This," I said waving my hand about the room, "and you, are too dangerous to leave unguarded. This is for your safety too."

"You do not get to decide that."

"I already have."

She took a deep breath, voice raising. "You can't drag people around through what, let me guess, a magical portal? I have a medical condition. I don't know about whatever you find is so wrong about my art but it's not—"

Cai surprised me cutting her off. "Bianca."

They began to stand up, shifting forward out of her arms to get their legs uncrossed, conserving energy with every slow movement. Their glamour had settled into a partial hold again, though the edges still wavered if one looked too directly.

"If there were a useful mortal answer," they said, "we would have preferred it. Quite strongly. Prince Felix and I came here looking for answers. We don't know what you are, and I believe you mean us no harm, but what this looks like is you have somehow been drawing and stealing the true names of fae. You want to see what that does, just look at me. I can barely stand."

"This is kidnapping," she said.

"Yes," I said. "If you require the blunt version."

Cai closed their eyes for half a breath. "Felix."

Bianca made a short, incredulous sound. "Jesus Christ."

"I have a job," she said. "I have—"

One moment the wall existed. The next, a vertical seam of light had replaced part of it, thin as a blade at first, then widening until the room beyond it stood in view. Cold stone. Carved arches. Light moving across the floor as if filtered through water or glass. The corridor was empty, but it carried the watchful stillness of a place built for power and kept under it for a very long time.

I expected the vision of a portal to stop her annoying prattle. What I did not expect was her serene face. She was focused, curious even.

She took one step towards the opening.

"No," she said, "No."

She looked from the corridor to the desk, to the corridor again, and when she spoke the words came out stripped of all the anger and spite she had been using to defend herself.

"I've drawn this place."

I came here believing I would find the mortal with information about the breach to our world. Instead, I found the breach to our world in the mortal.
