# Chapter 3 — Scene 1
**POV:** Bianca
**Word count target:** 1,500 (±200)
**Entry tension:** 7 · **Exit tension:** 7
**Seeds:** heel · wrack · lancet · pine · unspool

---

The cold hit me first. Then the recognition.

I knew this corridor. Same carved ribs across the ceiling. Same pale veining in the floor catching light like something moving under ice. Same narrow windows cut too high to see through, only to spill that strange filtered brightness down the walls. I knew the turn ahead before I saw it. I knew the empty plinth in the alcove on the left. I knew the way the right wall bowed inward just enough to feel built by someone who understood symmetry intellectually and disliked it on principle.

The portal closed behind us with a small mechanical click. I did not turn around.

My body had opinions about that. My stomach was unspooling itself into something low and cold. My legs knew exactly how to walk forward and they were doing it anyway, because the alternative was staying near the wall that had been my studio thirty seconds ago and was now, definitively, stone.

Two figures stood farther down the corridor. Not exactly armor. Not exactly meant to comfort me. They wore the kind of dark clothing that resolved to the eye as *competent.* They were watching us with the bored alertness of people who did this for a living and were curious only insofar as the new arrival might change their evening.

So this was the part of the kidnapping where the kidnappers had backup.

Cai made a small sound beside me. Felix had their elbow before I had processed any movement. Cai's color was worse in this light. The wrongness at the edge of their face — the slipping I had filed earlier under *bad layer comp in Photoshop* — was harder to file here. It read as wound. Their feet did not quite track in a straight line.

"This way," Felix said.

That was apparently for all of us.

I did not move for a second. Inventory was better than panic.

Corridor. Two silent guards behind. One direction forward. Cai needing help. No visible doors within sprinting distance, which was useless data because I had nowhere to sprint to. My phone in my pocket. Felix's heel ahead of me on stone. The pine-needle smell of something cold and resinous coming from a corner I could not place.

I checked the phone anyway.

8:14 a.m. No bars. Not even the *No Service* indicator I was used to seeing in elevators. Just clean nothing, like the device knew it was off the grid in a way that did not have a polite human label yet.

"What is this place," I said.

It came out flatter than I felt. I would take that.

Felix did not turn around. "Loren."

"That answers basically nothing."

"You will get the rest in order."

That was exactly the kind of sentence a person used when they wanted to control not just the information but the timing of my blood pressure. I let it go because Cai had stopped under their own weight again and Felix had moved without breaking stride to catch them, and the small graceful brutality of how he kept Cai upright while not making it visible to the corridor was the most unsettling competence I had seen in person.

The two silent figures fell in behind us. Not crowding. Just present in a way that narrowed the hallway by feet I did not have to spare.

We walked.

The corridor was a quarter-mile in my drawings. In person it was shorter. That made it worse — every detail I had been making for sixteen years was confirmed faster than I could discount it. The plinth was where I had drawn it. The wall bowed inward at exactly the place my hand had bowed it. There was a small hairline crack in the second pale stone that I had once spent a Sunday afternoon stippling and had been faintly proud of.

I had not made this up.

I had been documenting it.

That landed in my body before it landed in my brain. My ribs got tighter. The back of my throat tasted like the metallic edge of a lancet hitting porcelain — a small clean shock I had no context for and could not place. My heel skidded once on the polished floor and Felix's hand was at my elbow before I had decided to need help.

"Don't," I said.

He let go before the word finished.

That was — not nothing. I filed it for later.

Felix changed direction before we reached the main bend and pushed open a side door I had known would be there half a second before he touched it. The handle was wrought in a pattern I had drawn into a sketchbook the summer my mother decided I might benefit from a quieter color scheme in my bedroom.

The chamber inside was smaller. Less ceremonial. Or what passed for less ceremonial here, which was still ancient-grievance-with-furniture by mortal standards, but the proportions were closer to a room a person could think in.

A long narrow table of dark stone in the center. A low sideboard with a pitcher, cups, a basin of water that was so clear it looked staged. Two chairs. Not six. One wall was just wall — nothing on it — and the bare stone after a corridor that had been imposing on purpose hit me like a small mercy I had not earned.

A woman stood at the end of the table.

She had been waiting for us.

She was the put-together I had only seen in women who ran HR meetings I had been afraid of. Her hair was pulled back so cleanly it sharpened her whole face. Her coat was the color of stormwater, fitted, expensive in a way that did not declare itself. She did not smile. She did not nod. She looked at me the way a building inspector looks at a structural crack.

Felix said something to her in a language I had not heard and somehow disliked immediately. Her answer was two words. The two figures behind us stayed in the corridor. The door shut.

The room got bigger by a measurable amount.

"Bianca Lawson," she said.

She knew my last name. Of course she did.

"Yeah," I said. My voice came out hoarse from the cold. "Hi. Sorry — you are?"

"Vael." She did not offer a hand. "Second to Prince Felix."

*Second of what,* almost arrived out loud. I kept it inside. This was not the kind of room you got cute in. I had read it in three seconds.

Cai sank carefully into one of the two chairs. Felix did not sit. Neither did Vael.

Neither did I.

Vael's gaze did not move from my face. "You crossed the threshold of your own will."

It was somewhere between a question and a charge. I had been in enough meetings to know which one.

"He opened the door," I said. Even at the gate where I had nothing left to bargain with, my voice still bent toward the version of me that handled customer service emails. "I had a stick. He had a portal. I would not say *of my own will* is what happened in there."

Vael's mouth did one thing, very precisely. It almost moved.

Then she said, "You will be shown what matters first."

The phrasing was identical to Felix's in the studio. Apparently they had a script.

She turned and walked to the sideboard. Cai's hand closed around the edge of the chair seat in a way that read as a body checking it had a chair to sit in. Felix stayed exactly where he was. Far enough from me to be respectful. Close enough that I did not have to think about whether I had been backed against a wall.

The room narrowed around the page Vael was about to put on the table.

I did not yet know what was on it. I knew it was already what mattered.
