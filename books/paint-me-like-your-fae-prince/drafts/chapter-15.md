# Chapter 15 — Bianca POV (You Can Move)

Six weeks is the length of time it takes a mortal life to absorb a fae prince without making a production out of it.

That was not the timeline anyone had given me. That was the timeline I had measured by — six weeks since my townhouse had stopped sounding empty when I unlocked it; six weeks since Felix's first morning trying to operate a Keurig like a person who had never met one before; six weeks since Mara had moved a duffel bag of clothes into my smaller bedroom and started staying over on weeknights *to supervise.* The shape of *home* had stretched without my having to explicitly redesign it, and the stretching had a pace I could keep up with so long as I did not stare too directly at the math.

The townhouse was the same townhouse. The Ring camera was the same Ring camera, although Felix had laid a small ward at the threshold that did, as it turned out, also work against squirrels. The mail still arrived on the same schedule. Slack still pinged at the wrong hours. The coffee on the warmer was, statistically, still the most likely thing to be going faintly burnt in my kitchen at any given moment because *I made coffee for one out of two-and-sometimes-three* was a habit I had not yet broken.

The bigger thing had landed first: the house no longer functioned like a bunker.

There were still systems. Of course there were. Mara had opinions about where extra keys should live, and Felix had strong feelings about the practical superiority of wards over my previous dependence on a Ring camera and spite. I still worked best with warning before people arrived. I still needed a room to go quiet in when my brain got oversaturated. None of that had transformed into extrovert sunshine through the power of romance and personal growth. Tragic, I know.

Two coats by the door, one of them infuriatingly elegant for suburban life. Mara's tote bag occupied a dining chair like it paid taxes there. Court dispatches came sometimes folded beside grocery lists and takeout menus, and Felix read them only after dinner instead of building his entire body around them. The kitchen held evidence of other people using it without asking permission first.

On that particular evening, Mara was at the counter decanting leftover curry into containers she had brought over because she did not trust either me or Felix with *portion realism,* which was rude but not unsupported by evidence.

"This one is yours," she said, pointing with a spoon. "This one is mine. This one is if the immortal prince learns how lunches work."

"I know how lunches work," Felix said from the table.

"You know how power works. It's different."

Cai, restored enough now to look almost offensively untroubled by physics again, leaned back in one of my kitchen chairs and watched this exchange with an expression of pure scholarly interest. They were here on a court dispatch run — Vael's signature was on the folded sheet at Felix's elbow, the kind of routine consultation a kingdom that had stabilized enough to send paperwork was now able to send paperwork about. Cai had stayed for dinner. Cai stayed for dinner most weeks.

"I think Mara is right," they said.

"Of course you do," Felix replied.

I came in from the hall with three clean mugs and found the fruit bowl on the table full of small silver fish.

I stopped.

The fish opened and closed their mouths in synchronized silence.

Cai looked at me with saintly innocence. "Something wrong?"

Mara turned, took one look, and pointed her spoon at them. "Do not put live-looking objects in her kitchen without notice."

"They are not alive."

One of the fish leaped delicately over the lip of the bowl and became a clementine halfway down.

I set the mugs down very carefully. "You're both banned from using the phrase *live-looking.*"

Cai's smile widened. Healthy magic suited them. Annoyingly.

A minute later the fish were just fruit again. Mara packed herself out with the leftovers and the kind of firm instructions usually reserved for convalescents and badly supervised college students. Cai left after that by the front door, pausing only to squeeze my shoulder once on the way out.

"No more catastrophic art crimes this week," they said.

"I'll do my best."

"Please do. I'm enjoying the novelty of not almost fainting."

Then they were gone, and the house settled around the two of us without sealing shut.

Outside, the evening was already darker than it would have been three weeks ago. The HOA had finally gotten the lawn-care company to clean up the property next door, which had stood empty since the Saturday of the convention — the public-facing story was that *Mr. Terry* had been hospitalized and then transferred into long-term institutional care after a violent break with reality, and the truth was secured under royal bindings in a hold in Loren that no mortal would ever find. The lawn was mowed. The truck was gone. The Ring camera looked out on a row of townhouses where the next-door window was dark for the first time in four years, and the dark was, by some unspecific measure I had been logging on a daily basis, becoming part of the architecture rather than evidence.

I had not yet drawn the empty house. I would, eventually. Not yet.

Felix was in the living room by the window with a book he had been reading for half an hour without turning a page. There was a lamp on behind him, another pool of light on the coffee table, and the quiet hum of the dishwasher from the kitchen. Ordinary sounds. Mortal ones.

I stood in the doorway and looked at him long enough that he lowered the book.

"What."

"Nothing. Sit there."

He set the book aside. "That sounded like instructions."

"It was."

I went to my desk.

For a second my old reflex tried to move in — the small interior brace before paper, the wariness built from years of waking to work I had not chosen. But this was my desk, my lamp, my pencils lined where I had put them, my hand fully awake and attached to the rest of me.

No static-hush. No current opening under my skin. Just paper.

I clipped a fresh sheet to the board and sat. Felix watched me across the room, one arm laid along the back of the chair, expression unreadable in the way it still was sometimes until I looked long enough to see the person underneath the prince.

This was the first time I had drawn him consciously.

The dishwasher ran. Mara's labeled leftovers cooled on a kitchen shelf. Felix sat in my living room under lamplight, and I put the first line of his shoulder onto the page.

Then the angle of his wrist against the chair arm. The shape of his mouth when he was trying not to ask too many questions and failing. The lamplight caught at the edge of his hair, too precise and then human again once my hand translated it.

I drew the way I had been drawing for sixteen years before the channel had taught me to mistrust my own hand — slow, deliberate, with the small corrections of a person making something on purpose. The page accumulated the visible decisions I was making. No name went at the bottom. No name needed to. The man in the chair was the man I was looking at, and the line on the page was the line I had decided to put there, and the difference between those two things was the smallest distance I had ever known a piece of art to measure.

After a minute he said, "Should I keep still."

I looked up.

"You can move," I said.

And when he did, I followed him across the page, awake for all of it.
