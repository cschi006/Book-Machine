# Chapter 4 — Scene 3
**POV:** Felix
**Word count target:** 1,400 (±200) — chapter close
**Entry tension:** 7 · **Exit tension:** 8 — chapter ends on pattern reveal
**Seeds:** yield · slew · pine · breach · grained

---

The building was beige.

That was not a court observation. It was the working sentence I gave it because beige was what the structure had in place of any other architectural intention. Three stories of leasable apartments above a row of small commercial storefronts that included a pet groomer, a vape shop, and a place selling bubble tea. The buyer lived on the second floor in unit 214. She had owned the original of Caelith Noct'Aravain for twenty-one months and reviewed it as *the prettiest piece in my whole apartment, perfectly captured the vibe.*

I parked across the lot in a row that gave us angles on both the main entrance and the service door at the side. Bianca had texted ahead from the shop account — *materials issue on a piece from your June order, mind if I swing by and replace the backing? Will only take a minute.* The buyer had replied within four minutes: *Yes please, I love that one and I want it safe.* The phrasing in the reply had registered to me as the kind of mortal commitment that would yield in our favor if Bianca did not require it to.

Cai sat in the back seat. The recovery had not yet started and they were already conserving motion.

I turned to Bianca.

"You speak first. I follow."

"Yeah."

"If she asks me a question, I will not answer it. The follow-up will come from you."

"Got it."

"Cai stands behind me on the right. They will move only to receive the original after the swap."

"Yeah."

She tucked the folio under her arm. The replacement was inside it, glamoured. Mortal sight would not catch the swap. Mortal handling, on a piece that lived inside a frame on a wall, would not stress the working before we had moved on to the next house.

We went up.

The carpet in the corridor smelled like a cleaning product designed to mask other cleaning products. The wall paint had a faint grained texture where someone had skim-coated over an older surface. The doorframe to 214 had a small string of pine garland still pinned to it from a holiday two seasons ago. The mortal apartment building was, by every available metric, ordinary. The wound on the inside of unit 214 was not.

Bianca knocked.

She had set her face into the customer-service version she had used on me at her studio door yesterday morning, only now the register was warmth without effort — small, practiced, the smile a person used when they were genuinely glad to fix a thing for someone who had paid them. I watched her deploy it in real time. The mask was not new. The application of it to a stranger I was about to help her deceive was new.

The buyer opened the door.

She was perhaps thirty, in scrubs, with the bone-deep tiredness of a person at the end of a double shift. The recognition on her face when she saw Bianca was warm in a way I had not yet seen mortals deploy on a Tuesday afternoon.

"Oh good," she said. "You actually came. Come in, come in. Sorry, the apartment is a mess."

"Not a problem," Bianca said. "I'll be quick. This is my assistant Felix and my associate Cai."

Neither title was true. Neither was disputed.

The apartment was small and neat. A galley kitchen on the left, a living room on the right, framed prints on the wall in the careful arrangement of a person who had spent a Saturday with a level and a pencil. Cai's portrait hung above a narrow cabinet near the window. The light from a southern exposure caught it at an angle that would have been good for any portrait and was particularly cruel for an active original — the light made the name at the bottom legible from across the room.

Bianca did not look at it directly.

She walked to the frame and started talking about acid-free backing board and a bad spring batch and the way certain papers absorbed adhesives that could discolor the original over time. She used words that were either real or made up convincingly enough that the buyer nodded along with the steady patience of a person who did not need to understand the mechanics in order to be reassured.

I positioned myself between the buyer and Cai's body.

Cai stepped close enough to receive.

Bianca lifted the frame off the wall. She set it on the small narrow cabinet underneath. She turned it face-down. She unscrewed the backing tabs with a small flathead from her bag — efficient, practiced, the way a person opened a frame she had assembled herself.

"It's this," she said, peeling back the old backing board and showing the buyer a small ripple along one edge. "See how the paper's just starting to lift? Caught it in time. I'll mount it on the new board and it'll last decades."

"Oh my god, thank you for catching that."

The original came free of the backing. Bianca held it in the palm of one hand and the replacement in the other, both inside the small private architecture of her body. I laid the glamour fully into the replacement in the slew of motion that the buyer was watching. The buyer saw one portrait come out of the frame. Bianca's hands rearranged. The buyer saw the same portrait — corrected, restored, on the new board — slide back into the frame.

Cai had the original between two sheets of inert backing in the folio before Bianca had reseated the tabs.

The whole sequence took ninety seconds.

The buyer thanked us twice. Bianca declined twice the offer of water. The mortal courtesies played out at a pace that allowed me to count how often this woman would have noticed if any of us had been a fraction less precise.

We left.

In the stairwell, none of us spoke. In the parking lot, Bianca shook once — not from fear, from the effort of not having shaken inside the apartment.

I led us around to the side of the building where a strip of crushed gravel ran beside the dumpster enclosure. I raised a containment ward narrow enough to fit the three of us. Cai handed me the folio. I drew the original out by the dry edge. The name at the bottom was — for a beat — present, then absent. I burned it in the ward. The ash did not stain the gravel.

Cai exhaled. Their shoulders dropped by another fraction. I watched them put both palms briefly against the brick of the dumpster enclosure as if testing whether they were still standing.

"How much," I asked.

"More," Cai said. "Not enough."

"It will take time."

"I am aware."

They almost smiled. The almost was new in their face.

We went back to the car.

In the passenger seat I opened the export on my phone. The CSV had loaded into a working format that gave me filterable columns. I sorted by buyer name first — the standard mortal-commerce sort — and got the predictable scatter of one-time purchasers and a few enthusiastic repeats.

Then I sorted by shipping address.

The pattern arrived in the third row.

A buyer using a sequence of post-office boxes — different numbers, different town designations, the same buyer account. Purchases at a steady rhythm. The first eighteen months ago. The most recent eleven days ago. Seventeen purchases.

Seventeen.

A retail mortal collector did not behave like this.

The breach I had been holding as a distributed disaster sharpened into something with intent.

I looked out at the parking lot. Bianca was watching me through the windshield with her keys in her hand, deciding whether to ask. Cai was upright in the back seat with their hand flat on the brass-baluster keychain ornament on the passenger console — they had picked it up off the dash without asking and were turning it over the way a person handled a small grounding object.

"Felix," Cai said quietly.

I closed the laptop interface on the phone.

"This is not random," I said. "Eighteen months. Seventeen purchases. One buyer. Postal anonymity."

Cai did not need the rest of the sentence assembled.

"Whose names," they said.

I did not have the cross-reference yet. I had what I had carried in my body for a decade and now had a shape for: a patient, indirect, mortal-channel campaign against my court, run through a small-business artist who had not known what she was producing, by someone who had eighteen months of head start on me.

"Names I will know," I said. "When I cross-reference."

Bianca opened the driver's-side door. The sound of mortal traffic resumed in the car. She did not ask. She had read my face and decided to let me say the sentence when I had it.

"Drive," I said.

She did.

The first recovery had taken ninety seconds. The next four hundred and eleven were going to take longer, and a portion of them belonged to a person who already knew what they were collecting.
