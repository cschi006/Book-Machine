# Chapter 7 — Scene 3
**POV:** Bianca · **WC target:** 1,300 ±200 · **Tension:** 7→8 · **MIDPOINT POLARITY FLIP**

---

"I'm not leaving."

I said it to the room first. Not to Vael. Not to Felix. To the room — the small Loren kitchen with the hearth still going and the tea cooling on the table and Cai upright by the wall and Felix at the head and Vael at the foot in her storm-coat and her unsoftened bearing.

Then I looked at Vael, because she was the audience the words were procedurally for.

"For the record," I said. "I am not leaving. Felix offered me the choice. The hour was real. I am declining it."

Vael's face did not move. Her register did not bend. "On what basis."

"On the basis that I made these. Every single one. I made them in a state I did not understand. I made them in a state I have spent half my life calling something else. I sold them. I shipped them. I packaged them with branded stickers. I wrote thank-you cards. I have been the production line for eighteen months for a man who used my shop to build a targeted weapon against people who have done nothing to me, including a woman I have been drawing since I was six years old, twenty-three years ago, before I had a word for any of this."

I did not raise my voice. I did not need to. The kitchen held.

"I am not leaving because I will not have a version of this where I went back to my townhouse and let Cai's body keep paying. I am not leaving because I will not have a version of this where the fae who voted on a matter of justice are the ones who absorb the cost of what my uncle's revenge cleaved through my unknowing hand. I am not leaving because I drew Anwyll when I was six and I do not yet know what that means and I will not have a version of this where I find out later, by accident, in the safety of my own studio, while a man who looked like a polite neighbor finished what he started."

I put my hand flat on the table beside the drives.

"I am staying. Until the campaign closes. Until every original is destroyed. Until the man with the post-office boxes has a face we can put a name to. After which I will stand in front of whatever Loren court I am required to stand in front of and answer for what I did and did not know."

The silence after that was not the silence Vael liked. Vael liked silences that resolved her case. This one did not. This one held the kind of mortal-cleave a court of arrogance did not have a procedure for and was not going to pretend it did.

She regarded me.

"You understand," she said, "that this commitment is recorded."

"Yes."

"That it does not entitle you to any reduction in your eventual reckoning."

"Yes."

"That if you fail to honor it, you become a different kind of problem to this realm than you currently are."

"Yes."

"That if you do honor it, the realm is not obligated to thank you."

"Vael," I said. My voice came out level. "I am not asking for thanks. I am offering work. There is a difference."

The corner of her mouth did one thing, very precisely. It almost moved.

"So noted," she said.

Felix had not interrupted. He had not corrected. He had not added a softening clause that would have made my words easier for Vael to file. He had let the kitchen contain what I had put in it and had let Vael respond as Vael, which was the only response that would have been operationally useful.

Cai's hand was on the edge of the table where it had been when Vael arrived. They had not moved. The small unspecific weight of their being upright through this — for the second time today — registered to me without my having to look at them.

I did look at Felix.

He had been watching me. Of course he had. He did not move when I caught the look — did not break it, did not soften it. He let me see him seeing me. That was the closest thing to a sentence anyone in this kitchen had offered me since I had said *I'm not leaving,* and he had not had to use a single word for it.

"What now," I said. Operationally. Because operationally was where I could put my hand right now and not break the thing the kitchen was holding.

Vael answered before Felix.

"You begin with the post-office buyer."

"Yes," Felix said.

I drew the small clean ring around the buyer's account line on the working surface that Felix had not closed.

"We start there," I said. "He has been writing me casual messages on the shop for eighteen months. He has tracking numbers for every piece. He has an account history. He buys in patterns. We use the patterns. We make him reach for me."

"Bait," Cai said.

"Yes."

"On what scale."

"As small as he will accept. As public as we have to make it. We do not need him to come to a P.O. box. We need him to come to me."

Felix did not respond to that with a sentence. He responded by going very still, which was the same response he had used in the studio when Cai's portrait was on my desk and he had understood that one of these was not a number anymore. The stillness was not refusal. It was the kind of pause a man took when he was deciding what part of an operation he was willing to put a person he had begun to value into.

Vael said, "We will plan."

"We will plan," Felix said.

Cai picked up the cooling tea pot.

"I am pouring three cups," they said. "Felix can have the small one because I am annoyed with him."

Felix did not contest the small cup.

I sat down at the table for the first time since the conversation had started.

The cinch in my chest let go entirely.

Outside the kitchen window, the courtyard was full of late Loren-evening light — the slow filtered gold that did not quite belong to any mortal hour. The work in front of me was four hundred and three names long and contained a man with a face I would learn before this was over and a council I would stand in front of and a friend whose body was waiting on my hand. The work was not new. The decision was.

I had not gone home.

I had made the room my home was inside of bigger.
