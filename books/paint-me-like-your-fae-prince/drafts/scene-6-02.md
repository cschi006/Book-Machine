# Chapter 6 — Scene 2
**POV:** Felix · **WC target:** 1,300 ±200 · **Tension:** 7→6 · **Drift flag:** DISCOVERY — see notes

---

When Bianca arrived, she did not look like a person who had slept either. Her hair had been twisted into a knot at the back of her head and held by a pen, which I had filed yesterday as one of her habitual fixes. There was a smudge of ink along the inside of one wrist — the line a graphic designer made when she had been resting her hand against fresh marks on her own paper. The pen-knot was the gesture of someone who had spent the morning working without quite admitting it.

"You found paper," I said.

"I always have paper."

"In a Loren guest room."

"I had the back of a Loren guest-room laundry inventory, which a Loren housekeeper had left in my desk drawer."

"You stole from a Loren housekeeper."

"I borrowed."

The conversation almost landed lightly. It did not, because the chamber was the wrong room for light. She felt it as I did and dropped the register.

"What did you want me here for."

"The royal household drawings."

I did not preamble. Cai had brought in a slim stack from the archives during the night — Bianca's older work, organized by Vael's people, pulled from her flat files yesterday and from her listing photos this morning. The stack was old work; younger linework, less certain hand, more deliberate strokes — the work of a thirteen-year-old, fifteen-year-old, eighteen-year-old artist who did not yet have the channel as a refined instrument.

She came to the table. She did not sit. She looked at the pile and her face did the small thing it did when she had recognized something faster than she wanted to.

"Those are mine."

"Yes."

She put two fingers on the corner of the top page the way I had put two fingers on the corner of the petition the night before. The gesture was hers. I had been observing it for two days now in the small economies of how she handled her own work.

She turned the page.

It was Idris. Younger. Less the king than he had been in her stairwell-portrait — that one had been recent; this was older. The line was thinner. The shadow along the cheek had been laid with a teenager's attention. He looked unmistakably himself, and unmistakably unaware that he had been seen.

"How old were you," I said.

"That one — fourteen, maybe fifteen. There's a date on the back. There's a date on most of them. I was a meticulous kid."

She turned another. Another Idris. Different posture. Younger Bianca, again. Then another that was not Idris.

A woman.

I felt the chamber slide a half-degree off its axis.

The woman in the portrait was mortal. The set of the jaw, the asymmetry at the mouth that always meant laughter had unsettled the line, the small impatience at the eye. Bianca's gift had drawn the fae of my court with the unnerving completed-arrival quality of the channel. This page did not have that. It had a younger Bianca's evident craft applied to a person whose face she had not constructed in the half-sleep. The lines hesitated. The face had the looseness of mortal living.

"That's not a fae," she said. Quietly.

"No."

"Then how —"

"Look at the date."

She turned the page over.

The date was twenty-three years old. Bianca had been six.

The drawing was a child's. Skilled for six, but a child's. And the face on it, even rendered in a six-year-old's competence, was unmistakable to anyone who had spent the last fifteen years carrying the rest of her.

"Felix," Bianca said. Carefully.

"That is my mother."

"Anwyll."

"Yes."

She set the page down with the same careful gesture she had set the petition with. She did not ask the question. She let me decide whether to give it.

I gave it because the chamber had already decided for me.

"She was mortal. From a town on the western boundary. Before my father knew her, there was supposed to be a campaign through that valley — the court called it correction. The town had resisted a levy dispute and a border claim long enough to become an example. She stalled the delegation. My father was part of it. She argued with him in public, embarrassed one of his generals, refused to look impressed by anyone in the room."

"And the campaign never happened."

"No."

"And then she married him."

"In time."

The hiraeth in my voice was not a word I had ever named in mortal English. There was no precise mortal equivalent. The Welsh word — Cai's first mortal language, learned from a woman two centuries ago whose grandfather had taught Cai to speak — came closest. A longing for a home that could not be returned to, even when the home was a person, even when the person was your mother, even when she was twenty years dead.

Bianca did not ask the next question.

She waited.

"Several houses resented the peace," I said. "More resented a mortal queen with influence over a future king. My uncle resented both."

"He killed her."

"Yes."

"In the palace."

"Yes."

"And the exile vote came after."

"Yes."

She closed her eyes for a beat. Not long. The amount of time a person took when they did not want to perform on what they had just been told.

"Felix."

"Yes."

"I'm sorry."

The harbor of her register was the same one she had given Cai on the bench. Small, specific, handed across with no claim attached. I had been told *I am sorry* before, by people who had needed me to absorb their grief on behalf of an institution, and I had absorbed it. This was a different transaction.

I let it sit there a beat longer than I had planned to.

"Thank you," I said.

She did not move. The pen-knot at the back of her hair had loosened during the conversation; the small graphite mark on her wrist had migrated to the side of her thumb the way it had migrated yesterday morning. She did not seem aware. She was holding the page with Anwyll on it and looking at her own work as if she had drawn it last week.

"I don't remember making it," she said.

"You were six."

"I don't remember any of the half-sleep pieces. I have never remembered them. But that one — I would have remembered if I'd drawn her on purpose. She doesn't look like a half-sleep piece."

"She was not a fae."

"So my channel didn't —"

"No."

"Then what."

I did not have the answer. The channel produced fae. Anwyll was mortal. There was no rule in the system that explained why a six-year-old Bianca in a town a thousand miles from Loren had drawn the woman who would later die in a hallway of my father's palace at the hands of his brother.

"I do not know," I said.

We stayed at the table with that for a long while. Cai did not come in to interrupt. The chamber's high windows oscillated through the colors I had begun to register as Loren-evening. The page of Anwyll lay between us. Bianca did not touch it again.

When she finally spoke, it was practical. The way she did when emotion had gone too high to handle in the same channel.

"What do we do with this."

"I do not know yet."

"That is not your default answer."

"No."

She let that stand.
