# Chapter 10 — Felix POV (Visible)

## Scene 1

She told me everything about her neighbor.

The list was short. Retired veteran. Quiet. Brought over misdelivered packages once a month. Had once asked about her shipping volume. Was politically unplaceable. Had a truck. Did not have visible relatives. Took in his own mail. Mowed his own lawn. Was, by every functional category Bianca had used to file him for the four years she had lived next door, exactly the kind of mortal background detail her life had run inside without supervision.

We sat in the annex parking lot until she had finished the list. Cai watched her work the small unspecific weight of the realization through her body in stages — she went still, she went paler, she went quiet, she came back to operational register, and by the end of the list she had moved through the kind of internal recalibration I had watched her do twice already in two days and had stopped trying to mistake for anything else.

She looked at me when she was done.

"It's not him."

"Bianca."

"It's not. It can't be. The math doesn't work. Mr. Terry is a real person. He has a real truck. He has been my neighbor for four years. He cannot also be my fae-realm great-uncle running a magical-arms collection out of a P.O. box."

"He can be exactly that."

"Felix."

"My uncle was stripped of magic in exile. He was not stripped of mortal time. He was forced to live on a mortal timeline for the first time in his life. He had eighteen years to learn what mortal infrastructure looks like before he found your shop. He had four years to be your neighbor."

"He cannot have been my neighbor for four years."

"He has been your neighbor for as long as the math required him to be."

She was quiet for a long beat. I did not push. The fact-set had to land in her own body or the operation would not survive the next phase.

"Okay," she said.

"Okay."

"What do we do."

"We do not go back to your house tonight."

"He sees us not going back to my house and he knows."

"He sees us going back to your house at all and he knows."

"So we — what. Drive."

"We drive. We use a hotel. We use the Loren guest rooms. We use whatever does not let him watch the front of your townhouse from his living room window."

She nodded.

We did not drive back.

Cai used a glamour on the car so it did not register to anyone watching the route. I had not asked Cai to expend the magic. Cai had done it before I had finished the calculation for whether to ask. Their reserves were better than they had been three days ago by the count we had been collecting. The reserves were not unlimited. The reserves would not survive the kind of operation that was now lining itself up.

We went to a hotel I had paid for in cash an hour later.

Bianca sat on the bed in her room and did not unpack the small overnight bag she had been carrying for two days and held her phone in her hand like a weapon she had not yet decided how to use.

"I want to call Mara," she said.

"Call Mara."

She did.

## Scene 2

Mara was at the hotel in forty minutes.

I had not expected the speed. Bianca had not warned her, had not given her an address, had given her exactly the message *I'm safe, I'm in town, I need you, come to a hotel I'll send you the name when you're in the car,* and Mara had taken that as the operational sentence it was meant to be and had not asked any of the questions that would have slowed her down.

She arrived with a duffel bag, a coffee for Bianca, and the kind of efficient stillness I had begun to associate with a person who had spent her professional life managing crises in venues with bouncers.

She did not look at me when she came in. She looked at Bianca. She did the small visual inspection an old friend did when checking whether the body she had known for two decades was the same body in a hotel room she had not been told the address of until twenty minutes ago. Then she looked at Cai, who had taken the chair in the corner. Then she looked at me.

"You must be Felix."

"Yes."

"Cai."

"Hello, Mara."

She did not extend a hand to either of us. She set the duffel on the floor.

"Okay," she said to the room. "What is happening."

Bianca told her.

It took twenty-three minutes. She told the long version — the half-sleep, the channel, the active originals, the burn, the rules, the targeting, the exile, Anwyll, the P.O. box buyer, the neighbor, the parking lot, the hotel. She did not perform on any of it. She did not work the customer-service voice. She gave Mara the version of the sentence she would have given herself, if she had been her own best friend, and Mara absorbed it the way she would have absorbed any difficult mortal information delivered by someone she had decided long ago to believe by default.

When Bianca was done, Mara was quiet for one breath.

Then she said, "Mr. Terry."

"Yes."

"The same Mr. Terry who brought over your Amazon package in May."

"Yes."

"Who you have texted me about being a *sweet old veteran* exactly twice."

"Yes."

"Bianca."

"I know."

"Bianca."

"I know."

Mara turned her head toward me. She did not stand up. She did the visual sort I had watched her perform on Bianca, on me again, more thoroughly this time.

"You're the one running the operation."

"Yes."

"And you have not, in two days of operating, identified that the source's *literal next-door neighbor* is the most likely candidate for your buyer."

"Until forty-five minutes ago, no."

She made a sound. Not contempt. Something more practical than contempt. "Okay. Walk me through what you have."

I walked her through it.

She listened without interrupting. When I was done, she did not pretend to know more than she did. She did, however, know mortal logistics in a way that no one in the room except Bianca did, and she had been managing a venue for fourteen years.

"He is not going to come out of his house," she said.

"He is not."

"Not for a stakeout. Not for a P.O. box. Not for anything you can engineer at the perimeter."

"No."

"So you need to bring him out."

"Yes."

"With Bianca."

"Yes."

"In public."

"Yes."

She nodded slowly. "How public."

"Public enough that he reads the appearance as opportunity. Private enough that I can keep eyes on every threshold."

She thought.

She turned back to Bianca. "The convention."

Bianca closed her eyes.

I had not seen the listing yet. I did not yet know that Bianca had spent four years not going to the convention Mara was about to name. I had spent two days not yet meeting the part of Bianca's mortal life that would, when she opened her eyes, have to look directly at the venue she had specifically built a life shaped against attending.

"The Eastside Artist & Fan Expo," Mara said. "Three weeks from now. Artist alley. They've been begging her to take a table for six years."

"I have not been ignoring those emails *that much,*" Bianca said. Without opening her eyes.

"You have been ignoring them exactly that much."

"Mara —"

"I'll work the table. I'll handle the public. I'll bring you in through service. He will not be able to get within ten feet of you that you have not pre-cleared. And he will come."

"Mara, I —"

"You don't have to like it. You have to decide whether it works."

I waited.

Bianca opened her eyes.

She looked at me. Then at Cai. Then at Mara.

"It works," she said.

## Scene 3

I rejected the convention plan.

I rejected it on operational grounds. I rejected it on threshold-management grounds. I rejected it on the basis that there was no version of a public event in a mortal convention center with thousands of bodies passing through it where I could control the perimeter to my satisfaction. I rejected it on the basis that the man we were trying to flush out had spent eighteen months building his approach to Bianca around her shop architecture, and a convention appearance was the kind of variable he would adjust to in a week.

I rejected it for twenty minutes.

Then I accepted it.

I accepted it because Bianca had said it worked. I accepted it because Mara had given me an operational plan that did, in fact, narrow the perimeter to a defensible set of thresholds. I accepted it because the alternative was a slower campaign that would, by the count we had, take three more weeks of recoveries at the current pace, and the count was no longer the constraint. Cai was no longer the constraint. The constraint was the man with the P.O. boxes who had just spent nine days reading the silence of Bianca's shop and had clearly decided to use the silence as data.

He would adjust. We could not afford to give him the time.

I told Bianca my decision. She did not look surprised. She had been waiting for it, in the small efficient way she had been waiting for a number of my decisions across the last two days.

Mara opened her laptop. We started planning.

We planned for the rest of the night.

By midnight, Mara had emailed the convention organizer with the message *Bianca Lawson would like to confirm her artist alley table this year, please advise on remaining booth options.* The reply landed in twenty-six minutes. The organizer's voice in the email was the voice of a person who had been hoping Bianca would say yes for six years and was now hoping in a different direction. *Of course. Booth B-14, end of row, easy access to the service hall. Need anything else, just ask.*

Booth B-14. End of row. Easy access to service hall. Mara had identified it on the floor plan in three minutes of looking.

Bianca went to sleep at one in the morning.

I did not sleep.

Mara stayed up with me at the small hotel table going through the booth map and the convention center floor plan and the threshold count and the schedule of opening and closing hours. She did not, at any point, ask me how I felt about Bianca. She did not, at any point, suggest I should rest. She worked the operational version of the problem with the kind of focused attention that did not require me to be anything other than the person also working it.

When she finally went to bed at three, she stopped on the way to the door.

"You're better than I expected," she said.

"At what."

"Letting her decide."

She closed the door behind her.

I sat with the booth map for the rest of the night.

At 6:14 a.m. my phone — the mortal one Bianca had set up for me yesterday for the operation — chimed with a notification from the shop account she had given me read access to.

A new message in the buyer's thread.

*Looking forward to meeting you in person.*

He knew.
