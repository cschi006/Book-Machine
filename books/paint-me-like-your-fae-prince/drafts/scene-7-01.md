# Chapter 7 — Scene 1
**POV:** Bianca · **WC target:** 1,400 ±200 · **Tension:** 7→7 (interior choice)
**Seeds (chapter pool):** char · bobbin · reveal · aureole · nape · cinch · cleave · pediment · current · tension · necrotic · slubbed · skeined · clavicle · hoarfrost

---

The portal closed behind me with the small mechanical click I had stopped flinching at, and my kitchen was mine again. Which was the wrong thing.

I had been gone less than two days. The mail on the entry table was where I had put it. The coffee maker's standby light was the same small green pulse. The refrigerator did its compressor cycle at the regular interval. Everything in this house had continued without me with the courtesy of an institution that did not need me to function.

That was new information. I had spent four years building a life that would run without supervision so I could disappear into the channel safely. It had run without supervision. It had run without me. And the version of *home* I had been clinging to in the chamber an hour ago had, in the actual house, dissolved into something I could not quite locate.

I stood in my own kitchen and felt the small uncatalogued grief of a person who had built a perfectly functioning exit and was no longer sure she wanted to use it.

I had an hour.

I went upstairs.

The studio looked the same. Felix's pale containment marks were still on the drawers. The lamp was angled where I had left it. The hardbacked notebooks were stacked along the bottom of the wall. The packing bench held the same five-orders-ready-to-ship and the same printout with the cotter-pin clip that I had been about to attach to a thank-you card forty-eight hours ago.

I set the Anwyll page on the worktable.

I did not turn the lamp on. The afternoon was bright enough through the studio window to draw by, and turning the lamp on would have been a commitment to a longer session than the hour Felix had given me.

I sat.

I looked at the Anwyll page.

I had drawn her at six. The hand was a child's. I had not yet been the person who would build a small business around the channel, would catalog the half-sleep originals as *special sketches,* would write Caelith Noct-Aravain at the bottom of a portrait of a person whose existence I would not be told about for another sixteen years. I had been a kid with a pencil and a face in my head, and I had drawn it the way kids draw faces they have decided are important.

Anwyll's small impatience at the eye. The asymmetry at the mouth. The looseness mortal living puts in a face that fae-living does not.

I had been seeing her since before I knew what seeing was.

That was the piece I had not been able to put down in the chamber. Not the murder. Not Evander. Not the targeting. The slow specific fact that the connection between me and this family ran deeper than the channel had ever explained — that whatever I was, I had been it before I had a vocabulary for any of it, and the woman whose death had structured Felix's entire life had been in my private gallery before I could long-divide.

I did not know what that meant.

I knew it was not a thing I could carry to a Loren guest room and put on a shelf and forget.

I looked at my desk.

My laptop was on standby. The label printer had not been run in two days. The shipping scale was where I had left it. The good Bristol was clipped into the upper-right cabinet where I kept the unstamped stock. There was a half-eaten energy bar I had abandoned on Tuesday whose wrapper had begun to slubbed at the open end where the foil had started to fatigue.

I could pack a bag.

I could put the Anwyll page in a portfolio, lock the studio with Felix's marks still on it, sit on the couch with Mara on the phone, and wait for the Loren guard Felix had offered me to take the position at the curb that Mr. Terry was currently in his living room not noticing. I could wait out the recovery campaign. I could not go to court. I could not speak before Vael's reckoning. I could be a mortal someone else managed, with the small mercy of plausible deniability about Cai's count and the household-officer cluster and the four hundred and nine names I would not have to watch get burned.

The hour Felix had given me was not, structurally, designed to make me choose the campaign. The hour was the actual offer. He was a man who had ruled a kingdom for fifteen years; he understood that an exit you did not give freely was a hostage you were holding in a more polite chair.

He had given me the exit freely.

The exit was sitting in front of me on the desk like a tool.

I picked up my phone instead.

I opened my laptop. I made coffee on the way back to the desk. I sat with the cup against my clavicle because the warmth was something to brace against. I plugged in the external drive labeled SHOP EXPORT — the one I had used yesterday — and reformatted it. I plugged in the second one. I plugged in the third one because two backups felt negligent and three felt like a person who had stopped pretending she was going to be unreachable for a while.

I exported the full order history. I exported the message archive. I exported every listing photo. I exported the customer notes, the payment records, the shipping label history, the analytics. I exported everything Etsy would give me and a second set of everything Etsy would not give me but my own spreadsheet had quietly accumulated alongside it. I labeled the drives. I wrote a paper key by hand so the folder structure would survive somebody else opening it.

The current of doing the work pulled me through the next forty minutes without my having to ask for it.

When I had three labeled drives and a written key, I picked up the good Bristol from the cabinet.

I clipped a fresh sheet to the board.

The half-sleep state did not arrive. The channel did not open. That was the point.

I opened the archived listing photo for the next local buyer — a piece I had shipped in 2022 to an apartment four miles from here — and started drawing the replacement awake. From the photo. With my hand. The hand that had drawn Anwyll at six and Cai's portrait two nights ago and now a copy of one of my own pieces in the bright afternoon light of my own studio.

I drew through the small ache at the nape of my neck where the bench at the park had left a hoarfrost-thin stiffness. I drew through the cinch in my chest that had not let go since the chamber. I drew through the page until the replacement looked enough like the original to pass under Felix's glamour.

When I clipped it to the rack to dry, the hour was nearly up.

I looked at the labeled drives. I looked at the replacement. I looked at the Anwyll page on the worktable.

I picked up a portfolio. I put the drives in one pocket of my bag. I put the Anwyll page in a sleeve and laid it on top. I put the replacement on top of that.

I went downstairs.

The portal had not yet opened. Felix had given me an hour and the hour was almost over and he was the kind of person who waited the additional minute rather than miscount.

I stood in my kitchen with the bag in one hand and the keys to my front door in the other and felt the small administrative grief of a person who had decided to bring her tools and not bring the version of herself who had been intending, until thirty minutes ago, to use them as protective architecture.

The portal opened.

The seam was the same vertical cut of Loren-light I had stopped flinching at.

I went through.
