# Chapter 3 — Scene 2
**POV:** Bianca
**Word count target:** 1,500 (±200)
**Entry tension:** 7 · **Exit tension:** 8
**Seeds:** mare's tail · virga · foehn · rubatosis · pine

---

She came back with a shallow bronze dish, not a page.

The page was Felix's. He had been holding it the entire time — the wrapped portrait of Cai he had carried out of my studio thirty minutes and one universe ago. He set the bronze dish in the center of the dark stone table. He unwrapped the portrait. He laid it on the stone.

The room tightened around it.

Cai's face on the page. The same dark sweep of brow. The same mouth set like a thought being kept on purpose. The name in dried ink at the bottom — *Caelith Noct-Aravain* — visible to anyone who could read.

I made the sound a person makes when their brain takes in input it has no folder for. Halfway between a laugh and a hiccup.

"What is happening."

Felix put two fingers beside the inscription, not on it. "What is written there is Cai's true name."

That landed harder than I had expected, given that I had been on a porch ten minutes ago telling a man with too much posture to leave my property.

I folded my arms because the alternative was letting my hands do something useful. "In what universe."

"In ours."

"It is a fake fantasy name. I have been writing the kind of thing under drawings since middle school."

"You have been writing real names under real drawings since middle school," Felix said. "You did not know. That changes nothing about the rules."

I looked at Cai.

Cai met my eyes with a steadiness that was worse than apology would have been. "He is not lying."

"I would prefer it if he were."

"I would prefer that too."

Felix went on, evenly. "A true name is the source of a fae's power. To compromise the name is to compromise the person."

"That sounds fake even in a magical kidnapping palace."

"It is older than your opinion of it."

"That is true of most terrible systems."

Cai's mouth did the small thing that was almost a laugh, and something — somewhere down the room — relaxed by a degree that did not have a name.

Vael did not move.

"Define *compromise*," I said.

"You make what we call an active original," Felix said. "Face and name, drawn through the state that takes you. That page becomes the link. Conscious art does not do this."

I tried to file that. The information sat on the surface of my brain like a hot coal I had picked up by accident.

He kept going. "Each active original weakens the named fae. The more originals exist, the more diluted the power. If a fae physically meets a person who holds an active original of them, the fae cannot use magic against the holder. At all."

"That seems like an aggressively terrible system."

"It is the system."

"I'm not making policy critiques. I'm asking what kind of system actively disarms its own people."

"Felix," Cai said quietly. It was almost the same tone they had used in my living room — *be nice* — only now their hands were folded together in their lap and the soft accommodation in the gesture was differently weighted.

Felix continued. "Beyond the shield, the named fae is drawn toward the holder. Trust. Warmth. Favorable judgment. The bond is involuntary. It strengthens with contact. It cannot be turned off."

The room did the rearranging thing again. Not in mythic terms. In practical ones. A sheet of paper. A face. A name. A person walking into a room and becoming, against their will, easier to like.

I did not look at Cai.

I made myself not look at Cai.

There was a column of the situation I had not yet permitted into the visible architecture of the room, and it was a column I was going to put back where I had found it for the foreseeable future. Cai had brought me water with a straw. Cai had put a pillow on the floor for the back of someone who had not asked. Cai had named what was happening in their body when I would not have known how to ask for that information. None of those facts had to mean what they had felt like meaning. I was going to think about that later.

"Right," I said. "Okay."

"Watch," Felix said.

He did not need to ask Vael for the dish; she had already placed it. He angled the portrait into it. He touched the edge of the paper with two fingers.

A pale line of fire caught.

I watched the material first, because that was what my brain could still process. The paper curled. The ink thickened, glossed for one second, blackened. The shadow I had laid along Cai's jaw broke apart. The written name at the bottom shrank into a distortion. The air over the dish did a small uneven shimmer — a kind of virga, water that did not arrive — and then settled.

The room changed.

No thunder. No light. Just a quiet release in the air, like a muscle unclenching when I had not known it had been clenched. Cai inhaled hard enough that I heard it across the table. When I looked, their shoulders had dropped by a fraction. The slipping at the edges of their face tightened — not gone, but settled — like a bad connection finally seated.

They put a hand flat on the stone beside them and looked at their own fingers with quiet concentration.

Felix watched them, not me. "How much."

"Measurable," Cai said after a moment. "Not enough."

Their voice had more weight in it than before.

I looked at the dish. The page was collapsing inward, black and fragile. My eyes went to the bottom of the page, to the name I had read twice in this room, trying to hold the letters in place by sheer stubbornness.

It was there.

Then it wasn't.

Not visually. The ash was still ash. But when I reached for the exact letters in my head, my mind closed over nothing. Not dizzy. Not a swoop. Just a clean, practical absence where information had been thirty seconds before. The awareness of my own pulse came up in its place — *rubatosis,* my therapist would have called it, the unsettling presence of your own heartbeat in a quiet room — and I made myself breathe.

"You felt that," Cai said.

I looked at them. Their attention had a different quality now. Not less. Different. Like they had been holding a chair under me without telling me, and the chair had been removed, and I had not, in fact, needed it. The easy soft thing was not in the room. Normal attention was. Whatever Cai had been to me twenty minutes ago was not the same thing they were now.

I would think about that later. I was making a long list of things to think about later.

"The name is gone," I said.

"From memory," Felix said. "The link ends when the original is destroyed. The page's exact contents go hard to retain unless they were preserved somewhere inert. A photograph. A scan. Anything that is not the original."

I had not preserved it.

The name was gone.

I had drawn the same face hundreds of times, and I had had a name for it for less than twelve hours.

"That removed one wound," Felix said. "If other originals of Cai exist, they remain active until destroyed."

Cai pushed off the chair and came around to the side of the table closer to me. They had more color than they had on my porch. Not enough. *Less bad,* as a category, was apparently going to be how I measured things here.

"I can breathe more easily," they said. "I am not restored."

No one in the room raised their voice. No one needed to.

I picked up the water on the table because my mouth had gone dry enough to make speaking irritating. The stone of the cup sweated cold against my fingers.

When I set it down, the questions were already in line.

"What counts. Exactly. Do digital images do this."

"No."

"Photographs."

"No."

"Prints. Scans. Anything reproduced from the original."

"Inert," Cai said. "They preserve the image. Not the link."

"And if I draw the same face. Awake. On purpose. With the name."

Felix's gaze stayed on mine. "That is ordinary art."

I let air back into my lungs. Half a breath. The other half stayed lodged somewhere behind my sternum, because my brain had skipped — traitorously — to the part of the inventory that was no longer physically in my house.

Hundreds of physical originals that I had mailed to strangers since 2021.

I closed that compartment hard.

"What activates it," I said. "The state. The paper. The ink. The name."

"We do not fully know why you can do it," Felix said. "We know what makes the original active. The state. The paper. The name. Your conscious work does not do this unless the channel takes you first."

*Channel.* My lifelong private disaster had acquired a technical term while I had not been looking.

I paced once, three steps to the wall, three back. The room was small enough to keep it from looking dramatic. I liked that about it.

Vael spoke for the first time since the page had gone on the table.

"How many originals are still in your possession."

I stopped.

"I don't know."

"Estimate."

The studio. The flat files. The shelves. The drawers Felix had sealed with his sigils. The archive boxes. The hardback notebooks. The stack on the packing table. The framed pieces leaning two-deep against the wall. None of the outgoing mailers, because those were not in the house anymore, but Vael did not know I had outgoing mailers, and I was choosing not to give her the math on that in this room.

"A lot," I said. "Hundreds. I have been doing this since I was thirteen."

Vael's eyes moved to Felix. Felix's expression did not change.

"I need every active original still in your possession," he said.

*Still in your possession.*

It hit so cleanly I almost looked away from him.

He thought the perimeter of the problem was my house. He had sealed the drawers. He had wrapped the freshest active piece in inert backing and put it in a dish and burned it. He believed he had inventoried the wound.

He did not know about the mailers. He did not know about the buyers. He did not know that the part of my brain I had spent years not examining had also, with the help of a Square account and a label printer, been distributing magical artifacts in padded envelopes across most of North America.

Old habit narrowed my thoughts down. Smallest survivable truth. Keep the room manageable. One compartment at a time.

I pictured my townhouse. The sealed studio. The drawers. The shelves. The evidence still there.

"I can show you what I have at home," I said.

The lie was clean.

The omission was enormous.

Cai's gaze flicked once to mine and held.

For one terrible second I thought they were going to say something.

They did not.

Their face had stopped doing the soft thing. The compulsion was gone. They had clocked the omission, and whatever they did next, they would do as themselves.

Vael said, "Then we begin there."

She turned toward the door without waiting to be agreed with.

The page in the dish had stopped being a page some time ago. The ash inside the bronze did not have the courtesy to retain a shape. Felix did not look at the dish again. He did not look at me. He looked at Cai for one beat that contained more than the room had seen so far in this conversation, and Cai's mouth did a small thing that was not relief and not exhaustion but something fae had a word for and I did not.

Then he followed Vael out.
