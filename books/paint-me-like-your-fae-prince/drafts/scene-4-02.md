# Chapter 4 — Scene 2
**POV:** Felix
**Word count target:** 1,300 (±200)
**Entry tension:** 8 · **Exit tension:** 7
**Seeds:** calloused · hiraeth · cordite · baluster · unspool

---

The export took eleven minutes.

Bianca worked through her shop backend without lifting her eyes from the screen. Her fingers were stained at the side of one hand where the ink had not come off, and the keyboard registered the stain in faint smudges along the spacebar that she did not seem to notice. She produced the CSV. She produced the message archive. She produced the shipping label history. She produced the photographs — every listing image, the entire archive of how she had documented each piece before it left her hand.

The photographs were what mattered.

I had not asked her to pull them. She had pulled them as a matter of professional reflex; she ran a business, the documentation was already attached to the orders, the export carried it. She slid the laptop two inches in my direction without speaking.

I turned the screen.

Names. Faces. Dates. Tracking numbers. Buyer notes in a chatty register that read in mortal English as enthusiasm: *can't wait to see this in person, thank you so much, will display where the light catches it.* The cheerful infrastructure of every mortal harm we were now going to undo.

"Inert," Cai said quietly from beside me.

"You are certain."

"The photographs preserve the image. Not the link. The original is the wound. The image of the original is a memory of a wound."

That confirmed the operating parameter for what I was about to propose. I closed the export and turned to Bianca.

"We cannot ask the buyers to give them back."

She looked at me. "I figured."

"If we tell mortals these are weapons, we widen the breach. If we tell them nothing, we have to reach the originals without their knowledge."

"Right."

"Recommend a method."

The instruction landed strangely between us. I watched her register that the next sentence was hers to write, not mine. She rubbed the side of her thumb against the keyboard where the ink stain was.

"I redraw them," she said. "Awake. From the listing photos. The conscious work is inert. We swap the original for the replacement on-site and burn the original on the way out. Buyers see a piece on the wall. They don't see a change."

I had been working through the same protocol for the last three minutes from a different angle. She had arrived at it faster, because she had arrived at it from inside her own commercial logic — she had spent four years thinking about how to make her work look identical in the mailers she sent to buyers and the proofs she kept in her studio.

"Glamour over the replacement," I said. "To bend mortal perception until the eye accepts it as the original. Necessary because the channel work and the conscious work do not feel the same to a buyer who has lived with it."

"They feel different."

"Yes."

She considered that. The room was quiet enough that I could hear the small click of her tongue against her teeth — a tic of consideration I had not catalogued.

"How long does the glamour hold."

"On a stationary object in a private home, indefinitely. On an active item under daily handling, weeks before it needs renewal. We will be working faster than the renewal window."

"Okay."

Cai said, "Local first."

"Yes."

Bianca pulled the filter back up. *Special Sketch Archive.* She added a location filter that I had not asked for — the kind of filter a buyer's-side platform should not have offered, but a seller-side analytics dashboard apparently did. The grid reorganized by ship-to ZIP code. The first result was an apartment building nine minutes away by car, and the thumbnail above the buyer name was Cai's face.

The room did not need me to point that out.

"Her," Bianca said. "She bought two years ago. Local. Easy access. I have her address from the order."

I looked at Cai. The slipping at the edges of their face had tightened over the last hour — the burn in the chamber had bought them measurable air — but I could feel the cost of the buyer's existence under their name like a small steady drain. Cai had named one of thirty-one, and we were about to remove one. The arithmetic was insulting to anyone who had asked me to wait two years to know it.

"Draw the replacement," I said.

Bianca went upstairs. She came back with a sheet of paper, two pens, an eraser, and the resignation of a person who had agreed to do a thing she had told herself she would never do — make conscious art of a face she had drawn in her sleep.

She sat at the dining table. She opened the archived listing photo on her phone, propped it against a small ceramic dish, and started.

I had not watched her draw before.

The work was slow. She blocked the head shape. She checked the reference. She corrected the jaw. She erased. She began again. Her hand stopped twice — once to flex the fingers, once for no visible reason that registered as anything but craft. The graphite gathered at her wrist in small calloused drifts of eraser dust. The page accumulated the visible decisions of a person making it. Her shoulders dropped a fraction lower than they had been since the chamber.

She was *good.*

That was inconvenient information.

I watched the deliberate work of her hand for forty minutes and built a working framework around what I was seeing. The conscious work was hers. It carried the texture of choice — the corrections, the false starts, the small dry humor she had let into the fox-and-teapot piece in her shop. The channel work was something else. The active originals I had pulled from her flat files had arrived complete: no erasures, no corrections, no evidence of line becoming line. A finished face on a piece of paper. A name written under it in a hand that was hers but not — too steady, too certain, too produced by a process she had not been awake for.

The channel did not belong to her. That was new.

She had not chosen it.

She had only carried it.

Cai noticed me noticing. They did not say anything. They had moved to a position behind my chair and slightly to the left where they could see the page without crowding her. They watched her work the way they watched any of the small repairs the world allowed; quietly, with the kind of attention that did not require acknowledgment.

When Bianca finished, she did not show me the replacement until she had inked over it with a brush pen that softened the corrections. She let the ink set. She squared the corners. She slid the page across the table.

It looked enough like Cai. It did not feel like Cai.

I laid my hand over it and threaded the glamour through.

The working settled into the paper the way it usually did into mortal artifacts — not deeply, only enough to bend the surface. Mortal sight would not catch the swap. The buyer would handle a replacement that had been laid by Bianca's hand in our dining room and not know she had ever handled the original.

"It will pass," I said.

"For how long."

"Long enough."

She nodded. She did not ask the question I had braced for — *what happens to the buyer's understanding of what she owns* — because she had already built the answer into her own commercial instincts. The buyer would keep what she thought she had bought. The buyer would never know what had been taken from her wall. The harm would be reabsorbed by the world quietly enough that no one in mortal space had to lose anything visible.

Cai picked up the wrapped original. They held it the way a person held a small live animal that had stopped struggling. The name on the bottom of the page — the one we were about to take away from them again, this time for good — bit faintly through the inert backing the way the freshest active piece had bit through the backing yesterday morning.

I closed the laptop.

"Drive," I said.

Bianca picked up her bag. The keys clattered against a small metal baluster ornament hanging off her keychain that I had not noticed before — a tiny brass spindle, decorative, hers — and the sound was domestic enough that for one short beat I had to relocate myself in the room.

The mortal who had spent four years distributing weapons her hand made but did not author was a different problem than the mortal I had brought through a portal yesterday.

I had not yet decided what kind of problem.

But the problem was no longer *the breach.*

It was *Bianca.*
