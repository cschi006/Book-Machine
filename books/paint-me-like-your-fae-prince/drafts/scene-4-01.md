# Chapter 4 — Scene 1
**POV:** Felix
**Word count target:** 1,300 (±200) — Felix POV runs lean
**Entry tension:** 9 · **Exit tension:** 8 — pivot from external crisis to internal recalibration
**Seeds:** incandescent · falter · liget · oscillate · pepper

---

*Hundreds* sat in the air long enough to require a containment decision.

I made it without speaking.

The first calculation was political. Four years. Distributed mortal infrastructure. Multiple buyer addresses. The exfiltration vector my uncle could have used if he had been clever — he was clever — was no longer theoretical. The second calculation was the one I did not let move my face: Cai's count, which I had suspected was higher than they admitted, was about to acquire context Cai could not afford. The third was procedural. The studio was no longer the perimeter of anything. The perimeter was the United States Postal Service and the courier networks that fed it.

I closed the flat file I had opened thirty seconds earlier. The drawer slid with the same small grit of metal. The sound landed strangely now that the room had been redefined around it.

Bianca had not moved. Her hand was still on the edge of the flat file where I had laid the first active piece. Her phone was in her other hand. The Etsy chime had fired twice since *Hundreds.*

"Laptop," I said.

She blinked. "What."

"Your shop runs on a laptop. Bring it. Downstairs."

There was a flicker through her face — the registration of an order she had not had time to prepare against — and then a small, dry rearrangement.

"Yeah," she said. "Okay."

She took the laptop from the side desk and the charger looped around her arm and started for the door. The phone stayed in her hand. Cai, who had not crossed the threshold of the studio, watched her pass with the kind of attention I associated with Cai noting something they would tell me later.

I followed.

In the kitchen the light was different than I had registered earlier — afternoon now, and the coffee on the warmer had stopped being a smell and started being a low note. Bianca set the laptop on her dining table. She did not sit. She opened the lid. The screen lit up with the muted incandescence of a workspace returning to a working person.

She typed something. The shop backend loaded. I had not asked what it would look like. She turned the screen toward me without being asked, which was a small concession I logged because it would matter later.

The shop interface was offensively cheerful.

A row of pastel category buttons across the top: *prints, pins, originals, stickers, ALL.* A featured image of a woodland scene rendered in soft ink — pretty, deliberately so, not the work my hand recognized as the channel. Below: a grid of listings. Each one a small thumbnail with a price, a star rating, a count of sales.

The count of sales.

I read the totals on the visible row. Twenty-eight. Forty-one. Twelve. Sixty-three. Eighty-nine. The numbers oscillated through ordinary commercial range. They became the wrong information almost immediately, because they were aggregate — not per-listing volume but cumulative across years — and I did not yet have the filter that mattered.

"Show me the special sketches only," I said.

"Filter button," she said. Her voice came out clipped. Not from contempt. From the effort of not crying about the wrong thing. "Top right. *Special Sketch Archive.*"

I clicked.

The grid reorganized.

*Hundreds.*

The number was not a metaphor and it was not a guess. The page reported it at the bottom corner, in small clean text: *412 completed listings.*

Four hundred and twelve.

I held the count for two breaths. Bianca was not looking at the screen. She had moved to the kitchen counter and put both hands flat against it. Her phone faced down. The set of her shoulders had become the set of a person who had decided not to throw up.

I would file that for later, with the other things.

I scrolled.

The thumbnails went on. And on. Each one a small magical wound photographed in good light and priced in mortal money and shipped on a Tuesday. *Original ink portrait — Caelith Noct'Aravain.* *Original ink portrait — Sylven.* *Original ink portrait — Branwych.* Names I knew. Names I had heard at court a decade ago. Names from the older roster I had not thought about since taking on the work of ruling. Some were people I had served beside. One was a councillor whose retirement I had personally signed off on.

The grid did not falter under my scrolling. It went on. *And on.*

Cai had come into the kitchen behind me. They did not need to read over my shoulder; the names rose off the screen in the way names did when the linkage was alive. They put a hand flat on the back of a chair. They said nothing.

I scrolled until the listings thinned out at the end — the earliest entries, from four years ago, before she had refined her packaging or her photography or her pricing. The first half-dozen looked like the work of a small-business owner who had not yet decided this was a business. The names underneath looked like the work of someone who had been doing this since she was thirteen.

I did not yet have the inventory of who held the names she had sold. I had the count.

The count was a war.

I scrolled back up.

I was about to ask her for the order export when I clicked the wrong category and the page jumped to *prints.*

What loaded there stopped me for a beat longer than I wanted to give it.

It was art.

Not the channel. Not the half-sleep work. Not the live-wound originals that read as exposed names to anyone trained to feel them. It was a small grid of consciously made illustrations: a fox curled around a teapot, a small reading lamp under a soft window, a row of paint tubes in colors that did not exist in fae light. Designs. The deliberate work of an artist. A storefront tagline at the top of the section, in a font she had chosen on purpose: *Bianca makes things. Some of them go in your apartment.*

I had built a category for her in my head over the last day. *The mortal. The source. The breach.* The category had been useful. It had let me hold what she had been doing against what she might still do, weighed against what she could still help us undo.

The category had not had art in it.

The art was — pleasant. Domestic. The fox had been drawn by someone who had given the fox an opinion about the teapot. Whoever had made these had made them on purpose, with thought, and a small dry sense of humor I had felt in the kitchen yesterday morning and had not connected to a hand on paper.

Cai said softly, from beside me, "Felix."

I did not look at them.

I clicked back to *Special Sketch Archive* before the rest of my categorization could rearrange itself in front of an audience. I did not need Cai watching me update a working framework.

"Order export," I said. My voice came out as it should have. "All records. Buyer names, ship-to addresses, message history, listing dates. Now."

Bianca turned from the counter. Her face was a careful nothing.

"Yeah," she said. "Yeah, I can do that."

She came back to the laptop. The work began.

I kept my attention on the screen and not on the small recalibration occurring under my ribs.

The number on the page was four hundred and twelve. The number in the room was still her.
