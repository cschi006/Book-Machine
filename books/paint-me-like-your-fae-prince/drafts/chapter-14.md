# Chapter 14 — Bianca POV (True Name)

## Scene 1

The court was exactly the kind of room I had spent most of my adult life arranging myself to avoid.

Too many people. Too much attention. Too many ways to be read incorrectly in expensive light.

The doors opened anyway, and I went in without checking where the nearest exit was.

That was how I knew something fundamental had already changed. Not that I wasn't aware of the exits. I was. My brain still mapped thresholds on contact like a smoke detector testing the air. But the math did not come first. The room did.

Stone rose in pale vertical lines to a ceiling I had drawn years before I knew it existed. Lanterns burned behind carved glass. The court itself arranged around a long central floor of dark polished stone, all of it luminous and watchful and controlled enough to make human courtrooms look like reality television. No shouting. No dramatic rustle. Just rows of faces turned toward me with the disciplined stillness of people who knew exactly what a breach could cost and had come to see the source of one standing under their king's eye.

Felix kept pace beside me and did not touch me.

At the far end of the room, Idris sat upright on the throne with no attendant near enough to steady him.

Vael stood below the dais with a slate in one hand.

Cai was present too, off to the side with the household witnesses rather than centered as proof of anything. Their glamour sat easy on them now, no visible drag at the mouth, no half-second lag around the eyes. They caught my eye once and dipped their chin, small and steady.

I stopped where Vael indicated, and Felix stopped beside me.

Vael waited until the room had fully settled, then said, "Bianca Lawson of the mortal realm is present to answer the record of the breach, its concealment, and its repair."

Her tone was formal enough to frost glass. It was also, unmistakably, a tone used for a person and not an object.

Idris looked at me directly. "You will answer plainly."

"Yes," I said.

My voice sounded normal. That felt suspicious, frankly.

Vael glanced once at her slate. "State how the breach began."

So I did.

Not dramatically. Not like I was pitching a tragic miniseries. Just the thing itself.

"It started when I was thirteen. In the space between sleeping and waking. I would find finished drawings I didn't remember making. Faces. Names. Places. I thought it was imagination or some kind of sleep issue I didn't know how to explain. I kept it private. Later I turned some of the drawings into a shop and sold originals without knowing what they were."

A controlled shift moved through the room at that last part. Not surprise. They all knew that already. More like the court acknowledging the point where ignorance stopped being background and became damage with postage.

Vael said, "Did you know the names were real when you sold them."

"No."

"Did you sell them anyway."

"Yes."

"Did you conceal the sales after your arrival in Loren."

"Yes."

Nothing in the room warmed or cooled. The questions were clean instruments. I let them do their job.

Vael looked up from the slate. "Why."

The honest answer was *because I had looked at the scale of it and my nervous system had gone for the oldest available survival strategy, which was conceal, delay, try to make the truth smaller by not speaking it out loud.* That was true and also too internal to be useful here.

"Because once I understood what the originals were doing, saying how many I had sent out felt like putting a second weapon in the room. I was wrong. It made recovery slower."

Vael made a notation.

The room stayed very still.

She asked, "Did any active record remain outside your possession once the recovery effort concluded."

"No."

"Did you retain any name, portrait, or leverage for personal use."

"No."

That one moved somewhere through the court differently. Not because it made me good. Because it made me legible inside their system.

Vael's eyes lifted to Felix. "My prince. Confirm."

"Confirmed," he said.

Only that. No softening edges around me. No defense. No reaching in to improve the record. The restraint of it pressed at my ribs in a way I was trying not to examine while standing in public.

Vael returned her attention to me. "When given opportunity to surrender the records and withdraw from active recovery, did you do so."

"No."

"Why not."

This one I could answer without translation.

"Because the harm didn't stop being mine because I hadn't meant it. And because leaving would not have made anyone less damaged."

I heard the words hit the stone between us and keep going.

No one interrupted. No one approved. The room simply took it.

Vael moved through the remaining documentary record, and I answered plainly.

She did not ask about the basement in any intimate way. She did not ask what it felt like to be strapped to a chair with a pen forced into my hand. She did not need to. This was not that kind of room. I was grateful for it with the kind of intensity I did not let show on my face.

When the questions ended, silence settled for a beat.

Idris spoke into it.

"You caused grave harm through ignorance," he said, and there was no padding around the sentence. "You deepened that harm through concealment once knowledge was given to you. Both are true."

The room held.

He continued, "It is also true that you surrendered every record in your possession, submitted to witness, and remained through the dismantling of the breach when departure was available to you. Repair does not erase harm. Harm does not erase repair. This court will record both."

Something in my body that had been braced for either obliteration or pardon — and trusted neither — went abruptly still.

Vael stepped forward one measured pace. "For the record, Bianca Lawson surrendered all available buyer data, archived images, correspondence, and shipping records. She withheld no active leverage after disclosure. She refused to use names, portraits, or court instability for protection or private gain. She remained present through retrieval, identification, and reckoning."

Her gaze met mine. Cool. Precise. Entirely herself.

"The court's assessment is revised accordingly."

Not forgiven. Not embraced. Not turned into some weird magical mascot of accountability. Recorded accurately.

Idris inclined his head once. "Entered."

Stylus moved across slate. No applause. No gasp. Just the administrative noise of a kingdom deciding what had happened to it and writing it down correctly.

Idris looked at me again, and this time I could see the king without the injury over him.

"You will not be required to remain in Loren against your will. Nor will the record be altered for your comfort. The matter stands as spoken."

"Yes, Majesty," I said.

It came out rougher than the earlier answers. I let it.

His attention shifted to Felix for only a second, but it was enough. Some line I had been watching since I arrived here — the one stretched between father, son, title, and vacancy — went slack in full view of the room. Not broken. Released.

Idris rose.

That was all it took. The court rose with him, movement carrying down the chamber in a single clean wave. No one hesitated. No one glanced toward Felix to see whether he would take the center instead. Idris turned and the room turned with him.

King, then. Clearly.

## Scene 2

Felix took me to a small room off the western gallery that I had never drawn and probably would not have chosen to enter on my own. That helped. It was quiet without being sealed. One wall held high windows turned dark with evening. A single lamp burned on a narrow table. The door stayed unlatched behind us.

He did not stand in front of it.

I noticed that first. The empty space between the door and him. The clear path out if I wanted it.

I leaned one shoulder against the stone beside the window and discovered belatedly that the court had used up more of me than I had allowed for. My wrists ached under their clean wrapping. Sleep deprivation had turned my bones into something slightly hollow. Without the structure of answering questions, I had no idea where to put my hands.

Felix seemed to know better than to comment on any of that.

For a moment he said nothing. The silence between us was different now — less like interruption, more like the absence left after machinery stopped.

"My father will sit petitions tomorrow," he said at last. "And the council after that. He would not schedule both if he meant to withdraw again."

"You sound almost annoyed by the competence."

"I am adjusting."

That nearly made me smile, which was unfair to both of us given the subject matter.

He took a step nearer and let his hands stay at his sides.

"For fifteen years," he said, "I have organized my life around the assumption that duty would always answer before I did. It has been useful. It has also been — structural."

There were about ten different dry things I could have said to that, all of them defense disguised as wit. They passed through and kept going.

"And now."

"Now it no longer answers first."

His voice had none of the court in it. No public edge. No careful strategic blankness. Just precision stripped down to the part that could survive without rank doing any of the work.

He looked at me the way he had in the work chamber sometimes, when I came back from the channel wrong around the edges and he was making sure I was fully present before saying anything important.

"There is something I will not place in the hands of anyone who does not understand the cost of holding it. You do."

The room narrowed around the sentence.

He did not make me ask.

He said his true name once.

It was shorter than I expected and older than any language I knew how to sort things into. Not ornate. Not theatrical. Exact. It landed in me like a weight set carefully into open palms.

*Felyr.*

That was it. No echo. No visible magic rippling through the room. Nothing except the fact of his face in front of me and the knowledge itself, impossible and irreversible.

I did not repeat it.

My mouth felt too blunt for it anyway. Some things were not improved by being handled twice.

He watched me receive it. Not testing. Waiting.

I closed my fingers, uselessly, around empty air at my sides and said, "I have it."

Something changed in his expression then. Not softened, exactly. More like a piece locked into place behind the public architecture and made the whole thing read differently.

"Yes," he said.

The lamp gave off a low, warm circle of light. Outside the windows, Loren had gone to glass-dark night. Somewhere farther down the gallery, a door opened and shut with the muffled courtesy of people who knew this wing was occupied and were wise enough not to care why.

Felix said, "If you had used what you held to control me, I would have deserved my own stupidity."

"That's romantic."

"It is honest."

"It can be both."

The corner of his mouth shifted. Barely.

Then he said, because apparently we were doing irreversible honesty in sequence now, "If you want me, I will come with you to the mortal world."

I did not move.

"I want the life that is yours," he said. "Not for visits. Not until the next crisis. For the span of your life." His gaze stayed on mine. "I can take a glamour that ages as you age. Loren will endure my absence. When your life is over, I will return."

The words entered the room with all the force of a blow delivered very carefully.

There it was. The real scale of him. Not flowers. Not vows. Not fantasy-pageant declaration. A finite life, chosen on purpose. The cost named cleanly enough that I could not pretend not to understand it.

My throat worked once before sound came.

"You've already decided."

His eyes held mine. "Yes."

Not apologetic. Not presumptuous. Just true.

"And you're telling me now because —"

"Because I would rather be refused honestly than accepted through momentum. And because I am tired of translating what I mean into forms that can be defended later."

Well. That was catastrophically attractive.

I looked at the open door behind him, then back at his face. It was one thing to be wanted inside catastrophe. It was another to be offered the whole long, ordinary stretch of a life.

Public judgment had been easier.

I took the step between us.

"I want you. And if you're offering the whole actual difficult version, then yes. Come with me."

For a beat neither of us moved.

Then his hand came up, slow enough to interrupt if I wanted, and touched the side of my face. Not command. Not management. Just contact, careful around the parts of me still held together by effort and bandages and low blood sugar and stubbornness.

I leaned into it before I could overthink the fact that I was doing that.

His forehead rested briefly against mine. The position was quiet enough to be more dangerous than a dramatic kiss would have been. Then he kissed me, and there was nothing strategic left in either of us to hide behind.

It was not frantic. Nothing in me had room for frantic. It was slower than that and far less survivable.

His other hand settled at my waist. He waited until I shifted into him before letting his fingers spread along the curve of my back, the touch deliberate, asking. I answered by closing the last small distance between us — and the *answer,* like everything else with him, was a thing my body did before my brain caught up to the decision.

The kiss changed.

Not faster. Lower. He kissed the corner of my mouth, then the line of my jaw, and the small hesitation between each was the deliberate room he was leaving for me to redirect him. I did not redirect him. I tilted my chin so he could find the place beneath my ear, and the sound that came out of me was the small embarrassed half-laugh of a person whose body had registered something her words had not yet caught up to.

"Felix —"

"Yes."

"Yes is not the question."

"It is the answer to whichever one you are about to ask."

That nearly broke me.

We did not make it across the small room. We made it to the chair beside the lamp. He sat. He brought me with him, and I ended up in his lap with one knee on either side of him and his hands at the line of my hips and the lamp's warm circle of light catching the edge of his cuff and the bandage at my wrist and the careful slow way he was now looking at me.

"Wrists," he said, low.

"Sore. Not from this."

"I know."

He turned my hand over and pressed his mouth to the inside of one wrist, above the bandage, with the small specific reverence of a man putting a thing on the record. The gesture went somewhere very particular in my chest. He did the other one. Then he looked up.

"This is not the whole conversation," he said. "There is more. There is going home. There is your bed instead of a Loren chair. There is the rest of your life, which is the actual offer."

"I know."

"I am only — beginning it."

"I know."

I leaned down. The kiss this time was the one I had wanted to give him before the court room. Slower. Mine. My hand against the side of his neck, then under his collar, then flat at the warm skin between his shoulder blades where the line of his shirt eased open. He inhaled against my mouth in the small involuntary way I had not yet seen from him, and the sound of it landed in me as the clean specific evidence I had been waiting two weeks for: *he was not, in fact, perfectly assembled when no one was watching.*

He let his head tip back against the chair so I could find more of him.

I did.

What we did in that small western-gallery room was not the whole offer either. It was a beginning. The lamp burned through it. The door stayed unlatched. Somewhere down the gallery the muffled courtesies of fae who knew when to leave a wing alone continued. He undid the small braid Mara had put in my hair this morning and ran his hand through it slowly enough that the gesture itself was an apology for every other thing in the last week I had not been able to feel without flinching. I undid the top three buttons of his shirt because his hands were busy. He laughed once, low — the actual laugh, the one I had only heard him use with Cai — and the sound of it almost undid me on its own.

When we finally separated enough to speak, it was only because the lamp had burned down by half and my body had remembered it was still recovering from a basement.

"Home," he said.

"Home."

"Tomorrow."

"Tomorrow."

I leaned my forehead against his and closed my eyes.
