# Chapter 8 — Felix POV (Mortal Logistics)

## Scene 1

The recovery campaign acquired the texture of mortal labor by the end of the second morning.

We did three suburban houses before noon. Bianca handled doors. Cai verified addresses against the listing photos. I burned originals in service alleys and parking-deck stairwells under wards tight enough to keep the ash from staining concrete. The protocol had stabilized into something I would not have called efficient by court standards but which moved at the pace mortal logistics permitted, and the mortal pace was, for once, in our favor — slower than my uncle was expecting, faster than the buyers could notice a pattern in their walls.

Bianca did not stop. That was the part I had not predicted. The customer-service mask she had deployed yesterday under threat had become the operational register she now wore by default. *Hi, sorry to bother you, materials issue, won't take long.* Three buyers. Three doors. Three pieces destroyed by lunch. She was working with the small hard precision of a person who had decided, on the record yesterday evening, that this was her work now, and the decision had survived the night.

She did not look like she had slept either. None of us had.

The fourth stop was an antique market in an old warehouse district that Cai had flagged as a problem before we arrived.

"Stall numbers were redone in May," Bianca said, looking up at the building from the parking lot. "The piece is here but the booth has moved."

"Map?"

"They updated the website map. I have it. Booth 47."

The market noise was audible from the lot. Carts. Vendors. The low public chatter of people browsing without supervision. Cai's mouth tightened by a fraction. I did not need a steward to translate the body-state of a fae whose magic was running thinner than the day asked for.

Bianca registered it before I did. "I can do this one fast."

"Define fast."

"Six minutes. Three at the booth, three to confirm the swap. I know the booth attendant — she was at a small show I did in February."

I did not argue. There were arguments to make. None of them were more useful than the fact that the buyer of the piece in booth 47 was a household-cluster name and the longer the original sat under fluorescent strips in an antique market hall the more dangerous the air around it became.

We went in.

The market made the kind of noise mortal commerce made when it was not being supervised. Carts. Vendors. A jam-packed aisle where a glass-fronted booth held the piece we had come for. The buyer was not present; the attendant was. Bianca handled her. Cai held the folio. I held the line between us and a couple drifting too near.

Bianca's stillness was a body-state I had been catalogueing for two days now. The careful economy of motion. The clipped answers. The way her attention had begun to move ahead of her, calibrating exits she did not yet need. The customer-service voice came out a fraction higher. The booth attendant nodded along; the booth attendant did not register what was costing the small mortal across the counter to make this transaction look ordinary.

I shortened the conversation. I asked the attendant for a liability form that mortal markets did not require, which produced exactly the bureaucratic confusion I had wanted; the attendant turned to unlock the case rather than continue the small talk; the couple drifting near the booth chose the next aisle when I shifted my weight to occupy the choke point.

Bianca made the swap.

Cai got the original into the folio.

We were back in the parking lot in five minutes and forty seconds.

She did not let her shoulders down until the doors of the car were shut.

I did not point that out. I burned the original in a ward at the lot's edge. Cai's color came back by a fraction. Bianca breathed out long enough to qualify as a recovery.

The route in my head adjusted itself before I named the adjustment. Crowded stops moved to morning. Private residences moved to midday. Any address I had been planning to put Bianca into after three in the afternoon — when fatigue would have eaten the customer-service register and her exit-calculation would have started to fray — moved to the next morning's run. By the time I had a coherent route revision, Cai had noticed.

"You are managing her thresholds."

"I am managing the operation."

"Yes," Cai said, mildly. "Same thing."

I did not answer.

The route revision held. We did two more stops by late afternoon. The originals burned. The mortal world did not notice. Bianca did not collapse, did not retreat, did not become brittle. We drove back to her townhouse at a speed mortal traffic permitted, and the small clean fact that I had built her thresholds into the operation as a procedural variable sat in my chest with the kind of weight I had stopped pretending was procedural twelve hours ago.

## Scene 2

By evening her dining table had become a working surface.

Laptop. Recovery ledger. The stack of finished replacements waiting for the morning's run. A second drive plugged into the back of the laptop in case the first failed. Bianca was at the table with the lamp from her hall pulled over to extend her working light, redrawing the next morning's lineup from listing photos with the steady precision I had been watching her produce all afternoon.

Cai had been delivered to the smaller of the two couches with a blanket, which they had accepted without their usual objection. That was either improvement or surrender. I did not yet have data to tell which.

I sat opposite Bianca with the order export open and Vael's annotations laid alongside it.

We worked in something close to silence for an hour. Then Mara texted Bianca, who answered her with one line and then turned the phone face down and kept drawing. Then Mara texted again. Then Bianca turned the phone all the way off and apologized to the room.

"She is fine," Bianca said. "She is just on day three of being told by me that nothing is wrong."

"Eventually she will arrive at your door."

"Eventually she will."

"With what."

"Probably brunch. Maybe a gun."

"I have not yet decided which I would prefer."

That almost got a smile out of her.

I did not push the Mara question. The Mara question would resolve itself on her terms; I did not improve any of the timing variables I had by trying to manage her oldest friendship from across a table.

Cai shifted on the couch. "Felix."

"Mm."

"Look at the message thread for the P.O. box buyer."

I looked.

The most recent message in the buyer's archive was from a year ago. The thread had a clean institutional silence stretched across the intervening months — no inquiries, no purchases requested by name, no contact at all that had originated from the buyer's side.

That had been true at sundown. It was no longer true at 7:42 p.m.

A new message had landed in the thread fourteen minutes ago.

*Hi. Any chance special sketches are available again? — D.*

Bianca went very still.

Cai said quietly, "He has been watching the shop."

"Yes," I said.

He had been watching for the orders Bianca had not been placing because the shop had not been live to him since the chamber two nights ago. He had given us one quiet week. He had let the silence pile up. He had decided — for reasons mortal anonymity did not require him to share — that the silence was data, and that the data needed adjusting.

Bianca did not move for a long beat.

When she did, it was to set her pen down very carefully.

"I haven't listed in nine days. That's longer than I've ever gone since the shop opened. He knows my rhythm."

"He knows your rhythm."

"He wants me reachable."

"Yes."

She put both hands flat against the table on either side of the keyboard. The cinch I had watched go through her chest last night when she made her declaration was back. Cai had come to a sitting position on the couch with the careful slow rise of a person who had felt the magic of a small static shift in the room and had been pulled out of rest by it.

I closed the export.

The next move would be the one that determined the shape of the rest of the campaign.

## Scene 3

"We answer," I said.

Bianca's gaze lifted to mine. "I answer."

"You answer."

"From the shop account. With a rush-order listing. Sized like a typical sketch. Express shipping. Updated address line — let him pick a P.O. box, let him show us which one is current."

"You do not give him your face. You do not give him your hand. You give him a piece of conscious art with no name on it, packaged the way you have packaged him before."

"Yes."

"And we follow it to the box."

Cai sat forward. "And then."

"Then we identify the buyer."

Bianca took her phone out. She turned it on. She watched the small parade of notifications climb. She ignored every one of them.

She opened the shop backend on the laptop.

The cursor blinked in the message field.

She did not perform the message for me. She did not look at me to check the register before she wrote it. She wrote with the small efficient precision of a working artist who had been answering buyers like this for four years — *Hi! Yes, I can open one rush special sketch slot tonight if you'd like. Same size and finish as previous pieces, express shipping only. If that works for you, I'll send over a reserved listing.*

She read it once.

She hit send.

The reply landed in seventy-three seconds.

*Perfect. Send the listing. Express is fine. Use updated address at checkout.*

The buyer was at his keyboard. The buyer was waiting. The buyer had been waiting, possibly, all evening. The mortal calm of the response was the mortal calm of a man whose patience for indirection had just received its first signal in nine days that the indirection was about to end.

Cai said, "He is fast."

"Yes."

Bianca did not look up. She was already drafting the listing.

The decoy would be a conscious sketch — Bianca's hand, awake, without the channel, without a name at the bottom. It would not weaken any fae. It would not hand the buyer leverage. It would, however, give him a tracking number we could follow to the P.O. box he was currently using.

I closed the order export. I stood. I went to the kitchen for the warded tin I kept in a small inside pocket of my coat — the one we used to burn originals in transit when a service-alley ward was inconvenient — and I set it on the table beside the laptop.

"For the packaging," I said. "When you ship it tomorrow, the tin goes in the mailer. Glamour-anchored to the box. If he tries to read the magic on the piece, the tin gives him nothing to read. If he handles it physically, the tin reads as ordinary archival packaging."

"Got it."

"Tomorrow he picks it up. Tomorrow we have a ZIP code."

"Tomorrow," Bianca said, "we move."

She did not finish the listing immediately. She closed the laptop and sat for a beat with her hands flat on the table and her shoulders set, looking at the small warded tin between us as if it were a door rather than an object.

"Felix."

"Yes."

"He's going to make a mistake. They always do. The patient ones especially."

"Yes."

"And when he does, I want to be the one who recognizes it."

"You will be."

She did not say *I'm scared.* She did not need to. Her vault was not the kind that put fear into a sentence; her vault put fear into the precision of how she sealed a tin. She picked up the warded tin, weighed it in her hand once, and set it carefully into the bag with the next morning's replacements.

The bait was set.

The next move was not ours.
