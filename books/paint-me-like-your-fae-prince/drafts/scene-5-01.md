# Chapter 5 — Scene 1
**POV:** Bianca · **WC target:** 1,400 ±200 · **Tension:** 6→8 · **Seeds:** woodsmoke · catafalque · tendon · temple · bobbin

---

By the second house, I was good at it.

That was the problem.

The first house had taken forty-five minutes and a low simmer of catastrophe under my ribs the entire time. The second house took twenty. The third was over before the buyer had finished apologizing for the state of her hallway. By the fourth, I had a customer-service voice on a hair-trigger and a script that contained the exact phrase *we caught a batch of bad backing board last spring* and a small bobbin of archival linen tape in my bag because at some point I had decided that authenticity props would help and they actually did.

The car smelled faintly of woodsmoke from someone burning leaves three streets over. Felix had been quiet between stops in the way he was quiet when he had decided not to talk about something specific. Cai was in the back seat with their legs crossed under them, conserving motion. They had improved over the morning — measurably, in a way I had now seen Felix register without comment — and the improvement had a ceiling we were not going to reach today.

We did the fifth house. We did the sixth.

The seventh was a small ranch on a cul-de-sac with a wreath on the door that announced it was *always wine o'clock somewhere.*

There was no car in the driveway.

The buyer had answered Bianca-the-Artist's email at 7:14 that morning with *of course, come whenever, I work from home today, just buzz the bell.* Bianca-the-Artist had buzzed the bell at 11:08. There had been no answer. Bianca-the-Artist had checked her email. The buyer had not written back since 9:42. Bianca-the-Artist had texted: *running a little behind, hope still ok.* No reply.

Felix had said, "Skip it."

I had said, "We know where the frame is. It's in the front window. If we skip it, we have to drive twenty minutes back across town later."

He had said, "Skip it."

I had said, "Or we get out, ring once, and if she doesn't answer in thirty seconds we go in through the unlocked side gate because she has not changed the gate code in any of the four pieces of correspondence we have from her and the gate code is visible in the listing photo of the lawn art she also bought from me in 2023."

Felix had cut the engine without saying anything else.

Cai had said, "I love when you brief Felix."

The gate code was 1208. Wedding anniversary, by the engraved bench in the corner. The side gate opened on a small breezeway. The kitchen door was unlocked because the buyer had a cat that liked to sit on the back step. The frame was twelve feet from the kitchen door, on the living-room wall, exactly where the review photo had placed it.

I had the backing off in forty seconds. The replacement was halfway into the new mat board when the garage door, somewhere deeper in the house, started grinding upward.

My tendons locked first.

Then my hands, in the perfect bad-timing way the body had of pretending I was still in college trying not to drop a beaker. Felix had the replacement in one hand and his glamour spinning into it before my brain caught up with what the sound was. Cai had moved.

The buyer came in through the laundry room with two grocery bags and stopped.

I was standing in her living room with her frame open on her console table.

For one beat my body did the freeze and my brain did the lap of the room I had walked into. *Bianca-the-Artist, customer-service voice, materials issue, sent the email, here for the fix.* My phone was in my hand before I had decided to want it.

"Oh hi! Sorry, I — I sent an email this morning and then a text and I was about to leave you a note." My voice was higher than it should have been and I let it stay there because higher was friendlier. "I'm Bianca, from the shop? You ordered a piece in March of last year. There was an issue with a batch of backing board I shipped with last spring. I emailed you this morning."

I held the screen out toward her. The email was right there. I had sent it at 7:14 a.m.

She looked at me, the frame, Felix, Cai, the email, the frame again.

"Oh," she said. "I forgot. I forgot. I'm so sorry, I had a temple pounding all morning, I should have — *you came into my house.*"

"Yes," I said. "And I shouldn't have. That's on me. I should have waited. But the materials degrade if they sit against the paper long enough and I wanted to catch it before it stained, and I had your email saying come whenever, and your gate code was in our shipping history."

I held up the old backing board. I bent the corner. The bad batch had, mercifully, actually been bad — the layer of acid-buffered foam had separated from the boardstock in a way that looked like real warping. I had not made up the backing issue. I had only made up the urgency.

"Look," I said, and bent the corner again. "This is why."

Her eyes went from the corner to the frame on the wall to me. The frame on the console table — replacement in, original in Cai's folio, glamour holding — sat innocent and finished.

She let out a breath that was half adrenaline and half relief.

"Oh god, okay. Okay. *Don't do that again.*"

"I won't."

"Like — call. Knock. Don't let yourself in."

"You're absolutely right. I'm so sorry."

She rehung the frame herself. She told me she loved the piece. She said the new mounting actually felt sturdier — Felix's glamour was doing a small structural-confidence trick I would not have asked for and was not going to refuse — and she walked us back through the kitchen and let us out the front door instead of the side, which was the polite mortal version of asking us to please for the love of god leave.

In the car, neither of us spoke for half a minute.

Cai broke it.

"That was *horrible.*"

"It worked."

"Those two facts are not mutually exclusive."

Felix did not turn the engine on. He looked at me sideways without making a thing of it. My hands were doing the small post-fight unspooling they did when adrenaline emptied out of them — not shaking, exactly, just the slow give of small tendons that had been holding too much.

"That was very fast," he said.

"I do customer service emails for a living."

"You do not."

"I do enough of them."

He almost said something. He did not.

He started the car.

"One more stop today," he said. "Then we are done."

I did not argue. I did not have the bandwidth to argue, and the relief of being told I could be done with public lying for the day registered in my chest like a small clean note.

We drove.
