# Chapter 7 — Scene 2
**POV:** Bianca · **WC target:** 1,300 ±200 · **Tension:** 7→7

---

Felix was in the kitchen on the Loren side of the portal.

That was new. Yesterday and the day before, the portal had opened directly into the strategy chamber or my own studio. This time the seam let me into a small kitchen with a stone hearth, a long pine table, and a single window facing what looked like an interior courtyard. There was a kettle on a low fire. There was a stack of paper on the table. There was Felix, in a dark shirt without the coat he had worn on my porch, with the sleeves rolled up to his forearms in a way that did not match any prior version of him I had filed.

He did not greet me. He looked at the bag in my hand and his face did the small thing it did when he had decided not to react until I had given him a sentence to react to.

I set the bag on the table.

"I brought the full export."

"Yes."

"Three drives. Different formats."

"Yes."

"And the next local replacement, ready for your glamour."

"Yes."

He did not ask the question I knew he was not going to ask. The hour had been the choice. I had walked through the portal. He was not going to require me to perform the rest of the sentence for him. The mercy of that registered somewhere I did not have time to inspect.

"Where's Cai," I said.

"Two rooms over. They drank actual broth this morning. I am told this is a measurable improvement."

"It is."

He poured tea into a small ceramic cup that did not match the kitchen. He handed it to me. The handle of the cup was warm. I had not asked for it. He had remembered that I drank tea and not coffee in this realm because mortal coffee was not a thing Loren stocked, and the small care of having a working cup ready for me ran into the cinch in my chest that had not let go since the chamber and loosened one wrap.

I drank the tea.

"Records," he said.

I unpacked the drives. He had a Loren equivalent of a laptop on the table — a flat panel of polished stone that lit when he laid his palm on it and produced, when prompted, a working surface readable to my eyes. He had run the same trick on my mortal laptop yesterday afternoon. The technology was not the point. The point was that he had set up a workspace that was neither my dining room nor the strategy chamber. A workspace I could exist in without being read by Vael's wards or framed by my own studio.

He had chosen it on purpose.

I did not point that out.

I plugged in the first drive.

We worked through the export for ninety minutes. Felix did most of the questioning; I did most of the answering. He asked about repeat customers, regional clusters, message tone, packaging changes over time. He asked which buyers had been collectors and which had been gift-buyers and which had been corporate accounts — there were not many, but there were three. He asked which buyers I would have flagged as eccentric.

I gave him answers I had not known I had until he asked.

The P.O. box buyer's account was at the center of the working surface. Felix had cross-referenced the dates with Vael's exile-vote roster overnight, and the names paired in the same neat columns Vael had shown me yesterday. Seventeen targeted purchases. Five household-officer purchases. Three pieces that did not fit either pattern — small enough to be either error or noise.

"What are those three," I asked.

"Court figures who did not vote in either direction. Neutrals. Possibly insurance against the council reshuffling. Possibly trophies."

"Possibly?"

"My uncle is not a man who buys without reason. The three are reason I do not yet have."

"Okay."

He flipped to the listing photos. He pulled up the buyer's full purchase history side by side with the archived photos of every piece. We sat with that for a long while. The faces on the screen were the architecture of a war I had been quietly building for eighteen months without knowing I was the construction crew.

Cai came in halfway through. They sat. They drank tea. They contributed three corrections — *that one is not Sariel, that is Sariel's brother* — that revised one of Vael's columns.

The corrections mattered. Three names off the war list. Three names not actually targeted. The architecture of my uncle's intent contracted by the smallest measurable degree. I let myself register that without performing on it.

When we had a clean list — buyers, addresses, named fae, the operational order of recovery — Felix closed the working surface.

"We will need every name traced," he said. "And the P.O. box buyer drawn out of postal anonymity. We have the count. We do not yet have his face."

"His face is what I do," I said.

"The half-sleep channel does not work on demand."

"Maybe not. But the man has bought eighteen months of my work. He has handled my packaging. He has written messages in my shop. He has a pattern."

"You will not draw him in his sleep for me, Bianca. You will draw him because we will find another way to bring him into the same room as you."

"Bait."

"Yes."

"Okay."

He waited. He had not asked me to say it. He had not engineered the conversation toward the sentence. He had built a kitchen, given me tea, run the records, taken Cai's corrections, planned the next operational phase — and let me move under my own momentum until I came to the version of myself that was going to say it.

Cai's mouth did the small thing.

"I need to say it in front of Vael," I said. "Don't I."

"You do not have to."

"For the record."

"For the record."

"Then let's get her."

He sent a thin slip of silvered paper through the air the way Vael had sent one yesterday. It folded itself into a directional shape and went out of the kitchen at the speed of a thrown stone.

We did not speak while we waited.

The cinch in my chest had loosened another wrap. The tea was cooler. The list of recovered originals was nine items long; the list of remaining originals was four hundred and three; the list of names I would have to learn before this was over included my own.

When Vael arrived, she did not bring papers. She had been called for the record, not for the work.

"My prince."

"Vael."

"Bianca Lawson."

"Vael."

She did not sit. Felix did not invite her to.

I did not perform on the moment. I waited until Cai was upright and present, until Felix had taken the position at the end of the table closest to the door — the one he took when he wanted Vael to see him as institutional, not personal — and until Vael had become the witness she was here to be.

Then I said it.
