# Chapter 3 — Scene 3
**POV:** Bianca
**Word count target:** 1,300 (±200)
**Entry tension:** 8 · **Exit tension:** 9 — chapter close on the Etsy hook
**Seeds:** spigot · instep · ossuary · cotter pin · brad

The portal opened into my kitchen and my house did not feel like mine.

That was the first wrong thing. The second was the smell — coffee left on the counter from before any of this had started, faintly burnt now from sitting on the warmer too long. It was the smell of a morning that had belonged to a different person. The light through the front window was the same as I had left it. The mail on the entry table was where I had set it three days ago. The hockey stick was still on the floor where it had hit the boards when I went loose-handed on the porch.

Nothing had changed.

Everything had been reassigned.

The second my phone found mortal service, it lost its mind.

Vibrations stacked through my pocket like a small panic event. Texts. Work email. Slack. A Ring alert from twenty minutes ago I had not registered. Three missed calls from Mara, who at this point had moved through a recognizable arc — *u up?* → *bianca answer me* → *answer me or im calling the cops.*

I did not answer any of it. I pulled the phone out and watched the banners stack across the lock screen while Felix looked through the front window and Cai leaned a shoulder against the wall in the careful stillness I was starting to recognize as cost.

Outside, the townhouse row sat in its usual suburban compliance. Small porches. Mulched beds. A recycling bin somebody had not yet rolled back in.

Mr. Terry's truck was in his driveway.

It was the most ordinary thing in my field of vision and it landed in my chest like a brad driven by a nail gun. Not because of the truck. Because of how absolutely normal it was. Mr. Terry was at home. Mr. Terry had probably noticed the lack of a delivery from me this morning. Mr. Terry might come over later with a misdelivered package and mild comment about the weather.

I had no idea how to be next to Mr. Terry on a sidewalk anymore.

That was not a thing I had budgeted for in this disaster.

I filed it under *also think about later* and put the phone away.

"Studio," Felix said.

It was not phrased as a request. I had stopped expecting his sentences to bend toward request structure.

I went up first because the alternative was watching Cai try not to limp on the stairs. My own instep ached from the corridor — stone hits the body differently than carpet. The hallway upstairs smelled of paper and dried ink and the dust-warm electronics of my printer, and that was the smell of a place I had built on purpose, and it should have helped. It mostly did not.

The studio looked the same and it did not.

Felix's containment marks were still there — pale lines at the drawers, at the cabinet handles, at the seams of the archive boxes. In the daylight I had not seen them in, they were almost not there. The lamp over my worktable was still angled where I had left it. The half-sleep portrait of Cai was not on the desk anymore; it was ash in a bronze dish in a stone chamber in a kingdom I had been drawing for sixteen years.

The packing bench was the part that hit hardest.

Padded mailers. The branded sticker pile. The label printer. The tape gun. The little ceramic dish where I dropped used blades. The shipping scale, half-buried under thank-you cards with my shop handle printed across them in the font I had once wasted an entire afternoon choosing. There were two mailers I had sealed yesterday — Wednesday — and not yet dropped at the annex. There was an open tote on the floor with five orders ready to ship. There was a printout of a packing slip with a small brass cotter-pin clip on it because I had been about to attach a thank-you card and had gotten distracted by the dinner I had not made.

I had a small business.

I had a small business that had been doing business.

The contents of the studio that Felix could see were not the contents of my business. The contents of my business were a four-year mailing list and an order history and a fulfillment cadence and a couple of glass-fronted shelves at consignment shops that took two of my originals each. The contents of my business were not in this house. The contents of my business were somewhere between here and the recipient's mailbox, or in the recipient's hallway, or above the recipient's couch in a frame they had bought at HomeGoods, or carefully wrapped in tissue paper in a closet because the recipient was waiting for the right occasion to give it as a gift.

The lie I had told in the chamber was no longer a lie I could keep.

Not because of guilt.

Because of math.

The math was happening in my chest where the breath should be.

Felix opened the nearest flat file. He did it the way he had done it yesterday morning — efficient, no theatrical buildup, the drawer just sliding out of its track with a small grit of metal. He did not look at me while he worked. He laid one active piece on the table, then another. Cai stayed at the threshold because crossing the doorline into the studio still cost them more than they wanted me to see.

Felix said, without turning, "We will catalogue what is here first."

"Okay."

"You will tell me what is missing from this catalogue."

The word *missing* hit a small clean spot under my ribs. He had not asked. He had read the air over the dish in the chamber, or read my hand on the door, or read the way I had said *I can show you what I have at home,* and he had filed the gap.

"Okay," I said.

I had not yet figured out how to say the actual sentence.

That was when the chime came.

It was bright. Distinct. Etsy's brand sound — a thing I had once cared about being a recognizable identity at the bottom of my brain alongside Slack's knock and the WhatsApp double-tone. New order.

My hand stopped on the edge of the flat file.

The phone was in my hand before I had thought about wanting it there. Orange and white lit the screen.

*New order — congratulations!* the app said, in the cheerful tone of a piece of software that had no idea what it was selling.

Felix went very still beside me.

He did not need me to translate. He had seen what the studio could do. He understood what the studio could do at scale only as a number, and he was about to understand it as an event.

I looked at him.

"That's not in this room," I said.

His face did not change.

"How many," he said.

I did not have a count yet. I had a shape. The shape was four years of one-of-a-kind ink portraits, individually photographed and listed and priced and sold and mailed to addresses across the country and a few internationally, with a packaging quality I had refined over four busy seasons and a tracking number for every package that had left this house.

It was, by any honest count, a lot.

The phone chimed again.

Another order.

Cai, at the doorway, said quietly, "Felix."

Felix did not look at them. He looked at me. The cold authority I had clocked on my porch yesterday was operative again — not performance, not management, just the actual structure of him in the actual position he was in — and he asked the question I had been preparing the wrong answer for since the chamber.

"How many."

The lie I had carried in the chamber was already in the past tense. The new sentence had to belong to a person who could still survive this room.

"Hundreds," I said.
