# Chapter 5 — Scene 3
**POV:** Bianca · **WC target:** 1,400 ±200 · **Tension:** 6→9 · **Seeds:** charge · spindrift · corbel · rivet · cordite

---

The strategy chamber was different at night.

Or it was different at this time of day in Loren, which I had decided was night based on the way the high windows had gone from filtered-water-bright to filtered-water-deep. Same chamber. Same long stone table. Same sideboard. The corbels that supported the upper gallery cast small triangular shadows down the wall now, geometry I had not seen in here at morning hour. Vael was already in it. So was Cai. So was a stack of paper at Vael's elbow that had not been there this morning.

Felix and I came in together.

We were not making a thing of that. We had walked from the chamber where I had given them my full order export — the second time today and ostensibly the last — and the walk had taken us past three guards and one corridor I had not drawn before and an alcove that smelled faintly of cordite, or what mortal training had taught me to identify as cordite, and that I now suspected might be a normal Loren smell associated with whatever Loren used in place of locks.

Felix had not commented on my map-making. He had taken me through the route as if I had every reason to know it.

Vael did not look up when we entered. "Sit."

I sat.

Felix did not. Cai had not been sitting either; they were at the side of the table with the careful upright posture of a person who had decided that standing was the right thing to do for this conversation. There were four pieces of paper laid in clean parallel lines on the dark stone. There was a fifth, separate, set apart.

Vael moved her hand over the four.

"Cluster one," she said. "Councillor Meret. Master of gates, Ilyn. Treaty voice, Sariel. Keeper of the western wards, Teren. Auditor of river tolls, Desmin."

The names meant nothing to me separately. Together they leaned toward something.

"Cluster two," Vael said. She turned a sheet so I could see it. "Three more. The chamberlain. One physician. The keeper assigned to King Idris's private rooms."

That one I caught.

Idris. The king.

The Idris-resembling portrait on my upstairs landing. The framed piece I had told Felix yesterday morning was *normal.* The piece Felix had glanced at once on his way to the studio and not commented on.

The cinder caught again under my ribs and did not go cold.

"Vael," Felix said.

"My prince."

"Show her the third sheet."

Vael turned the third sheet toward me. It was a court record — an actual document, with seal marks at the top in colors I had no vocabulary for. The heading was in a script I could not read until Vael moved it under her finger and the words rearranged themselves into something my eyes accepted.

*Petition of Exile. Vote tally. Year of the king's seventh decline.*

"Read the names."

I did.

The names on the petition of exile matched the names on the P.O. box buyer's list. Every name in the first cluster. *Every* name. The exact list. Eighteen months of patient retail acquisition by an anonymous buyer with rotating post-office boxes. Not aesthetic. Not random. *Targeted.*

I sat back hard against the chair. The chair did not yield. Mortal furniture compensated for body weight; stone chambers did not. The rivet of pressure I had been carrying for an hour fixed itself somewhere between my shoulder blades and stayed.

"Who is the exiled person."

Cai shifted. Felix did not.

Vael said the name without theater.

"Evander. The king's brother."

Felix's expression did the thing where it did not move and yet rearranged. I watched it without watching it.

"What did he do."

"He killed the queen."

Vael did not soften any of those words. I waited for them to land before I asked the next thing, because I could feel that Felix was the right person to answer it and Vael was not.

Felix did not answer it. Not yet. He put two fingers on the corner of the petition.

"The queen was my mother," he said. "Anwyll. She was mortal."

The cinder went all the way through.

I had not expected this room to be the room that contained that piece of information. I had not expected to find out Felix had a dead mortal mother by way of a court document on a stone table in a kingdom run on names I had been giving away through Etsy for four years.

I looked at the third sheet again. Then the fourth. The fourth — the one set apart — was a single page with a name on it I knew so well I almost laughed.

*Idris. King.*

There was a column of numbers under it. I did not have to read every entry to know what it was. It was the long, patient timeline of every quiet hour the buyer had used to acquire the household officers closest to the king, plus the carefully scattered acquisitions of the people who had voted to put his brother out.

The buyer wanted what he had lost. He wanted Idris exposed. He wanted Felix's people compromised. He wanted *everything.*

I could feel my breath rising up under my sternum and I made it stop there. The system I had spent my whole life pretending was a private quirk had been documented by a person who had used me as a quiet retail channel against the people who had punished him for murdering Felix's mother.

I had drawn every face he had asked for.

I had not known I had been asking.

"You said *targeted,*" I said, slower than I wanted to. "Not opportunistic. Not random. *Targeted.*"

Vael's gaze finally lifted to mine. It was not warm. It was less cold than it had been this morning.

"Yes," she said. "By name."

The chamber held that.

I had been an unwitting weapon for sixteen years and a deliberately steered one for eighteen months. The mortal who had been steering me had a name now. And the name had a face the dossier of my own life had been refusing to assemble all afternoon. The next compulsion I had to fight was the one to start scrolling through my buyer database in my head — to put the spindrift of every order, every package, every casually charming buyer message, against the picture I did not yet have.

I did not have his face yet.

But I knew what I had been part of, and I knew what I was going to have to do about it, and somewhere behind that the part of my brain I had spent years compartmentalizing under *also think about later* opened a new compartment and put *Evander* in it for now.

Felix said, very evenly, "We will find him."

"Yes."

"And we will burn every original he has bought."

"Yes."

Vael said, "Bianca."

I looked at her.

She did not bend her register. She did not soften the geometry of her face.

"This is not your fault," she said.

It was not absolution. It was a procedural statement, made for the record, by a woman who had assessed me as a hazard yesterday and was issuing today's revised assessment with the same evenness she had used for the first one.

"I know," I said.

I did not mean it yet. But I was going to.

I picked the petition of exile up off the table.

Vael did not stop me.

"What do we do tomorrow," I said.
