# Chapter 11 — Bianca POV (In Plain Sight) — DARK MOMENT

## Scene 1

By eleven forty, I had been holding the customer-service voice on for two hours.

That was the part I had budgeted for. The fluorescent lights, the bone-deep noise of three thousand mortal humans browsing tables in a convention hall, the wave of people who saw my prints and wanted to tell me about their childhood pets and their breakup art and the etsy seller they had ordered from in 2019 who had ghosted them mid-commission — I had budgeted for all of it. Mara had built a system. The system was working. The booth was at the end of the row. There was a wall at my back. A stool I could sit on between sales. A back-hall exit Mara had marked as my personal break route. A folder of pre-cut prints I could hand over without doing live commission negotiations. Cai working the aisle as a tester for the air around me. Felix six feet away in the position of someone trying very hard not to register as my bodyguard.

The system was working.

What I had not budgeted for was the way my body would stop being a thing I could distinguish from the noise.

By eleven forty, the difference between Bianca-the-Artist and Bianca-not-on-edge had collapsed into a single careful breath cycle I was running in the back of my throat. I was selling. I was smiling. I was answering questions about my creative process with the rehearsed line Mara had vetted in the car at six this morning. I was scanning every aisle every thirty seconds without making it visible.

I had not yet seen anyone who registered as Evander.

I had seen a hundred and twelve men who, briefly, in the wrong light, had registered as the *shape* my brain had decided his shape was — older, average height, the kind of person who had spent a working life around hardware. None of them had been him. I had logged each of them, marked each off, and gone back to selling prints.

Felix had moved closer twice in the last hour. He had not asked. He had read the small accumulating cost in my posture and inserted himself by exactly six inches each time. I had not asked him to. I had not refused him.

At eleven forty-three, I looked up to thank a buyer for paying with cash, and Mr. Terry was at my booth.

I did not freeze.

That was the strangest part. I had thought I would freeze. I had budgeted for it, the way I had budgeted for the lights and the noise. Instead my body did a small fast survey — *Mr. Terry, navy polo, khakis, the gray windbreaker I had seen him wear in October before, hands in his pockets, eye contact already established with me, smiling the small mild smile I had categorized as suburban-neighbor for four years* — and the survey resolved into the operational sentence I needed.

*He is here. He is at my booth. He is exactly twenty inches from my hand.*

I smiled.

Customer-service voice. Higher than normal. Friendlier. The voice of a person who had recognized her own neighbor at an event and was performing the small polite social work of acknowledging the coincidence.

"Mr. Terry. What a — what are you doing here."

"Oh, my granddaughter dragged me." The smile did not move. "She said you had a booth. I thought I'd come say hi."

He did not, by any of the available filters, have a granddaughter. I had lived next door to him for four years and the only family member who had ever been visible in his driveway was a sister-in-law I had once seen carrying a bag of cat litter into the house at Christmas.

"That's so nice of her." My voice did not bend. "I didn't know you were into the convention scene."

"Oh, well, you know. Trying new things in retirement."

"Of course."

He looked at the prints on the table. He picked one up. He examined it the way a person who was not actually looking at art examined art. The small mortal courtesies were happening around us at exactly the rate small mortal courtesies were supposed to happen. None of it was meaningful. None of it was *real.*

Cai was three booths down the aisle. Felix was at my four o'clock position behind a display rack.

Mr. Terry set the print down. "Beautiful work, kid. Always has been."

He had never called me *kid* before. He had also never used the phrase *always has been* about anything I had made, because in four years he had not, by any indication I had ever received, looked at any of my work.

I kept the smile.

"Thanks. Hey, are you sticking around for the panel block? It's around three."

He smiled. "We'll see. Take care, Bianca."

He moved on.

I did not watch him go. I did not turn my head. I picked up the print he had touched and put it back into the rack at a different angle, like a person who was straightening her display, and I caught Felix's eye through the gap in the booth dividers for exactly the length of one breath.

He had read it.

I did not need to do anything else.

## Scene 2

The plan had been: Cai signals if a target enters proximity. Felix moves to position. Mara holds the front. I stay at the booth and complete the visible recognition. Once the target leaves the immediate area, Felix follows at distance. Once the target exits the building, the trap closes.

The plan was sound.

The plan did not survive the next four minutes.

At eleven forty-seven, Cai's glamour collapsed.

I felt it before I saw it. The small static-hush in the air at the corner of my hearing — the one I had been registering for two days as Cai-adjacent — went out the way a room went out when somebody flipped the breaker. Then a sound. Not a scream. A small sharp human gasp from somewhere down the aisle, and a body hitting the floor.

I stood. I did not think about standing. My customer-service mask sloughed off me in the smallest fraction of a second I had ever known a mask to slough.

"Cai," I said.

Mara was already moving.

Cai was on the floor three booths down. Their face had gone — not the slipping, not the wrongness at the edges, but the full version, the version I had only ever seen in fragments. The glamour had stopped holding. Mortals who had been browsing prints two minutes ago were now looking at a person whose face did not resolve to mortal sight. Phones were already coming up. A small woman near them was saying *oh god is he okay* in the voice of a person who had recognized that a face was wrong but had not yet recognized why.

Felix was in motion. Mara was in motion. I had moved out from behind the booth without making the decision to move.

A hand caught my elbow.

"Easy, Bianca."

Mr. Terry's voice.

He had not left. He had been waiting for the convergence. He had been watching me as much as I had been watching for him, and he had identified the exact moment the system Mara had built around me would collapse to one person — *him* — being the only available adult at my booth.

His hand on my elbow was the casual neighborly catch of a man helping a woman who had risen too fast in a crowd.

His other hand was at my hip in the careful pivot of a person guiding a body sideways.

I did not have time to react before the needle.

The drug was — fast. Faster than I had budgeted for. I had assumed a knockout drug would feel like the kind of fade I had experienced once in college getting a wisdom tooth out. This did not feel like that. This felt like my body had stopped being a thing I could send instructions to. The instructions were still leaving my brain. The body was not receiving them. I could see Felix's profile from behind a display rack three booths away, his entire focus on Cai on the floor, *Cai I cannot leave Cai I cannot get to Cai* —

"There we go," Mr. Terry said. Quiet. Kind. The voice of a neighbor talking a panicking artist out of a hallway. "It's okay. We're going to get you out of here. You're having a panic attack. Let's get you some air."

He was speaking loudly enough that the few mortals near us heard him.

He was speaking loudly enough that they would, later, remember a kindly older neighbor helping a flustered young woman out of a chaotic convention floor before she could pass out from overstimulation.

He was speaking loudly enough that he had pre-built the narrative for them.

I tried to scream.

The sound came out as a small soft exhale that, in any other context, would have read as relief.

He guided me toward the service hall.

Mara was thirty feet away, on her knees beside Cai, calling 911. Felix had a hand at the back of Cai's neck and was checking for a pulse.

Neither of them was looking at me.

I tried to reach for the table. My fingers did not reach. The table slid past my hand at exactly the wrong distance. The little stack of business cards I had set out an hour ago tipped sideways and fanned across the floor in a slow inevitable curve.

Mr. Terry's other hand was at the small of my back. Steady. Practiced.

We crossed the threshold into the service hall.

A volunteer in a STAFF shirt looked up. "She okay?"

"Just overstimulated. I'm getting her some air. My granddaughter's the artist next booth over."

"Oh, sure, exit's down the hall to your left."

The volunteer turned back to whatever they had been doing.

We were out the back door in ninety seconds.

## Scene 3

I did not get the mercy of clean unconsciousness.

The drug held me in something less useful than sleep. I could see. I could hear. I could not move in any direction my body did not already happen to be moving. Mr. Terry — *Evander,* the name finally finished forming inside me — had me in the passenger seat of his truck and was driving with the calm of a man on an ordinary Saturday errand.

The truck was the same truck I had seen in his driveway four hundred times. The console had a half-empty water bottle in the cupholder. The dash had a small magnetic phone holder that was, currently, holding a phone that was not mine. A receipt from a hardware store was tucked into the visor. The truck smelled faintly of motor oil and the same cleaning product I had once watched him hose his porch with.

He talked, occasionally. Not to me. To the windshield.

*You see,* he said. *You see what they do. They make you wait. They make you smaller. They make you less than what you were.*

I tried to lift my hand. My hand twitched against the seat. The seatbelt held me in a posture of polite participation in his errand.

*Idris,* he said. *Idris deserves every hour of it.*

We turned onto my street.

I had spent enough hours watching the live feed of my own block that I could have identified the order of mailboxes in my sleep. We passed three. We passed the corner where the neighbor with the white minivan parked. We passed my driveway. He pulled into his own driveway. The truck stopped.

He came around to my side. He unbuckled me. He drew me upright the way a relative would help a person out of a car after a long flight.

The walk from his driveway to his front door was twenty feet.

My ring camera, currently aimed at my own front door, did not include any of the twenty feet.

He keyed his front door. He brought me inside.

The house had the same floor plan as mine. Same narrow entry. Same turn toward the kitchen. Same staircase up. Different air. Old paper. The chemical exhaust of a basement workspace. Coffee on a warmer too long.

He took me down to the basement.

The basement was prepared.

It was the part I had not budgeted for in any of the available scenarios. The basement was not a place a person built in panic. The basement was a place a person built across months. A folding work table, large. A lamp angled low over a cleared work surface. A chair with restraints fitted to the arms — actual restraints, not zip ties — the kind of restraints you ordered online from a hardware store catalog if you knew which one. Banker boxes labeled in neat block handwriting along one wall. Mailing supplies along another. A small ward-anchor that even my mortal-trained eye could now identify, set into the wall above the worktable.

The boxes had labels.

*Cluster One. Cluster Two. House. Idris.*

The Idris box was the biggest.

Evander walked me to the chair. He fastened my wrists with quick efficient motions. He did not pull them tight enough to bruise. He did not need to. The drug was carrying the work for him.

He crouched in front of me.

His face had changed since the convention hall. The mild neighborly smile was gone. The thing underneath was older, sharper, and surprisingly less alien than the version of him I had been imagining. He looked, mostly, like a man who had finally exhaled after holding a breath for eighteen months.

He held up a pen.

"You're going to draw for me, kid."

I tried to shake my head. The head did not move enough to qualify.

"Don't worry. You've been doing it your whole life. You'll do it for me too."

He put the pen between my fingers. My hand closed around it. Not because I wanted it to. Because the drug had taken the difference between *want* and *grip.*

He set a sheet of white paper on the table in front of me.

He sat down across the table.

"The most powerful fae you know," he said. "Idris. Or Felix. Either one will do. Choose."

I stared at the page.

Somewhere upstairs, I could hear the faint mechanical thrum of his refrigerator. Through the basement window, the late-afternoon sun cut across the concrete floor at the same angle the late-afternoon sun cut across my own basement, which I had never used as anything but storage.

I had been next door to this for four years.

My hand wanted to drop the pen. The drug kept the grip.

The half-sleep state opened under my skin like a door I had been keeping locked since adolescence. The current took. The page brightened. The lamp dragged a halo across the table edge.

*Idris. Or Felix. Either one will do.*

I could not afford to give him either.

The only face I had that was already in his possession in mortal time, already known to him, already inert to his purposes — the only face I had ever drawn that was not fae and would not give him the leverage he was asking for — was the face I had been drawing since I was six years old.

My hand moved.

The line came down in the careful conscious register I had spent two days proving to Felix I had control of, threaded through the channel like a small hard interruption.

Anwyll.

I drew her again.

And again.

The pen made its way down the page in the steady measured strokes of a hand that had decided, somewhere behind the drug, that the only refusal available to me was inside the line itself.

Evander did not realize for several minutes.

When he did, his voice cracked.

"*Not her,*" he said.

He hit the table. The pen jumped in my hand. I drew her again on the next page.

"*Bianca.* Not her."

I drew her again.

The basement had gone full late-afternoon. The Idris box sat against the wall. The Anwyll pages stacked on the work table in a small clean column.

Somewhere in mortal Atlanta, Mara's phone was tracking the location of my phone, which was in the pocket of the cloak Evander had not removed.

Somewhere in Loren, Felix was — I hoped — already moving.

I drew her again.
