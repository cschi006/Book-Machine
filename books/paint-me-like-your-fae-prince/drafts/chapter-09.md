# Chapter 9 — Bianca POV (Only Art)

## Scene 1

I drew the decoy sketch in the morning light with Felix at my desk.

That was, in retrospect, the first wrong thing.

He had not asked to watch. He had carried his coffee from the kitchen, registered that I was already at the worktable, and taken the chair I had left by the second desk for when Mara visited and felt entitled to opinions about my workspace. He had set the coffee on a coaster. He had opened a book that was not actually a book — a polished stone panel I had now seen him use as a working surface twice — and read whatever was on it with the kind of focus that did not, technically, require him to be in my studio for him to apply it.

He was in my studio.

He was watching me draw on purpose, awake, in mortal light, for the first time.

I did not point that out.

I had a decoy to make. The decoy was the piece Evander — *the buyer,* I was still trying to file him under, but my brain had started using the name Vael had given me, and it had started using it whether I wanted it to or not — had asked for last night. Same size as the special sketches. Same finish. Same packaging. No name at the bottom. No channel. No half-sleep. A piece my hand made consciously, the way my hand had been making consciously for the entire commercial half of my career, only now the conscious work would be doing a job the channel work had been quietly doing without my consent.

I blocked the head shape. I checked the reference — I had pulled a stock face from a model who had given the rights away years ago, modified through three layers of compositing so the face would not match any real person. I corrected the jaw. I started in on the line work with the brush pen I used when I wanted a piece to look like its older relatives in the shop.

Felix did not interrupt.

I drew for forty minutes. The light through the window changed by a measurable degree. I did not look at him. I looked at him exactly twice — once when my line slipped at the cheekbone and I had to redo it; once when I changed pens. Both times he was reading his stone book.

Both times I knew he had been watching me a beat before I looked up.

When I clipped the finished page to the rack to dry, I let myself look at him properly.

He was looking back.

"You work in silence," he said. The first sentence he had said since I had started the page.

"Mostly. I have music sometimes. I find it depends on whether I'm doing channel work or this."

"This is — different."

"Yeah."

"You correct."

"All the time."

"You hesitate."

"At the right places."

He nodded. He did not say the rest of the sentence. I did not need him to. The rest of the sentence had been said yesterday afternoon in a Loren kitchen and the day before that in a chamber that smelled like cordite and last night in the way his hand had set the warded tin on the table beside my laptop. The rest of the sentence was something we were both currently deciding whether to use.

I did not pick it up.

He did not either.

We packaged the decoy.

## Scene 2

By midafternoon the package was at the postal annex and we had two more retrievals on the list.

We did them. Cai came along. Cai was steadier than they had been yesterday by a small measurable degree. Felix's containment marks in my studio had thinned overnight as we destroyed more originals — the active pieces left in my flat files were now down to a count I could hold in my head without flinching, which would have been good news in isolation, except that the operational count of pieces in the wider world was four hundred and one and the man who had been buying my work for eighteen months was now waiting at a P.O. box for a piece of conscious art with no name in it.

The retrievals were clean. We were getting better.

When we came back to the townhouse with the originals burned and a third drive of updated records in my bag, Cai went straight upstairs to one of the guest rooms I had not yet realized I had decided to call guest rooms — they were just the smaller of the two bedrooms upstairs — and shut the door without saying they were going to sleep. The shut door was the most explicit *I am going to sleep* gesture I had ever seen Cai produce.

Felix and I stood in the kitchen and did not move for half a minute.

I picked up my phone. The shipping notification was there.

*Package delivered to PO Box annex. Available for pickup.*

He read it over my shoulder. He did not move closer than was reasonable for two people sharing a screen. The proximity, which would have been unremarkable in any other context, was unremarkable in this one too, except that I had been logging the small specific shape of Felix's care for two days and his proximity had stopped being unremarkable some time around when he handed me the tea in the Loren kitchen.

"Tomorrow," I said. My voice came out level. "At opening."

"At opening."

He did not move.

I did not move either.

I had not slept properly in three nights. I had been wearing the same kind of customer-service mask in eight strangers' houses for two days. I had said *I'm not leaving* on the record yesterday in a Loren kitchen, and I had meant it, and the words had walked around inside me ever since with the small unspecific weight of a sentence that had cost me something I could not yet name.

The thing I had not been letting myself look at directly was a category of question that did not have a folder in my brain because I had built the brain on purpose without one.

"Felix," I said.

"Yes."

"I need to ask you something operational."

"Okay."

"Did you check whether I had ever drawn you."

He went very still in the small specific way I had begun to log as *Felix making a decision he had been avoiding making.*

"Yes," he said. "I checked."

"And."

"You have not. There is no portrait of me in any of your records, archived listings, or studio inventory. You have not drawn me through the channel. You have not drawn me consciously. I am not in your shop. I am not in your sleep. You have never seen my face the way you saw Cai's."

"Why did you check."

He did not soften the answer. "Because if you had drawn me, the part of me that has been moving toward you for two days would have had an explanation it could survive. And I needed to know whether the explanation was available before I let the moving be anything else."

I let air in through my nose.

The cinch in my chest — the one that had loosened in stages over the last day and finally let go in the Loren kitchen — did not return.

"Does that ease you," I asked. "Or the opposite."

"The opposite."

"Why."

"Because now there is no working framework available to me that explains it except the one I have not yet been willing to use."

"Use it."

"I'm not going to ask you to do anything in this kitchen, Bianca. I am telling you the working framework has cracked and I am not putting another one on top of it until you tell me what you would like it to be."

The smallest, cleanest sentence I had ever heard a man say to me in my own house.

I did not file it under *think about later.*

I put it in the room.

"I would like to kiss you," I said.

"Okay."

I crossed the kitchen.

He did not move toward me. He let me come.

When I reached him, I put my hand against the front of his shirt without making a production of it. The fabric was cooler than I had budgeted for. His chest rose under my palm. He did not breathe in the way a person breathed when they were performing a held moment; he breathed as if the air was a thing he was using.

I lifted my face.

He kissed me carefully — not tentatively, carefully. There was time in it to stop. Time to step back. I did not. His mouth was warm and precise and then less precise when I turned into it instead of away. My hand tightened against his shirt. The other one ended up flat against the angle of his jaw without consulting me. He shifted closer by inches, not enough to crowd, just enough that the distance stopped existing.

When he drew back, it was barely far enough to look at me.

The kitchen had not changed. The refrigerator was still humming. The shipping notification was still on my phone on the counter. Mara had texted again at some point I had not registered. The world had not gone anywhere.

Neither of us said it was a mistake.

Neither of us apologized.

His thumb rested once, briefly, against the angle of my jaw before his hand fell. I did not let him go quite yet. I held onto the front of his shirt for one more breath.

"Tomorrow we go to the annex," I said.

"Tomorrow we go to the annex."

"Tonight I sleep on a couch and you sleep on a different couch."

"Yes."

"For operational reasons."

"For operational reasons."

I almost laughed. I did not. I let go of his shirt.

## Scene 3

I slept harder than I had slept in days.

When I woke, the morning was already in the kitchen and Cai was at the counter making tea like a person who had been recovering for forty-eight hours and was no longer pretending they were not.

I did not say anything about Felix. Felix did not say anything about Felix.

Cai looked at the two of us once, registered something in the air I had been failing to file, and said only, "Eat the toast. It is on the plate."

I ate the toast.

We left for the annex at 8:42.

The postal annex was, by every metric a mortal building could be measured by, depressing. Fluorescent lights. Tiled floor. A clerk who had not been told to expect us and had, by the look on her face when we walked in, decided we were not the kind of customer she had budgeted her morning for.

Bianca-the-Artist filed a request to confirm delivery on a package shipped under her shop account. The clerk located the package. The package was unclaimed. The package had been at the annex since yesterday at 4:11 p.m. and had not been picked up.

Felix's expression did not move.

We bought a small box of mailing supplies we did not need so the clerk would have a reason to log a transaction and our presence would have a paper-trail explanation. We left the building.

In the parking lot, Felix opened the trunk and lifted the corner of a small black case I had not noticed yesterday — a portable wardline anchor, by the look of it, with a working surface that read motion patterns within a fifty-foot radius. The annex was exactly fifty feet across the parking lot. The case glowed faintly. Felix waited.

We waited.

For four hours.

Three customers came in. None of them was at the right box. One was a courier picking up for someone else; one was a mortal retiree collecting medicare paperwork; one was a young woman with a delivery slip the shape of a package nothing like ours.

By two in the afternoon Felix closed the case.

"He is not coming."

"He is not coming."

"He used the address as bait."

"He used the address as bait."

Cai, from the back seat where they had slept through most of the last hour: "Or."

Felix and I both looked.

Cai said, "Or he came at three in the morning. Or he had it forwarded. Or he is not the one who picks it up. The annex sends unclaimed packages back to sender after ten days; if he wanted it, he had it forwarded."

"He has a forwarding setup," I said.

"Yes."

"That is more sophisticated than I would have given Mr. Terry credit for."

I did not realize until the sentence had left my mouth that I had used the wrong name.

I had not been thinking *Evander.* I had been thinking, in a small uncatalogued corner of my brain, of the kind of mortal infrastructure a person needed to have access to in order to run a P.O. box rotation across multiple annexes for eighteen months without ever physically appearing at any of them. The kind of mortal infrastructure a person who had a name, a house, a truck in a driveway, an occasional misdelivered package, a habit of noticing weather, and a small dry comment about how many mailers I shipped in a given week would need to have.

Felix's gaze sharpened.

"Mr. Terry," I said again, more carefully, as if naming a different person, "is my neighbor."

"Bianca."

"It's not him. It's a — coincidence — it's just my brain being tired and reaching for the most available mortal man who fits the shape of *patient retail collector.* It's just my neighbor."

Felix did not move.

Cai did not move.

Neither did I, after a beat, because I had heard myself say *just my neighbor* and the phrase had landed in the parking lot like a small clean object that did not match any of the other objects we had been working with.

Felix turned the key.

He did not start the car.

"Tell me everything you know about your neighbor."
