# Chapter 12 — Felix POV (Next Door) — RESCUE

## Scene 1

By the time I reached Bianca's booth, Cai was on the floor with Mara at their head, and Bianca was gone.

The booth still held the small architecture of her morning. The stool was angled at the wrong distance from the table. Her sketch reference was open on her phone, which was still face-up on the counter. Her bag was tucked behind the pipe-and-drape where Mara had stowed it at load-in. The signing sheet had a smear of ink along one corner where her hand had rested between sales. The little wire keychain spinner had been turned half a rotation by someone passing through.

She had not left. She had been taken.

I locked the immediate chaos down with the brutal efficiency the room required and no part of myself approved of.

"Medical," I said to the nearest staffer. The staffer nodded too fast and got on a radio.

Cai was conscious in flashes. They had the small focused fury of a person whose body had betrayed them in the wrong moment, and they were spending most of their remaining strength refusing to apologize for it. Mara had a hand at the back of their neck and was already on her phone with the other.

"Find My is loading," she said. "She shares location with me. Always has." She did not look up. "Because unlike some people I do not confuse disappearing with competence."

The screen loaded. The dot for Bianca's phone moved through the lookup. Not the parking lot. Not the arterial road. Not a warehouse on the edge of the city. Not anywhere I could have parsed against the operational maps I had been running for three weeks.

Her street.

Next door.

For one full breath the convention hall went out of focus. Mara's phone resolved Bianca's location with the clean impersonal precision mortal infrastructure used when it did not know what it was reporting on, and the address it gave us was the address of a townhouse I had glanced at from Bianca's front walk on day one of this operation and filed as *mortal background.*

He had been her neighbor.

Cai pushed up onto one elbow.

"Felix."

"I know."

"He has been living next door."

"I know."

Mara was already moving. She had Cai's elbow under one arm and her own bag under the other and the kind of forward locomotion I had seen only in people who had decided, several decisions ago, that they were no longer going to ask anyone for permission.

"We are not waiting for an ambulance," she said.

"No."

"You can portal us closer than driving."

"Yes."

"Then portal us."

I did not normally take instruction from mortal civilians in the middle of a convention floor. I made an exception.

We went through the service hall. Cai, who was still walking unevenly, leaned against the cinderblock for the small ward I needed to open without spectacle. The seam cut through the back-hall wall into a narrow strip of afternoon shadow behind Bianca's townhouse row. Mara stepped through without flinching. Cai followed with my hand at their elbow. I went last and sealed it behind us.

Bianca's house stood exactly where it always had. So did the one beside it. The obscenity was proximity.

## Scene 2

Cai stopped at the edge of the walk and shut their eyes for a careful breath.

"Not him. Not fresh casting. Anchors."

I followed their attention. A bent nail driven too neatly into the porch trim. A strand of thread along the railing seam. A charm worked small enough to pass for weathered hardware beneath the mailbox. Old ward-work fixed into ordinary matter, fed by something deeper in the structure.

"Below," Cai said. "Paper. He tied the concealment to stored leverage."

Of course he had. If exile had stripped him to mortal time, he had learned to live off infrastructure instead of strength.

Mara was already moving along the side path. I watched her crouch by the rear door, take one assessing glance at the small upper pane, and reach into the garden bed for a decorative stone the HOA had presumably approved on a spreadsheet.

"That is not subtle," I said.

"I am not here to impress your court."

The glass gave on the first hit. She cleared the frame with her sleeve, reached through, unlocked the interior knob, and opened the door.

Human entry. Crude. Fast. Correct.

I went through first.

The kitchen smelled of stale coffee and old paper. Rinsed mugs in the sink. A side table stacked with flyers in fussy precision. The recliner in the front room held the deep sag of long use. Paths through the dust repeated the same routes over and over. Under the ordinary clutter, the anchored warding ran like wire.

From below came Evander's voice.

Not loud. Not theatrical. Worse for being intimate. Directed downward with the certainty of command. Bianca was beneath us. What state she was in I did not yet know. He was still talking about exile, blood, and the years he believed had been stolen from him.

He had been here the entire time.

Mara shut the rear door behind us and kept the broken pane turned from the street. Cai leaned a hand against the counter, face gone pale beneath the stolen human lines, and lifted two fingers toward the ceiling as if listening through bone.

A scrape of chair legs from below.

Paper shifting.

I was already at the basement door.

Halfway down, the first page came into view on the concrete floor. At first I registered only charcoal and the line of a cheek. Then the mouth. The angle of the jaw. The shape I had known first from court portraits, later from the oldest private memory I possessed.

My mother.

A second page lay crumpled near the bottom step. A third under the table leg. A fourth beside an uncapped bottle of ink.

My hand went still on the rail.

By the time I reached the last stair, Anwyll looked up from every scattered sheet in the room.

Not one page. Dozens.

Bianca sat strapped to a metal chair beneath a low lamp, head tipped slightly forward, black mask still hiding the lower half of her face. One wrist had rubbed raw against the restraint. Ink marked two fingers. Her hand still held the pen, fingers locked around it as if the drug had not yet loosened its grip. Evander stood beside the table, one hand braced near the paper, his human face loosened now at the edges so the older thing beneath it showed through.

He turned at the sound of my step.

For one beat he looked only irritated to be interrupted.

Then he smiled, and there was nothing neighborly left in it.

"You took your time."

His right hand dropped toward the table where a lighter sat beside the nearest stack of finished pages.

I crossed the room before he touched it.

## Scene 3

The fight was fast and ugly and intimate rather than grand.

I drove him into the worktable. The lighter skidded off and vanished under the boxes. Evander caught the side of my face with the heel of his hand, then dropped his weight sideways under my arm trying to drag us both across the tabletop toward the paper cutter fixed at the far end. I checked him at the shoulder. He pivoted off the failure and kicked for the lamp cord — heel braced against the table leg, intent on wrecking paper if he could not reach the blade.

"Felix," Mara said sharply behind me.

She did not mean me.

She meant Bianca. She had gone straight to the chair, one hand tearing the mask loose, the other already sawing through the wrist strap with a box cutter taken from the shipping table.

Evander twisted under my grip, driving backward hard enough to rattle the lamp. "Do you see," he spat. "He chose her. He chose a mortal woman and let the rest rot around it."

The words had the shape of an old argument I had heard in other forms for years.

I caught his wrist before he reached the lamp cord again and slammed it flat to the tabletop. Bone shifted under my hand with a sound like branches under weight. He hissed through his teeth and went for the stairs instead, abandoning the room in favor of escape the instant audience failed him.

He made three steps.

Cai, swaying in the doorway, put one shaking hand on the banister nail that anchored the concealment and tore.

The ward snapped with a sound too small for what it did. The air in the stairwell folded inward. The basement door above us slammed so hard the frame groaned, and the broken line of the ward lashed back through the stair rail into Evander's legs.

He went down on the steps hard enough to lose breath.

Cai nearly followed him.

I caught Evander by the collar and dragged him back to the concrete before he could reach the top stair or the boxed portraits stacked closest to the wall. He fought with what he had left — teeth, elbow, the dead weight of a body that had learned how to make angles do the work of strength. I put him facedown, took a fistful of plastic zip ties from the open shipping drawer, and cinched his wrists until the ties bit white into his skin.

Alive. Bound. Breathing.

It would have been simpler to do otherwise. Simpler had no standing here.

When I stood, the room rearranged itself around what mattered.

Mara had Bianca half out of the chair and down onto the concrete, wrapped in the cheap dark cloak still hanging from her shoulders. Bianca's eyes were open but not steady. Her pupils had not yet decided what distance to trust. Mara had one hand braced behind her shoulders and was speaking low and blunt, the way one talks to someone whose body has gone somewhere without permission.

"Stay with me. Look at me. Not him. Me."

Bianca blinked once, then again. Her mouth moved against the edge of dry lips.

"Not—" she got out.

"I know," Mara said.

Cai still stood by the stairs because sitting down likely meant being unable to stand again. Their glamour had gone thin enough that it no longer fully hid the sharpness of their actual face. Sweat darkened the collar of their shirt. They looked at the pages around Bianca, at the repetitions of Anwyll's face, and then at me.

I looked down again.

Not one likeness. Many. Charcoal ground into the concrete under Mara's knees; wet ink drying beside Bianca's shoe; the same mouth, the same jaw, the same impossible familiarity repeated until the room narrowed for a different reason.

She had used what I had trusted her with to keep him away from every living target.

Behind the overturned chair, the basement resolved into more than a workshop. Along the foundation wall sat banker boxes and labeled crates arranged around printed shipping labels sorted by annex, order logs in careful block handwriting, and index cards cross-referencing names I knew from court votes. One box bore only HOUSE. Another, in darker ink, IDRIS.

"Mara," I said.

She was already reaching for her phone. "Photographing everything."

"Labels. Every index page. Fast."

"Doing it."

I crossed to the nearest crate first.

Printed receipts. P.O. box receipts. Dates cross-referenced to Bianca's shop listing numbers. Evander had kept records with the same ugly thoroughness he had used to build the collection. Good. I would use that.

The box marked IDRIS sat at the bottom of the stack under two other crates. When I lifted the lid, the oldest paper in the room lay inside archival sleeves meant to guard against damp and handling.

Bianca's younger line was unmistakable.

Thinner. Less certain in some places and, because of that, more honest. My father's face repeated across years of paper in a child's improving hand. He looked no less kingly for it. Only more visible. These had been feeding the wound for years before I understood what was worsening him.

My jaw tightened once. Then I closed the box and carried it to the clear patch of concrete beside the utility sink.

Evander had rolled onto one side against the leg of the overturned chair. Blood darkened the corner of his mouth where he had split it on the table. He laughed once when he saw what I held.

"There," he said hoarsely. "Now you understand."

I set the box down.

"No," I said. "Now I have proof."

There was an empty metal wash basin under the utility sink. I drew it onto the concrete and began with the anchor-fed stack nearest the stairs — the one Cai had marked by instinct before they had broken the ward. Mara moved around me taking photographs in tight, efficient sweeps. Box label. Index cards. Receipt ledger. Next box. She did not ask what mattered. She understood systems.

The first portrait caught fire cleanly.

The change in the house was immediate if one knew the shape of what had been imposed. The pressure in the stairwell loosened. The muffled quality that had sat over the rooms above us thinned. Cai's shoulders dropped by a degree, as if some weight attached to the foundation had shifted off them.

"Again," they said quietly.

I fed the next one to the flame.

I worked through the anchor cluster first, then the box labeled HOUSE, preserving what we could before each burn. Not frenzy. Sequence. Records before fire. Fire before delay.

When I opened the first Idris sleeve and set it alight, something in me registered the change before thought did. Not relief. Relief implied conclusion. This was narrower and more exact: the constant drag I had lived beside for years eased by a measurable fraction, as if a line pulled too tight across distance had finally been given slack.

I paused for one breath.

Then I burned the next.

Behind me, Bianca made a small sound and reached blindly. Mara caught her wrist, then looked up at me.

"She wants you."

I crossed the room and crouched. Bianca's hand found mine with more force than I expected from someone still half inside the drug. Her grip trembled. Ink stained the side of her thumb. She looked at me without full focus and then, with visible effort, brought me into it.

"Not done," she said.

"No."

Her fingers tightened once, then eased. It was not comfort she wanted. It was agreement.

I returned to the fire.

By the time the last of the basement cache had gone to ash, the room felt like a place again instead of an instrument. The concealment had failed with the anchors. The house above us now held only its own bad choices. Evander sat bound against the chair leg with no remaining architecture between him and consequence.

Mara had photographed every ledger page within reach and set the file crate aside for transport. Good. We would need it.

I looked once more through the room to be certain we had not missed anything active. Shipping supplies and archival sleeves lay abandoned near the wall. Bianca's repeated Anwyll pages still scattered the floor like the record of a battle fought with the only weapon available.

No false ending presented itself. Bianca was recovered. Evander was not anonymous. The network still existed beyond this house in every portrait we had not yet found.

Mara helped Bianca sit more upright. The cheap cloak had slipped from one shoulder, exposing the angry red band around her wrist where the restraint had rubbed. Bianca's breathing had evened somewhat. Her eyes had not. She tracked the room as if each object had to be identified from scratch before it could be trusted.

"I'm getting her upstairs," Mara said.

Bianca shook her head immediately, small and furious.

"Not yet," she said.

Mara's expression sharpened. "You can barely sit up."

"I know."

"Then do not be difficult for sport."

Bianca looked past her to the ash basin, the photographed ledgers, the surviving file crate. Then she looked at me.

There was no clean recovery in that face. No return to competence. Drug residue still held at the edges of her pupils. Her hair was matted where the mask strap had caught. One hand still shook against Mara's sleeve.

"Felix," she said.

I went back to her.

Her fingers caught mine again, less hard this time because she had less strength left to spend. She swallowed once before the word came out.

"Paper."

For a moment I said nothing.

She did not mean now, in this basement, with Evander tied at our feet and the house one neighbor call away from notice. She meant after. The next step. The work still alive beyond rescue.

Safe, then, was insufficient.

She wanted paper.
