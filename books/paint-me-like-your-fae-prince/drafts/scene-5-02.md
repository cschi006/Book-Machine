# Chapter 5 — Scene 2
**POV:** Bianca · **WC target:** 1,300 ±200 · **Tension:** 7→6 · **Seeds:** scored · lanolin · charge · cinder · mildew

---

We did the last stop without anything happening.

A small artist co-op on the wrong side of a strip mall that had let one of my originals end up on a consignment shelf marked $90. The shop owner thanked me for catching the materials issue and asked if I would be doing the fall craft show this year. I told her I would have to get back to her. The original came out of its frame, the replacement went in, Cai held the folio, Felix burned the original in the back alley under a ward that did not even discolor the asphalt.

That made eight for the day.

Felix did not drive us back to my townhouse. He drove us to a small parking lot beside a public park with an empty playground and a row of pines that had let down enough needles to crunch underfoot. The sun was starting on the slow gold descent that mortal Atlanta did in early October. We had not eaten. Cai had stopped pretending they were not exhausted. The fact that they could stop pretending was, by the math we were using here, an improvement.

I expected Felix to make us go inside somewhere. He did not.

He turned the engine off and walked to a bench.

Cai followed slower. They sat with both palms flat to the wood and looked at the trees as if testing whether the trees were a kind of room.

I sat.

For a long minute no one spoke. The bench was warm in the patches the sun reached and cold where it did not. The faint mildew smell of damp wood under one armrest was the smell of a park bench at the end of a season. Somewhere a kid yelled. Somewhere closer, a sprinkler started.

Cai said, "When did the drawings begin."

It was not a request to start somewhere. It was a request to start at the beginning.

I looked at my hands. The ink stain along the side of my dominant hand had migrated since this morning — old streaks scored into the cuticle, faint new ones from the gate-code house. The lanolin smell of the hand cream I had used in the car had not entirely covered the ink.

"Thirteen," I said.

Cai did not interrupt.

"I started waking up next to drawings I didn't remember making. Not every night. Enough." I rubbed at the cuticle. "First I hid the paper before anyone came in my room. Then I stopped leaving paper out. Then I learned that didn't matter if there was a notebook anywhere near me. So I locked things up. Built routines."

"Did your parents know."

"My parents thought I was gifted." I almost smiled. "I let them. The alternative was therapy I could not have explained the necessity of to anyone."

"You explained it to a doctor."

"Once. At sixteen. The doctor said *dissociative amnesia,* which was a frame that fit the visible part of the problem closely enough that I took it home and used it like a tool."

"And you never told anyone the rest."

"I did not have a version of the rest that sounded survivable."

Cai's mouth did the small thing that was not quite a smile but was on the same side of the room as one.

"Bianca."

"Yeah."

"I am sorry."

It landed. Not as performance. As a small concrete thing handed across a bench.

I did not answer because I did not have an answer.

Felix had stayed by the car. He was close enough to hear. He had not pretended to be doing something else. After a beat he came over and stood with his back to the bench, looking out at the playground, hands loose at his sides.

He said, "I have been managing a kingdom since I was too young for the work."

It came out as a sentence the way an inventory line comes out — not confession, not request for sympathy, just information someone had decided to put in the room. He did not turn around to say it. He did not check to see whether I was looking at him.

I made myself not look at him. The same way I had made myself not look at Cai in the chamber the day before.

"Since when," I said. My voice came out level.

"Fifteen years. My father has been ill that long. The court has been treating me as king in all but title."

"How old were you when it started."

"Younger than I should have been to be useful at it. Younger than I am now to still be the only person who can run it."

The cinder-and-cold smell of an autumn park is a specific thing. The breeze went through the pines twice. Cai breathed out.

"Why are you telling me," I asked.

It was a real question. I needed to know which compartment to put it in.

He turned then. Not toward me. Toward the playground. The light caught one side of his face and confirmed the wrong-symmetry beauty I had already filed and stopped registering as the same kind of thing it had been on my porch.

"Because the cost of the question Cai asked you was the answer you gave. I am not going to ask you for that without offering one of my own."

There was a small charge in my chest I had not budgeted for, and the cinder caught somewhere under my ribs and did not go cold for a while.

"Okay," I said.

"Okay."

He went back to the car.

Cai did not say anything for a while. When they did, it was small, and not about either of us.

"It is going to take time."

"I know."

"And it will work."

"Will it."

"It is working."

The folio was on the floor of the car. The ash in the small ward dish was scattered along an alley behind a strip mall. Cai's name had come out of memory hours ago and not come back. Two of their other thirty-one were already gone today; we had cross-referenced after the third burn and confirmed it.

"Less bad," I said. "As a category."

"It is going to be enough," Cai said. "Eventually. *Less bad,* enough times in a row, becomes *good.*"

I almost laughed. I did not. I leaned my temple against the bench back and closed my eyes for one breath. The park made park sounds.

We sat there until the sprinkler stopped.
