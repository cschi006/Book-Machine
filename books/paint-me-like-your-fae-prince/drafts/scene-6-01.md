# Chapter 6 — Scene 1
**POV:** Felix · **WC target:** 1,200 ±200 · **Tension:** 8→7 · **Seeds:** list · iron · oscillate · graupel · hiraeth · slew · ellipsism · rustication · tether · crepuscular · harbor · lintel · mordant · shuttle (chapter pool)

---

I had not slept.

The chamber had been cleared overnight and reset by someone whose job it was to clear rooms when princes worked through them. The petition of exile was no longer on the table. Vael's clusters had been replaced by a fresh layout: the same names, the same dates, the same buyer account, only now arranged in the typography of a formal record rather than a working theory.

The room had become an indictment.

That was the first thing I registered when I entered. The second was that the indictment was, by the audience-arithmetic Vael had constructed, addressed to two people. The first was my uncle. The second was a small mortal who was, even now, asleep in a guest room I had given her last night because returning her to her townhouse with the Etsy account active had become indefensible.

I sat. I did not invite Vael to begin.

She did not require invitation.

"My prince. The records cross-reference cleanly. Every name in cluster one is a vote-of-exile signatory. The household-officer cluster is consistent with leverage acquisition against the king. The buyer's pattern is operational, not collector behavior. The mortal is the production line. We have spent eighteen months in a war we did not know we were in."

"I am aware."

"Then the procedural recommendation is unchanged."

"State it."

"The mortal is removed from operational decisions. Her records, her replacement work, and her shop access are transferred to court custody. She is held under restricted contact until the recovery campaign concludes, after which the petition of her status is heard by the king."

The recommendation was iron. It had been iron when she made it the first morning. It had become more iron with every confirmation since.

"Noted," I said.

"My prince."

"Noted, Vael."

Cai had come in two minutes after I did. They had not sat either. The slipping at the edges of their face had stabilized over the last day in the small measurable increments I had begun cataloguing the way I catalogued cordon strength on a border. They stayed near the lintel of the doorway and did not move from it.

Vael looked at Cai once. Did not address them.

"The mortal's continued involvement," she said, "is now a category of risk distinct from the breach itself. She has demonstrated competence in the field. She has also demonstrated concealment. She lied about scale on day one. We had to discover the lie ourselves. Whatever utility she offers, the institution cannot construct it on top of withheld information without expecting the next withholding to be the one that costs us."

I did not move. The argument was sound. It had been sound when I overruled it the first time.

"The institution," I said, "will absorb that risk."

"On what basis."

"Speed. Accuracy. Source familiarity. Replacement production. None of which we replicate by removing her."

"With respect, my prince — those are operational arguments. The decision to keep her is political."

"Yes."

"And the political cost attaches to your judgment."

"Yes."

A slew of silence. The chamber had a habit of holding silences the way carved stone held cold. Vael did not need to extend the silence; it extended itself.

"For the record," she said. "I advised removal."

"For the record. You did."

"My prince."

"Vael."

She gathered the papers in the same order she had laid them and stacked them with the small clean motion of a woman whose authority had been overruled and who would absorb the overrule without bending her register. The mordant in her bearing did not soften.

"Tomorrow's recoveries proceed."

"They do."

"With the mortal."

"With Bianca."

The name went into the room without theatrics. I had been thinking it for two days. I had not yet said it in front of Vael.

She did not react. Her register did not bend.

"Very well," she said.

She left.

The chamber held more empty than the door's closing had earned. The petition, which Vael had taken away with the rest of the papers, had nonetheless left an imprint on the stone where it had sat. I could still feel the heat of yesterday's reveal in the air the way a glamour left its print on backing paper after the working released. Anwyll was in the room now. She had not been in the room yesterday. She had not been in any room I worked from in fifteen years. She was here because Bianca had walked into the chamber last night and had been told, on a stone table under a watchful Vael, what my uncle had done.

The wound my father and I had carried as private structure was now operational information shared with a small mortal artist who would, before this day was out, be sitting across from me again with a pen.

I did not yet know how I felt about that.

I knew which compartment Bianca would have put it in if it had been hers to hold.

I was beginning to use the same compartments.

Cai stayed by the lintel. They waited until the door had shut. They were the only person in this realm with whom I could afford the small administrative concession of looking tired.

"That cost you," they said.

"Yes."

"Was it worth it."

"Ask me at the end of the campaign."

Cai's mouth did a small thing. They came as far as the table and stopped. They did not sit. Their refusal to sit was a tell I had not catalogued before in their body — the kind of vertical alertness a person held when they had information they were waiting to deliver and did not yet have permission to.

I did not ask them yet. I had another conversation to have first.

"Where is Bianca."

"Awake. In the small library. She did not eat the breakfast I left her, but she drank the tea, which by mortal standards is functionally similar."

"Bring her here."

"My prince."

"Cai."

They went.
