# Chapter 6 — Scene 3
**POV:** Felix · **WC target:** 1,300 ±200 · **Tension:** 6→7 · **Chapter close**

---

I sent Bianca to a side room with the Anwyll page and the request that she not destroy it. I did not yet know what we would do with it. I knew she had earned the right to look at it alone.

Cai was in the alcove off the archive corridor when I found them. They were standing with one hand braced against the wall — the casual lean of a person who had been resting against the wall longer than the casual lean accounted for. They had been waiting for me.

The crepuscular light from the high archive windows softened the edges of their face but did not hide what the day's recoveries had cost them.

"How many," I said.

They did not pretend not to know what I was asking.

"Thirty-one."

The number landed cleanly enough to make anger briefly useful.

"Thirty-one active holders."

"Three of which we burned this morning. Twenty-eight."

"Twenty-eight is also a number you did not give me."

"I gave you *more, not enough.*"

"That is not the same thing."

"No," they said quietly. "It is not."

I did not move closer to them. I did not let my voice harden. The shuttle of fear and anger that had run under my work for two days oscillated once and resolved into the colder thing it had been trained to resolve into: triage. I had a count. I would carry the count. I would burn the next four hundred and nine names and recover the twenty-eight that belonged to my closest friend, and I would do it in order, and Cai's body would not have to wait longer than the math allowed.

"You have been concealing this since Spring."

"Yes."

"Why."

"Because if I had told you, you would have changed every operational decision you had made since. The work would have slowed. The court would have noticed. Vael would have built a case sooner. We would have lost ground in places we could not afford to lose it."

"Cai."

"I am not asking you to agree, Felix. I am telling you why I made the choice. You can be angry about it for as long as you need to be."

"I will be angry about it for the duration."

"That is fair."

We stayed in the alcove. They did not soften. I did not extend. The rustication of stone behind their shoulder caught the crepuscular light at an angle that turned the wall briefly into the kind of weathered architectural surface I had been raised to register without comment.

"Why are you telling me now," I asked. Not because I did not know. Because I wanted Cai to say it.

"Because Bianca is here. Because the campaign is operational. Because the political case for removing her was made today and you overruled it and the cost of that overrule attaches to your judgment. Because if I let you carry the count alone any longer, the next time Vael presses, you will not have full information when you push back."

"That is generous of you."

"It is procedural."

We almost smiled at each other. Neither of us did.

I left them there. I did not say what I intended next. They would know in the next ten minutes when I came back to the chamber with Bianca and put the choice in the room.

* * *

Bianca was in the side room with the Anwyll page in her hand and the small unfocused look of a person who had been turning a piece of paper over for an hour without quite reading it. She looked up when I came in.

"Felix."

"I owe you a sentence."

"Okay."

"From the beginning, you asked to be returned to your townhouse. I overruled it on operational grounds. I am giving you the choice now."

Her face did the thing where it did not move and yet rearranged. Cai did the same thing. I had not registered before this beat that Bianca had begun doing it the way Cai did. They had been around each other for two days.

"You're letting me go."

"I am giving you the choice."

"Define the choice."

"You hand over the remaining records and the finished replacements. You receive guard at your house — passive presence, not custody. No one from Loren enters without your permission. You stay there and we proceed without you. You are not under restriction. You are not under investigation. You are a mortal we have inconvenienced and we will leave you alone."

She did not answer.

"Or," I said.

"Or I stay."

"Or you stay. Same terms as today, with the addition of full transparency about Cai's count, the targeting pattern, and any leverage we obtain on my uncle. No further withholding from either side."

She held the Anwyll page against her chest the way a person held something they had not decided whether to carry.

"What happens to that," she said. Of the page.

"Yours. If you stay or if you leave. It was always yours."

She nodded. Once.

"How long do I have to decide."

"An hour. Alone. I will come back when the hour is over."

She did not say anything else. She did not look like the answer was the simple one I had told myself it would be when I made the offer.

I left.

In the corridor I stopped, briefly, by the lintel of the empty chamber. I had not budgeted for what her face had done when I gave her the option. I had not budgeted for the small unspecific weight of the realization that *leaving* and *home* had stopped being the same word for her sometime between her porch yesterday morning and this corridor now.

I did not let myself wait for her answer there.

I walked back to the chamber to wait for it formally.
