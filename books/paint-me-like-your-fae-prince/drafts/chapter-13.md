# Chapter 13 — Bianca POV (Still Here) — AFTERMATH + DELIBERATE CHANNEL

## Scene 1

The room had no paper in it.

I noticed that before I noticed the bed, or the carved stone walls, or the shallow basin of water Mara had bullied into existence on a side table beside a folded stack of clothes that were not mine. No loose sheets. No notebooks. No lamp bent low over a workspace. Even the light came from sconces set high in the wall, angled outward instead of down.

Nothing in the room was pointed at me.

That helped less than it should have.

Mara moved around the suite with the hard efficiency of someone refusing to sit still long enough to think about why. She had taken off her convention badge at some point, but the lanyard mark still pressed pale across the back of her neck. She set a glass in front of me, waited until I drank half of it, and pushed two tablets into my palm.

"Those."

"I know how painkillers work."

"Then this should go smoothly."

I swallowed them because arguing required more structure than I had. My wrists were wrapped now — clean linen, not nylon, not tight — and the skin underneath pulsed its own small private complaint. Someone had cleaned the ink off my fingers. Someone had missed the black crescent in the cuticle of my thumb. I kept looking at that, as if the part left behind might tell me which hand was mine.

Mara crouched in front of me with a clean shirt open in both hands.

"Can you change by yourself, or do you need me to stop pretending you're at all functional."

"I'm functional."

"Incorrect. Try again."

I took the shirt from her. The shirt went over my head on the second try. The first time I got my bandaged wrist tangled and had to sit there with fabric over my face and a stupid immediate spike of heat under my skin because for one second it was dark and close and my arm would not move the way I wanted. Mara said nothing. She just peeled the shirt back up, guided the sleeve over the bandage without touching more of me than she had to, and handed me the hem.

"Again."

I got it on.

There was a washcloth on the table. Folded into quarters. The edge of it caught the light in the exact flat way paper had under Evander's lamp, and the room went thin so fast I did not have time to stop it.

Chair. Straps. Pen placed between my fingers.

The glass hit stone somewhere to my left. Mara's hand caught my elbow and then I was bent over, not the table, not the basement floor, just my own knees with the blood rushing loud and stupid in my ears.

"Bathroom," she said, already moving me toward the open door at the back of the suite. "Come on."

"I'm fine."

"Yes. This is what you always look like when you're fine."

I made it to the tiled floor before my stomach gave up on holding anything. Mara got my hair back with one hand and shoved the basin under my chin with the other in the kind of swift undignified choreography only old friends ever really get to perform on you. When I was done, she rinsed the cloth, pressed the cool side of it to the back of my neck, and stayed down on the floor with me instead of trying to get me upright too soon.

The tiles were cold. Good. The grout lines made a pattern I could follow.

Mara sat with one knee up and studied the opposite wall like she was giving me privacy by looking annoyed at the architecture.

After a minute she said, "I talked to a detective."

That got through.

I lifted my head a little. "And?"

"The police are calling it an obvious violent break with reality." She wrung out the washcloth and put it back on my neck. "Cai had a public medical emergency. Convention staff behaved exactly like convention staff. The neighborhood gets to have a horrifying anecdote and a temporary obsession with doorbell footage."

My mouth tasted like metal and cheap convention coffee I had not even drunk. "That's holding?"

"Better than you'd think. Apparently suburbia is always one unstable retiree away from a group-chat religion."

That almost made me laugh, which hurt enough to count as punishment.

The suite door opened in the other room.

Mara looked up. I did not. I could tell it was Felix from the way the air in the doorway changed — less crowded by accident, more precise by design.

"Vael has him," he said.

I sat back against the side of the bath and held the cloth to my own neck so Mara could stand. My arms felt badly assembled.

"In Loren?"

"Yes." He stayed in the doorway. "He went through the gate under royal bindings. Vael took custody herself. He is in the lower hold. He will not be returning to the mortal world."

The knot under my ribs loosened by a fraction too small to call relief.

"And the house?"

"Cleared. The records are secure."

I looked down at my bandaged wrists. "Good."

He was still standing where the stone changed from the suite's main room to the bathroom threshold. Not blocking. Not leaving. One cuff of his shirt had a dark streak on it that I tried not to identify too closely.

"Cai?"

"With a healer. Exhausted. Upright long enough to object to being treated as exhausted."

"That sounds right," Mara muttered.

Silence held for a moment.

Felix looked at the basin beside me, then at the cloth in my hand, then back to my face. "There is no need for the court to see you tonight."

All I heard was *out of sight.*

Something hot climbed my throat so fast I had to set the water down before I dropped that too.

"No."

He went still.

"You may remain here. Until —"

"No."

Mara folded her arms and leaned one shoulder against the doorframe. She did not interfere.

I pushed myself to standing because it was harder to say what I meant from the floor. The room swayed once and resettled.

"If the solution you're offering is a nicer version of being put somewhere convenient until other people decide what to do with me — absolutely not."

"It is not convenience."

"I know. That does not make it different enough."

Felix took that in without interrupting. His eyes went to my wrist and away again.

"What do you want, then."

The answer arrived before I had a chance to make it sound reasonable.

"Paper."

Mara's head turned sharply toward me. Felix's did not. He just stood there, watching me with the kind of concentration that made lying feel structurally impossible.

"Not alone. Open doors. No one behind me. No restraints. If I say stop, we stop. If I come back wrong, nobody touches me unless I ask."

"Yes," Felix said.

Not after a pause. Not after negotiating terms down into something more manageable. Just yes.

"And you stay where I can see you."

Mara looked at me then and away fast enough to be polite.

Felix's voice changed almost imperceptibly on the next word. "All right."

## Scene 2

The chamber they set aside for the work had two doors, three windows, and nothing fixed to the floor.

They had cleared it to match every condition I had named. I checked anyway.

The table was wide enough that no one had to lean over me to use it. Light came in sideways through glass and stone, not down from a lamp. Both doors stayed open. One looked onto a corridor with guards posted far enough back not to crowd the threshold. The other opened into a smaller records room where Vael had already established command over ledgers, copied manifests, and a stack of buyer histories arranged with military contempt.

I stood in the middle of it and made small adjustments until my skin stopped trying to crawl off me.

"That chair moves."

A servant shifted it three feet left.

"Not there," I said to Cai before they could take the space behind me out of habit.

Their mouth twitched. "Demanding. I'm proud."

They took the chair at my right. The color had returned to them a little since the basement, but not enough to hide the cost of the convention or the broken ward. Their glamour held cleaner at the edges now. That was new.

Vael stood at the far end of the table with a sheaf of reports in one hand and a slate in the other, waiting through my rearranging without comment.

Mara came in with my phone, two sharpened pencils, a stack of printed listing thumbnails, and a sandwich wrapped in cloth.

"You eat half of this before you get weird about work."

"That is a slanderous way to describe my personality."

"It's a precise way."

Felix took the chair I had left in my line of sight near the nearer door and sat with his hands loose on his knees.

We started with ten listings.

Vael had chosen that number after one look at my face. Ten archived photos from my shop. Ten order numbers from my exports. Ten matching entries from Evander's ledgers where he had cross-referenced my titles with names in Loren. The originals were either already ash, still active somewhere in the mortal world, or not placeable from the surviving records.

The first thumbnail felt like nothing.

Glossy paper. Print artifacting. The face on it familiar and not familiar in the way all my shop images had become — mine, but no longer fully inhabitable once the original was gone. I put it in the left pile.

The fourth answered.

Not with sound. With that thin static-hush I had heard in artist alley just before Cai hit concrete, only smaller and closer, running under the print like a live wire. My fingers went numb around the edge.

"Bianca," Felix said.

I looked up.

"The nearer door is open. And the left window."

Stone. Glass. Wood. His face.

The room assembled itself again.

"Still live."

Vael marked the slate. Cai pulled the corresponding file toward them and laid out the address, the buyer history, and Evander's notation.

After that the process got uglier fast. Inert listings stayed paper. Live ones bit back. With the channel open and the archived image under my hand, my pencil would move before I consciously chose the line and give us three shelves, a slanted attic ceiling, the wrong ceramic lamp, a storage-unit number, the edge of a resale sticker. Mara took each sheet the moment I finished and slid it to Vael. Vael turned it into retrieval orders. Cai cross-referenced cities and transfers while I kept going.

The first session lasted fifty-two minutes.

By the end of it my bandaged wrist throbbed around the pencil and my shoulders shook with the kind of exhaustion that came from doing something my body had spent years treating as an ambush. Mara called a stop before I could argue and pointed me toward the cot they had put in the records room. I slept there because walking back to the suite felt absurd.

## Scene 3

The work took four days.

Not a blur. Four separate days with their own costs. On the second morning I woke with my pulse already racing because dawn across stone had hit some part of me as basement lamplight again. Mara handed me tea before I could pretend otherwise. A sketch from session three sent Felix with the first retrieval team through a gate to Chicago because the page had come out with a resale tag and storage-unit number no one else could have guessed.

By that night my hand cramped so badly Mara took away the pencil and replaced it with charcoal. I hated charcoal. It smudged and blackened my fingers and was easier to drop, which apparently counted as mercy now.

Reports came back in a rhythm I never learned to like. Seattle recovered and burned. Queens recovered from estate storage. Worcester already dead before we got there. Vael crossed out each line on her slates while I waited for the recoil to settle from the last live image. Sometimes that meant only a deep shiver through my hands. Sometimes it meant both palms flat on the table and Mara pressing a cup into them until the room stopped looking doubled.

By the third day Cai no longer braced themself on furniture before sitting. Their glamour stopped showing strain at the mouth. Felix still came and went with the retrieval teams, but never in the middle of a session; if he had to leave, he waited until I had both feet back under me.

On the third afternoon we found Cai's last active portrait.

I knew it before they did. The thumbnail was one of the earlier ones from my shop, back when my product photography involved a windowsill, bad weather, and whatever potted plant I had not yet killed. The moment my fingers touched the print, the static sharpened so hard it made my eyes water. My hand moved over the page in a rush of ugly lines — glass-front cabinet, floral wallpaper, brass lamp, Ohio.

Felix went himself.

Cai objected. Naturally.

Vael ignored the objection and reassigned three other recoveries while he was gone.

When he came back near sunset, he carried the original in a warded folder and set it in the basin without ceremony. Cai had been standing at the table sorting confirmed burns from unresolved moves. They stopped with one page half-turned in their hand.

Felix looked at them. "Do you want —"

"Yes," Cai said.

Their voice was even. That was what made everyone else go still.

Felix handed them the tongs.

Cai crossed to the basin and lowered the sketch into the waiting flame with the care of someone returning something dangerous to its proper state. Fire ate from one corner inward, quick and bright. The image blackened, curled, and gave out.

Cai did not move for several seconds after it was done.

Then they lifted one hand and flexed their fingers as if testing a joint that had been wrong for months and had only just clicked back into place. Their glamour stopped looking like effort. Their shoulders dropped.

"I had forgotten what this felt like," they said quietly.

No one answered. There was nothing useful to add.

The royal-linked portraits took longest. Evander had been careful with them, and the consequences of being wrong were still sitting on a throne down the hall. I found some by old archive photo alone and some through his ledgers. One had ended up in a collector's storage box. Another had crossed three addresses inside the same family. By the time the final one came into the chamber, even Vael paused.

It was one of my oldest Idris drawings. I knew that from the line of it before I knew anything else. Thinner strokes. Too much reverence in the mouth. The work of a thirteen-year-old trying to render a face she did not understand and somehow getting the loneliness right anyway.

Felix waited.

He looked at me once. I nodded.

He burned it.

Vael's slate sparked once at the edge and then cleared. In the corridor, voices changed register — still hushed, but no longer carrying that long habit of carefulness. Cai looked toward the nearer door before any of us spoke.

A few minutes later it opened.

Everyone in the chamber turned.

Idris entered without herald and without the hush that had always gone before him in recent weeks — the hush of people preparing to pretend a king was not fragile. He was not fully restored. No one with functioning eyesight would have mistaken him for untouched. But he crossed the threshold under his own power in a dark coat heavy with Loren's particular version of understatement, and the room adjusted around him as if remembering a shape it preferred.

His gaze went first to the basin, then to Vael's slates, then to Cai. Last of all he looked at me.

"Is it done."

Vael answered before anyone else could. "The active network is dismantled, Majesty. Remaining records are archival only."

Idris nodded once. "Then the court sits."

That was all.

He left the chamber the way he had entered it — without asking the room's permission to behave as his again.

The door closed behind him.

No one reached for another file because there wasn't one. The slates were full of crossed lines. The basin held only ash. Mara's hand stayed on the back of my chair. Vael set down her chalk.

Silence sat down across the table from me and waited.

Vael shifted her attention to me at last.

"The court will hear the account of the breach and its repair tomorrow."

I had been expecting the summons. I had been expecting it since the basement, the way a person expected a bill they had decided to pay in full. I did not yet know what kind of room the court chamber would be. I knew what kind of room I had decided to walk into.

"Tomorrow," I said.
