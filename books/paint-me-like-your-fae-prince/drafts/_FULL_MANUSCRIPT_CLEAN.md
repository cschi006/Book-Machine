# Paint Me Like Your Fae Prince

*A paranormal romance*

# Chapter 1 — Bianca

A phone alert buzzing through my desk woke me up. My fingers were sticky with ink and there was a finished portrait under my face. Neither detail was ideal.

The lamp over my desk burned itself into a small white sun behind my eyes. Outside my studio window, late afternoon glow had gone dark, which I hoped meant late evening and not early morning. I rubbed at the faint damp patch on my sleeve where my mouth had drooled just a little.

On the page, a familiar face waited for me.

Ink clean and deliberate, shadows laid in with a patience I definitely had not possessed an hour ago, if an hour ago was even the correct number. The head was turned slightly, eyes aimed just past the viewer, mouth set in that Mona Lisa expression of "I know something you don't know." The person had short dark hair and fine, dark brows. There was a line of light at one cheekbone even the best make-up influencer would be hard pressed to recreate. Oh, and of course the cute pointy ears of a fae.

Like all the others, this one had a name written at the bottom. Caelith Noct-Aravain.

Like all the others, I had no memory of making it.

Arms overhead, my head swiveled about the room. The stacks of completed drawings laid haphazardly about my workspace judged me. Here I was creating a new drawing instead of taking care of the ones waiting to be photographed or packed. I needed to be better at maintaining my Instagram. I was pretty good at paying attention to my Etsy, the money maker, but Instagram, the 'salesperson' part of my business, made my skin crawl just a little.

I was just at the doorway of my bedroom when my phone buzzed again. Ring had caught movement at the front door.

At 11 pm I doubted it was the squirrel that lived somewhere near the shared fence or my occasional visit from Mr. Terry from next door, standing on my stoop with a misdelivered package. I liked my neighbor; he was quiet, retired, impossible to place politically, and not too nosy.

My jaw unhinged as the live camera feed loaded. Two strange men stood on my porch.

One of them was a little back from the door, hands visible, posture relaxed. Even through the warped fish-eye view, there was something off about how their face resolved on the screen. Not ugly. Not even remotely that. It has a bit of uncanny valley in there; proportions were too exact.

The other man was worse.

Nothing about him was overtly threatening, though he was taller by half a foot, and he was just as gorgeous, but he was wearing what I was fairly sure was a costume piece. A dark, structured coat that looked like something a general might wear from the 1800's. He had excellent posture as he squared up with my door.

"Hello, Miss Bianca," he said.

My stomach dropped. This did not sound like someone whose car broke down and their phone was dead. He dripped cool confidence even through the mic.

I hit the microphone button. "That's already too familiar for a stranger on my porch."

The beautiful wrong one glanced sideways at him, eyebrows lifting but mouth staying clamped.

"We need to speak with you," he said.

"No, you don't, especially at 11 o'clock at night."

The other one stepped a little closer. Their voice was not masculine, but not exactly feminine either. "I know this is strange," they said. "We'd only like a few minutes."

People only said that when they planned to annex the next forty minutes of your life.

"Unfortunately, I'm using all of mine."

The dark-coated man stepped closer to the camera, close enough that the fisheye warped his face slightly at the edges. "If you would open the door, we need to ask you some questions."

I stared at the tiny image of him on my phone.

"No," I said, and cut the audio.

I smashed the volume button on the side of my phone just to be sure as I crept to my front door. On the way I grabbed my old field hockey stick in the hall closet. My stomach hurt, my palms were sweating, but yes, my door was locked. I did the same for all my back doors, and then set the alarm in my house. They knew my name.

I got ready for bed per usual, but checked my Ring camera one more time to make sure they had left. They had not. I called the non-emergency line to let them know two people were in front of my house and wouldn't leave. As shaken as I was, I didn't feel safe enough to take my "as needed" prescriptions for anxiety that made me a little sleepy. I wanted to be fully alert, just in case.

My alarm went off with its usual soft tones. I rolled over wanting to hit snooze when something heavy hit my wooden floor and jerked me awake. My field hockey stick I kept next to my bed last night just in case had fallen over from me moving around.

I snatched my phone off my nightstand, ripping the charging cord out. Opening my Ring app, I saw a bazillion movement notifications overnight, or 42. One at 3:42 am was longer than the others. A police car came into view, parking on the opposite side of my street. An officer got out walking toward two people on my curb. They were lying down, their feet in the street and their backs flat in my yard. One of them lifted a hand in the air, and the police officer turned around, drove away. They had not made it halfway across the street from what I could tell, never even spoke. Just left.

My soul left my body. I went through every mundane explanation I had, distracted, called off on the radio, deciding they weren't worth the paperwork…none of them felt good.

I switched to the live feed, and there they were, lying down. I downloaded the videos of the officer walking away AND the video of me talking to them last night. I sent them to Mara with a run down. She wouldn't see it for hours, 6:30 was way to early for her.

*There are two people outside my door that will not leave since last night. They waved OFF the police. Like magic or something. Wtf. What do you do when police don't work.*

My stomach was doing something unhelpful. My legs knew what was waiting at the end of the front walk and had opinions about it that I was choosing not to entertain. My own heartbeat was audible in my ears.

"What would Mara do?" I said, trying to calm myself down, "She would march right out there with her 9mm gun and tell them to fuck off."

I was not Mara, and didn't own a gun, but I did have nasty resting bitch face and a mean-looking field hockey stick. That would have to do.

With every step I made myself angrier. They had slept on my lawn. They had ignored a police officer. They had done it on purpose, in front of my house, because they had decided I was a person who could be waited out. That was not information I was going to let stand. By the time I reached my front door, I was 90% certain I wouldn't throw up.

The early morning cold air slid into the entryway as I pulled open my door. The stalking duo were already standing and halfway up my walkway. Up close, neither man improved.

The gentler one, if that description was going to hold, smiled at me without teeth. Their dark hair was tucked behind one ear. They wore dockers with a golfer's belt and a navy blue polo tucked in. Maybe a golfer?

The other man looked like bad news in a tailored coat. His confident swagger was still there, but now he was pissed, like maybe someone who spent all night sleeping on asphalt. Good.

He took in the hockey stick, the narrow opening, my position behind the door, and said nothing about any of it. Which was, irritatingly, the correct move.

"That is close enough." I braced my weight against the door.

"We aren't here to cause harm. We just have a few questions." Cai's voice was the gentler of the two.

Felix's expression suggested he might, in fact, be here exactly for that.

I kept my weight braced against the door. "Great. Then this can stay a very short interaction."

The gentler one gave a small nod. "I'm Cai. This is Felix."

Felix said, "Is there anyone else in the house with you or have there been strange people coming around?"

For a second I thought I had misheard him.

Then I laughed once because the other options were worse. "That's an unhinged opening question. And no other than you there are no strange people around. You do not need to know who does and does not live here."

"Answer it."

"No."

His face did not change. "Are you by chance a witch, or do you have fae ancestry?"

The porch seemed to narrow.

There were a lot of bad ways for a stranger on your porch. Most of them existed on a scale between scam and violence. None of them included this.

I tightened my grip on the stick. "If this is some kind of performance art thing, I hate to tell you, but your audience research was off."

Cai shot Felix a look that translated clearly as *you are doing badly*. "We know how this sounds," they said.

"Congratulations on your self-awareness. Look, I don't have time for this. I need to get to work." I cringed giving them any detail about my life. "If you need help, you should have talked to the officer last night. You are not welcome here."

Felix ignored that. "Have you had electrical malfunctions or unexplained lapses in memory, maybe last night before we spoke?"

That one landed hard enough that I felt it in my teeth.

Every visible inch of myself in the doorway registered at once, the open slice of house at my back, the fresh portrait still upstairs on my desk like evidence. *Can they see me in my house?*

"You need to leave." My anger was going to turn into an anxiety monster if I did not wrap this up.

Cai's voice softened further. "Has anything appeared recently that frightened you?"

"Ok, still not conversation topics for strangers in my yard." Why was I answering him? I needed to go in, shut the door, and wait this out.

Felix said, "Something happened last night. It happened in your house and we are trying to figure out what it was. If you know something, please help us."

"I know you're trespassing on several levels."

His gaze flicked once over my shoulder into the house, not trying to push past me so much as assessing the space.

My thoughts split cleanly in two. One half stayed at the door doing the conversation. The other began running through exit strategy. Close door, lock it, call someone. Yell, create witnesses. Hopefully Mr. Terry had his hearing aids already on. Swing hockey stick if it came to that, though I had never actually been in a fight.

For a fraction of a second, Cai's face blurred.

It was like a bad layer comp in Photoshop, two versions of the same image slipping half a degree out of register before snapping back together. I blinked hard. My tired brain reached for exhaustion, bad light, anything with a receipt.

I had been staring. I looked at the porch railing instead.

"Bianca," Cai said, and even their voice seemed briefly double-tracked, one note over another. "Please. We are trying to prevent harm."

Felix's attention snapped to Cai. It was a tiny change, but it altered him completely. The control didn't vanish; it compressed.

"Enough." He wasn't talking to me.

Cai made a minute dismissive gesture like they had heard him and planned to ignore him. "I'm fine."

That was the moment the situation tipped from threatening to impossible.

As they spoke, the careful arrangement of their features gave way for an instant, like a mask slipping on skin that wasn't built for it. I saw another face underneath.

Then Cai folded. One second they were standing there, one hand lifted slightly in what might have become reassurance. The next, their knees gave and the whole of them dropped sideways.

Felix caught them before they hit the ground.

The speed of it was ugly. He moved with no wasted motion, one arm across Cai's shoulders, the other bracing their fall, but the thing that jolted through me wasn't the competence. It was the break in his composure.

"Cai."

Their name came out stripped down, raspy.

And in that split second, with their head tipped back in his arm and whatever had been holding their face together no longer doing it, I saw them clearly.

Caelith Noct-Aravain. The portrait on my desk.

The same angle of cheekbone catching light. The same dark sweep of brow. The same pointy ears.

My hand loosened on the hockey stick. It hit the floorboards with a hard wooden knock.

Felix looked up at me sharply. "Open the door."

There was an unconscious impossible person in his arms and my ability to process had taken a temporary leave of absence.

I opened the door all the way and stepped back.

He did not thank me, which was good because I wouldn't have trusted him if he had.

He got Cai over the threshold and into my living room. I kicked the hockey stick farther out of the way with my foot, shoved a stack of padded mailers off one end of the couch, and pulled the throw blanket down where it had bunched against the armrest.

Felix lowered Cai onto the couch, one hand at the back of their neck, the other at their wrist.

Cai's skin had gone pale with labored breathing. Their face was simply their face now, and the lines I had laid down hours ago were all I saw. Or yesterday.

Felix said something low to them I didn't catch. Then he looked at me.

"What did you see?"

I stayed by the entryway instead of moving closer. The distance wasn't enough to qualify as safety, but it was a line and I wanted one.

"I saw a person collapse in my yard."

His patience, if he had arrived with any, had clearly died before crossing town. "Before that."

Felix stood. Not quickly. That would have been easier, somehow. He rose with deliberate control, but now urgency sat right under it, close enough to break the surface.

"What has been happening here?" he asked. "Speak plainly."

The instinctive answer was *none of your business*.

The problem with that answer was Cai on my couch wearing a face I had drawn, the fae face I had drawn. Cai made a faint sound, not quite conscious. My mouth was dry. I swallowed once, annoyed at my own body for participating in this.

"I don't know what you are talking about. But how did they turn into my drawing?" I asked.

His expression did not move, but the room did, somehow. It seemed to gather around the words.

"I know their face," I added, before I hyperventilated. "That's why I let you in. They look exactly like one of my drawings."

# Chapter 2 — Felix

Confirmation we were in the right place didn't feel so great when it came at such a high cost.

Cai lay half-curled against the back of the mortal's couch, one hand still braced where they had tried to push upright too soon. Their glamour had torn badly. It was no longer a clean veil over their body. I put two fingers to the inside of their wrist and counted. It was beating a little fast but steady, a good sign.

I steadied what I could with a thread of power. I felt them accept it, their heart beat evening out.

Cai opened their eyes.

"If you wanted to try out a mortal couch that badly, I would have gotten us a hotel room."

"Ha. My head is hurting. Where is she?"

She made a small sound that might have been disbelief. "Um, source of what? I want to call an ambulance but not if you are just going to wave them away. Which how did you do that by the way? Do you need water?"

"Yes. That would be lovely thank you."

"Ok, I am going to grab some, and then you are going to explain to me why you look like my drawing."

She backed away from us before disappearing into what I assumed was the kitchen. Without taking my eyes off the room she went into, I helped Cai to a sitting position, legs still outstretched longways taking up the length of the couch.

"Cai, she drew you." I said.

"Truly? Where is the drawing? Have you seen it?" they said, controlling their breath.

"Not yet. But I intend to one way or another."

"Be nice, don't scare her anymore."

"Scared? She is not scared. She is surly and contrite, maybe distrustful but not scared."

"Prince Felix, you catch more pixies with nectar than a silver net."

I huffed, letting the conversation drop as I could hear her approaching. She carried a glass of water with straw and a ceramic mug with a paper tag hanging out.

"I brought you some tea with honey too, in case you are having a sugar crash or something. Here." She set the tea down in front of them, handing the water directly. Both mine and Cai's eyebrows rose when she went a step further to put a small pillow on the floor behind him. *Nectar it is then.*

"Thank you, Bianca." I said. "I think we may have gotten off on the wrong foot earlier. Could we sit down and talk calmly?" I tried to keep my voice level, and the slight nod from Cai told me I had accomplished my goal.

Without a word she reached behind the couch and pulled out a folding chair with a hammock bottom. She sat down closest to Cai, causing me to step aside to give her room.

"Ah, I don't suppose you have another chair?"

"Afraid not. But the floor is clean unless you want to force your ill friend to move their legs."

So much for nectar.

I chuckled, taking a seat on the floor near Cai's feet. Even sitting on the floor, I was nearly eye level with the small angry mortal.

Before I could speak, Cai jumped in.

"I know you likely have an endless amount of questions for us, as we do you. I am so sorry for the shock my fainting must have given you. I understand that even more shocking, you seem to recognize…" they made a gesture towards their face. They had yet to put their glamour back into place.

"Yes. I drew you last night."

Cai looked at me.

"Last night, was it right before we knocked on your door?"

"Um, yeah I guess."

"You guess… Do you not know?"

"I do it's just, I kind of lose track of time when I draw."

"What do you mean lose track of time?" I pressed, leaning forward.

She began fiddling with the bottom hem of her shirt.

"How about you tell me WHO you are and why you are so interested in me."

I leaned back, not bothering to correct the displeasure expressed on my face.

Cai set the drained water glass on the floor to reach for the tea.

"You know," they said between blowing on the tea, "that is an excellent question. It may be hard to accept, but we are different than you. We are not mortals…"

"You are fae," she said slowly.

"Indeed. There is a problem with our magic. Something is causing interference and we think it is coming from your domicile. We felt something last night, shortly before we knocked on your door…"

"…when I was drawing your portrait."

Cai just nodded.

"Wait here." This time, Bianca was not careful about backing away to keep her eyes on us. Cai just smiled at me, sipping the tea, while I sat seething.

When she came back downstairs moments later, she was texting on her phone while a portfolio of something was under her arm.

"Ok," she said, putting her phone in her pocket. "I will show this to you, but I want to know more. You promise to answer more of my questions?"

"Of course." Cai said, then they both looked to me. I sighed.

"This was a discovery mission, I suppose discovery can extend to you…"

She handed the hardbacked folder to Cai as they set their tea down.

Their expression changed to something like horror, hand coming up to cover their mouth.

"What is it…" I said getting up too quickly for mortal sensibilities. I felt her nearly fall over in her hammock chair next to me.

It was Cai, but not just their true form. It had their true name in the bottom corner for all to see.

"How…" I said looking from the picture back to the anxious human.

"Weird right?" she said wetting her lips.

I could feel the faint pull of magic. This was the source. I needed to contain this problem, lock down the townhouse and the human in front of me.

"Are there more?"

"Of Caelith Noct-Aravain?" she dropped their name like they were casual friends, pronunciation perfect.

"Of any. Do you have more drawings of them, of any fae person?"

"Yeah…" she looked more and more uncomfortable. "I mean I draw, that's what I do."

"Show me."

"No." She said, leaning back. "I have questions too. Like why is my drawing causing problems and what does your face look like? Do you have pointy ears?" she asked looking over my face, "Can you cast spells?"

"Bianca, this is serious. We need to know how much of a problem this is before we know how to solve it. What you are doing is dangerous for us." My hand hovered gently over the paper, stirring the notes of magic embedded within. "This is why Cai collapsed. You are hurting them with your drawings…"

"How do you know my—" I cut her off with a raised hand.

"Fine," she said. "But if you touch anything without asking, I'm… I will start screaming and my neighbor will come over and he is a vet and he has guns."

I opened my mouth to tell Cai to stay here, but one look told me it was futile.

The townhouse was small by my court standards. My bedroom was larger than her ground floor. The front room was a bit chaotic as a landing area, but the rest of home was labeled, controlled. I inspected the neatly hung portraits on the wall at the top of her stairs. One reminded me of my father, King Idris. Bianca noticed me noticing and went still for half a step.

"That one's normal," she said.

*Normal.* The word rang absurdly in a house where Cai's true name had appeared under a mortal hand. Still, I clung to everything she said. Anything could be a hint, a clue as to what we are dealing with.

"How would you know what is normal or not? Can you sense magic?"

She turned, walking backwards towards a closed door on the landing.

"Because I didn't… I didn't lose myself when I drew it. I have this thing, a medical condition, it's called dissociative amnesia. Sometimes I black out when I am drawing, like a seizure."

*Seizure?*

"Did you lose yourself when you drew Cai last night? Is that why you didn't know when you drew it?"

She nodded once before turning back to the closed door. Her shoulders tucked up under her ears with one hand on the door knob.

"Open it." I commanded.

"I want to call my friend here… This doesn't feel right."

Cai gently placed one hand on the backside of her arm.

"Whatever happens, I promise we will not harm you."

Her shoulders sagged, the handle turned with a click.

There were two workstations on either side of the room. One had a white laptop and two monitors. There was barely anything else on the desk, besides a mug that said "no drama lama," filled with pens. The other desk had pencils, pens, ink, stacks of paper. There was a lamp attached to the side of the desk, facing down to the surface. Another laptop sat open but off with stickers all over it. Shelves were full of books and little dolls with big eyes.

Along bottom of the wall were stacks of empty glass frames and the mattes. There was packing supplies, giant plastic totes labeled in neat handwriting, and stacks of hardbacked notebooks in neat piles everywhere.

Cai took a few steps in, only to sink down to the absurdly fluffy white rug dominating the room. "My Prince… it is everywhere."

It felt like walking into the royal armory. Ancient magical artifacts were stored there, waiting to be used. The magic was both active and inert in this room.

"This is wrong. So wrong," I said.

"My prince?" she said as she stooped down to my ailing friend. "Cai, are you ok?"

"Yes, Prince Felix. He is the prince of Loren, our kingdom."

I approached the desk with the drawings, starting to sift through them carefully.

"Loren? That's the name of where you are from?" Before they could answer she noticed what I was doing. "Um, excuse me, prince or not, you can't just touch my stuff. Seriously, what is happening to Cai?"

"You are happening to Cai." I said, a little too harshly. This idiot woman was killing my friend, draining my kingdom, and she was concerned about me touching her 'stuff.'

"What is that supposed to mean? You don't even care they collapsed again. Fae or whatever we need to get them to a hospital… Are you listening to me?"

I began to look through her 'stuff' and found portraits of fae I knew. There were multiple portraits of the same person, only a few in the multiples had the tingle of magic. I didn't understand what I was seeing. Not yet.

"It means," I said, "that this room is not safe."

Her laugh came out short and sharp.

I opened another portfolio. There were enough exposed names in here to compromise a household, perhaps more. Enough that if any had already left this room — I did not want to finish that thought. I had an inkling I knew what this was, and it would explain everything.

I began touching each notebook, each drawer, laying a containment sigil on the surface. It flashed pale once, then sank into the material. The pressure in the room changed by a degree, easing up. I went to every container, box, folder, bookcase, anything that could contain the stolen names. Mortal eyes would not slide past them; this was not that kind of working. It would, however, slow accidental contact and keep the active pieces from answering so loudly to one another until I returned with proper containment.

"What did you do?"

"Limited access."

"So you can cast spells."

I turned around to see her kneeling next to my friend, who sat legs crossed on their bottom. Both her arms were around their shoulders in a side hug. She was both supporting and protecting them. Cai's eyes were shut, while hers were wetter than usual. Maybe she was scared. Maybe she should be.

I took Cai's portrait from they still held under their arm. I slid it between two sheets of inert backing paper. Even through the buffer, the name bit. Their eyes fluttered open.

"You are coming with us," I said.

"No."

"I will not leave you here. This," I said waving my hand about the room, "and you, are too dangerous to leave unguarded. This is for your safety too."

"You do not get to decide that."

"I already have."

She took a deep breath, voice raising. "You can't drag people around through what, let me guess, a magical portal? I have a medical condition. I don't know about whatever you find is so wrong about my art but it's not—"

Cai surprised me cutting her off. "Bianca."

They began to stand up, shifting forward out of her arms to get their legs uncrossed, conserving energy with every slow movement. Their glamour had settled into a partial hold again, though the edges still wavered if one looked too directly.

"If there were a useful mortal answer," they said, "we would have preferred it. Quite strongly. Prince Felix and I came here looking for answers. We don't know what you are, and I believe you mean us no harm, but what this looks like is you have somehow been drawing and stealing the true names of fae. You want to see what that does, just look at me. I can barely stand."

"This is kidnapping," she said.

"Yes," I said. "If you require the blunt version."

Cai closed their eyes for half a breath. "Felix."

Bianca made a short, incredulous sound. "Jesus Christ."

"I have a job," she said. "I have—"

One moment the wall existed. The next, a vertical seam of light had replaced part of it, thin as a blade at first, then widening until the room beyond it stood in view. Cold stone. Carved arches. Light moving across the floor, filtered through water or glass. The corridor was empty, but it carried the watchful stillness of a place built for power and kept under it for a very long time.

I expected the vision of a portal to stop her annoying prattle. What I did not expect was her serene face. She was focused, curious even.

She took one step towards the opening.

"No," she said, "No."

She looked from the corridor to the desk, to the corridor again, and when she spoke the words came out stripped of all the anger and spite she had been using to defend herself.

"I've drawn this place."

I came here believing I would find the mortal with information about the breach to our world. Instead, I found the breach to our world in the mortal.

# Chapter 3 — Bianca

The cold hit me first. Then the recognition.

I knew this corridor. Same carved ribs across the ceiling. Same pale veining in the floor catching light like something moving under ice. Same narrow windows cut too high to see through, only to spill that strange filtered brightness down the walls. I knew the turn ahead before I saw it. I knew the empty plinth in the alcove on the left. I knew the way the right wall bowed inward just enough to feel built by someone who understood symmetry intellectually and disliked it on principle.

The portal closed behind us with a small mechanical click. I did not turn around.

My body had opinions about that. My stomach was unspooling itself into something low and cold. My legs knew exactly how to walk forward and they were doing it anyway, because the alternative was staying near the wall that had been my studio thirty seconds ago and was now, definitively, stone.

Two figures stood farther down the corridor. Not exactly armor. Not exactly meant to comfort me. They wore the kind of dark clothing that resolved to the eye as *competent.* They were watching us with the bored alertness of people who did this for a living and were curious only insofar as the new arrival might change their evening.

So this was the part of the kidnapping where the kidnappers had backup.

Cai made a small sound beside me. Felix had their elbow before I had processed any movement. Cai's color was worse in this light. The wrongness at the edge of their face — the slipping I had filed earlier under *bad layer comp in Photoshop* — was harder to file here. It read as wound. Their feet did not quite track in a straight line.

"This way," Felix said.

That was apparently for all of us.

I did not move for a second. Inventory was better than panic.

Corridor. Two silent guards behind. One direction forward. Cai needing help. No visible doors within sprinting distance, which was useless data because I had nowhere to sprint to. My phone in my pocket. Felix's heel ahead of me on stone. The pine-needle smell of something cold and resinous coming from a corner I could not place.

I checked the phone anyway.

8:14 a.m. No bars. Not even the *No Service* indicator I was used to seeing in elevators. Just clean nothing, like the device knew it was off the grid in a way that did not have a polite human label yet.

"What is this place," I said.

It came out flatter than I felt. I would take that.

Felix did not turn around. "Loren."

"That answers basically nothing."

"You will get the rest in order."

That was exactly the kind of sentence a person used when they wanted to control not just the information but the timing of my blood pressure. I let it go because Cai had stopped under their own weight again and Felix had moved without breaking stride to catch them, and the small graceful brutality of how he kept Cai upright while not making it visible to the corridor was the most unsettling competence I had seen in person.

The two silent figures fell in behind us. Not crowding. Just present in a way that narrowed the hallway by feet I did not have to spare.

We walked.

The corridor was a quarter-mile in my drawings. In person it was shorter. That made it worse — every detail I had been making for sixteen years was confirmed faster than I could discount it. The plinth was where I had drawn it. The wall bowed inward at exactly the place my hand had bowed it. There was a small hairline crack in the second pale stone that I had once spent a Sunday afternoon stippling and had been faintly proud of.

I had not made this up.

I had been documenting it.

That landed in my body before it landed in my brain. My ribs got tighter. The back of my throat tasted like the metallic edge of a lancet hitting porcelain — a small clean shock I had no context for and could not place. My heel skidded once on the polished floor and Felix's hand was at my elbow before I had decided to need help.

"Don't," I said.

He let go before the word finished.

That was — not nothing. I filed it for later.

Felix changed direction before we reached the main bend and pushed open a side door I had known would be there half a second before he touched it. The handle was wrought in a pattern I had drawn into a sketchbook the summer my mother decided I might benefit from a quieter color scheme in my bedroom.

The chamber inside was smaller. Less ceremonial. Or what passed for less ceremonial here, which was still ancient-grievance-with-furniture by mortal standards, but the proportions were closer to a room a person could think in.

A long narrow table of dark stone in the center. A low sideboard with a pitcher, cups, a basin of water that was so clear it looked staged. Two chairs. Not six. One wall was just wall — nothing on it — and the bare stone after a corridor that had been imposing on purpose hit me like a small mercy I had not earned.

A woman stood at the end of the table.

She had been waiting for us.
